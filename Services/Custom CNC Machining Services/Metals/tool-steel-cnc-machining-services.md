## Section 2: Why Tool Steel for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Tool Steel — Hardness and Wear Resistance in Precision Components

Tool steels are high-carbon, high-alloy steels designed to hold a cutting edge, withstand abrasion, and resist deformation under load at elevated temperatures. The same properties that make them useful as tools — hardness up to 64 HRC, wear resistance, compressive strength — make them the correct material for moulds, dies, fixtures, and structural components that must maintain dimensional integrity under repeated mechanical or thermal loading.

The engineering logic for choosing tool steel is specific: when the application requires surface hardness and wear resistance that alloy steels like 4140 cannot provide, or when a mould insert must survive tens of thousands of injection cycles without dimensional change, tool steel is the specification. The common grades each target a different set of requirements — D2 for maximum wear resistance, H13 for hot work applications and die casting, P20 for plastic injection moulds as the pre-hardened standard.

Machining strategy depends entirely on the condition in which the part is supplied and the intended delivery condition. P20 is machined in a pre-hardened condition (28–34 HRC) — no post-machine heat treatment required. D2 and H13 are typically machined in the annealed or pre-treated soft condition, then heat-treated after machining to achieve final hardness. Finish machining after heat treatment is possible but requires grinding or hard milling — this is a sequence decision made at quote stage.

Wire EDM is the complement to CNC machining on tool steel — for profiles and slots that cannot be achieved by milling after hardening, EDM cuts hardened tool steel accurately at ±0.002–0.005 mm.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**Design:** Two-column icon+text grid. White background.

**H2:** Where Tool Steel CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Plastic Injection Moulds**
P20 mould cavities and cores for short-to-medium volume plastic injection moulding. H13 for glass-filled or high-temperature plastic applications. D2 for highly abrasive or reinforced thermoplastic materials. CNC machined, then polished to specified SPI grade.

**Die Casting Tooling**
H13 die inserts and core pins for aluminium, zinc, and magnesium die casting. H13's resistance to heat checking (thermal fatigue cracking) from repeated rapid heating and cooling cycles is the reason it is the die casting industry standard.

**Stamping, Blanking, and Forming Dies**
D2 for blanking punches and dies where edge wear is the life-limiting factor. A2 for general-purpose cold work dies where toughness and lower distortion on heat treatment are priorities. O1 for short-run tooling.

**Precision Jigs and Fixtures**
Tool steel fixture plates, locating pins, and clamping components where dimensional stability under load and wear resistance at datum surfaces matter. P20 for mould and die fixtures; D2 for wearing contact surfaces.

**Aerospace and Automotive Production Tooling**
Trim and pierce dies for sheet metal, composite layup tools, and precision gauges. Dimensional stability after heat treatment is confirmed by CMM before delivery.

**Precision Gauges and Metrology**
Gauge blocks, form gauges, and calibration fixtures. O1 and D2 for dimensional stability and wear resistance at contact surfaces.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Tool Steel CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on tool steel CNC machined parts?**
Standard tool steel CNC work (in annealed condition) follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions. Precision features hold to ±0.005 mm, CMM-confirmed. Post-heat treatment dimensional change must be accounted for — most tool steels move during hardening. For tight tolerances on hardened tool steel, finish dimensions are achieved by hard milling, grinding, or wire EDM after heat treatment. Achievable tolerances depend on the delivery condition and sequence — the engineering team specifies the machining and heat treatment sequence at DFM review to ensure final part dimensions meet the drawing.

**Q2: Should I machine tool steel before or after heat treatment?**
The standard sequence for most tool steel parts is: rough machine in the annealed condition → heat treat to final hardness → finish machine or grind to final dimensions. The reason is practical: machining in the annealed condition is faster and easier — material removal is efficient and tooling life is acceptable. After heat treatment, only fine material removal (hard milling, grinding, or EDM) is practical for high-hardness grades. The exception is P20, which is supplied pre-hardened at 28–34 HRC and machined directly to final dimensions without additional heat treatment. Confirm the sequence on your drawing or at quote stage — the engineering team will advise for your specific grade and geometry.

**Q3: What is the difference between D2, H13, and P20 tool steel?**
D2 is a cold work die steel alloyed with 12% chromium for maximum wear resistance — it achieves 58–64 HRC after hardening and is the go-to for blanking dies, abrasive plastic moulds, and cutting tools where edge retention is the priority. H13 is a hot work die steel designed for repeated thermal cycling — it resists heat checking (thermal fatigue cracking) that would destroy cold work steels in die casting and hot stamping applications. Operating hardness 42–52 HRC. P20 is a plastic mould steel supplied pre-hardened at 28–34 HRC — it is engineered to machine well without post-machining heat treatment, making it the standard for short-to-medium volume plastic injection mould cavities where maximum hardness is not required.

**Q4: Can tool steel CNC parts be welded?**
Welding tool steels is possible but requires strict process control — high-carbon content makes these materials susceptible to hydrogen-induced cracking in the heat-affected zone. Preheating before welding, controlled interpass temperature, and post-weld stress relief are required for structural welds. For mould repair welding (building up worn or damaged cavity surfaces), specialist TIG welding with matched filler rods is used — this is a repair process rather than a manufacturing process. Confirm weld requirements at quote stage if your design includes welded joints in tool steel.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for tool steel CNC machining?**
No minimum. One tool steel part is a valid order. Single mould inserts, prototype dies, and production tooling runs all receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time and heat treatment costs are fixed, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for tool steel CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tolerance callouts, heat treatment specifications, hardness requirements, surface finish grade (SPI standard for mould polish), or GD&T callouts appear.

**Q7: How much does tool steel CNC machining cost?**
Tool steel CNC machining costs more than aluminium or free-machining stainless work — material cost is higher, cycle times are longer in annealed condition and significantly longer in hardened condition, and heat treatment adds both time and cost. P20 is the most economical because it is supplied pre-hardened and machined directly. D2 and H13 require heat treatment after machining, adding cost and lead time. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours — state the grade, delivery condition (annealed, pre-hardened, or post-HT), and required hardness. DFM review is included with the quote.

**Q8: Can tool steel mould inserts be made by wire EDM instead of CNC machining?**
Wire EDM and CNC machining are complementary on tool steel. CNC machining removes the bulk of the material efficiently in the soft (annealed or pre-hardened) condition — complex 3D geometry, pockets, and faces. Wire EDM machines precision profiles and slots in the hardened condition that would be impractical to achieve by milling — thin slots, sharp internal corners, and profiles on hardened material where ±0.002–0.005 mm accuracy is required. Most complex mould inserts require both processes: CNC machining first, heat treatment, then wire EDM for the precision profiles that need to be in the hardened part. TOP PROTO offers both processes in one facility.
