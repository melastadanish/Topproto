## Section 2: Why Copper for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Copper — When Conductivity Is the Primary Requirement

Copper is specified in CNC machining when electrical or thermal conductivity is the governing design requirement. No other common structural metal approaches copper's conductivity — C101 (oxygen-free copper) reaches 101% IACS electrical conductivity, and C110 (electrolytic tough pitch) reaches 100% IACS. Aluminium achieves approximately 61% IACS and brass approximately 26%. For busbars, electrical contacts, transformer components, heat sink bodies, waveguide sections, and induction coils, the conductivity cannot be substituted.

Thermal conductivity is the parallel property. C110 copper has thermal conductivity of approximately 400 W/m·K — more than twice the thermal conductivity of aluminium 6061 (167 W/m·K). For heat spreader applications, cold plates in electronics cooling, and laser component mounts where heat must be transferred away from a source as rapidly as possible, copper's conductivity is the specification that no other machined metal matches.

The machining challenge specific to copper is its softness and high ductility. Copper produces long, stringy chips that wrap around the tool rather than breaking cleanly. Without correct tool geometry and chip control strategy, these chips re-enter the cut and damage the workpiece surface. Positive-rake tooling, high feed rates, and consistent coolant delivery are the standard approach. Surface finishes on copper can be excellent — Ra 0.8 µm as-machined is achievable — but require attention to tool condition and chip clearance.

Copper is dense (8.96 g/cm³), expensive per kilogram relative to aluminium, and oxidises readily in air. Surface treatment after machining is common — tin plating for solder-compatible surfaces, silver plating for minimum contact resistance at high-current interfaces, and nickel plating for corrosion protection in humid environments.

## Section 3: Copper Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Copper Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties comparison — C101 vs C110:**

| Property | C101 (Oxygen-Free) | C110 (ETP) |
|---|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 220–370 MPa | 220–390 MPa |
| Yield Strength | 70–300 MPa | 70–310 MPa |
| Elongation | 6–50% | 6–50% |
| Hardness | 45–100 HRF | 45–100 HRF |
| Density | 8.94 g/cm³ | 8.96 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 391 W/m·K | 400 W/m·K |
| Electrical Conductivity | 101% IACS | 100% IACS |

**Machinability notes:**

Copper's high ductility is the defining machining challenge. The material doesn't fracture — it flows. Chips are continuous and stringy without the natural break-point that exists in free-machining brass or even aluminium. The tooling response is sharp, high positive-rake geometry with polished flutes to reduce friction and chip adhesion. High feed rates and aggressive depths of cut produce better results than light, slow passes — the latter rubs and work-hardens the surface.

C101 (oxygen-free) is slightly easier to machine than C110 — the lower oxygen content reduces tool wear at the grain boundaries. Both grades are significantly softer than structural metals like steel or titanium. Heavy depths of cut on thin-wall copper features require careful fixturing to prevent workpiece deflection.

Copper oxidises on contact with air and moisture — the characteristic surface darkening from bright copper to brown and eventually green (verdigris) is oxide formation. Parts that require a bright copper appearance or low contact resistance should be plated or sealed shortly after machining.

**Electrical and Power Systems**
Busbars, switchgear contacts, transformer windings, current-carrying terminals, and electrical enclosures. C110 ETP is the standard for electrical distribution hardware — 100% IACS conductivity at an accessible cost. Silver-plated contact surfaces on high-current joints.

**Electronics Cooling and Thermal Management**
Heat spreaders, cold plates, vapour chamber bases, and laser diode mounts. Copper's thermal conductivity (400 W/m·K) is the specification that drives the material choice — where aluminium's 167 W/m·K is insufficient for the heat density, copper is the next material. Machined copper cold plates for electronics cooling are a common order type.

**RF and Microwave Waveguide Components**
Waveguide sections, resonant cavities, and RF filter bodies. Copper's electrical conductivity is critical for RF performance at high frequencies — skin depth effects mean the surface conductivity directly affects RF losses. C101 (oxygen-free) is preferred for waveguide manufacture to minimise inclusions that could affect surface finish.

**Semiconductor and Precision Instruments**
Electrode components, induction heating coils, vacuum chamber fittings, and precision measurement fixtures. Oxygen-free copper (C101) for vacuum-compatible applications — standard C110 contains small amounts of oxygen that can outgas in high-vacuum environments.

**New Energy**
EV charging connector contacts, battery pack busbars, fuel cell current collectors, and electrolyser electrodes. The transition to high-current battery systems has increased demand for precision-machined copper busbars and connection components.

**Decorative and Architectural**
Ornamental hardware, lighting fixtures, and architectural fittings where the warm copper appearance and corrosion patina are specification requirements.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Copper CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on copper CNC machined parts?**
Standard copper CNC work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.8 µm is achievable as-machined on copper with correct tooling; polishing achieves Ra 0.1 µm. Copper's softness and ductility require careful fixturing and chip control to hold tight tolerances consistently — these are assessed during DFM review.

**Q2: What is the difference between C101 and C110 copper?**
Both are commercially pure copper — the distinction is in oxygen content and intended application. C110 (electrolytic tough pitch, ETP) contains approximately 0.04% oxygen, which produces a tough, economical copper with 100% IACS electrical conductivity. It is the standard for busbars, switchgear, and general electrical conductors. C101 (oxygen-free, OF) removes almost all the oxygen (maximum 0.001% oxygen) — this eliminates internal oxide inclusions that can cause porosity in welds and outgassing in vacuum environments. C101 achieves 101% IACS electrical conductivity and is specified for waveguide manufacture, vacuum-compatible applications, and high-purity electrical assemblies. For most electrical machining applications, C110 is the correct and more economical specification.

**Q3: Can copper CNC parts be welded?**
Copper can be welded by TIG, MIG, and electron beam welding processes, but requires attention to copper-specific challenges: high thermal conductivity means heat dissipates rapidly away from the weld pool, requiring higher heat input than for steel or aluminium; oxygen in C110 can form steam pockets during welding (hydrogen disease), which is why C101 is preferred for welded copper assemblies. For C101, TIG under argon shielding produces acceptable structural welds. For C110 welding, specific filler metals and preheating are required.

**Q4: What surface treatment is recommended for copper CNC parts?**
The correct treatment depends on the application. For electrical contact surfaces: silver plating (2–25 µm) provides the lowest contact resistance and oxidation protection. For solder-compatible surfaces: tin plating (5–25 µm). For corrosion protection in humid environments: nickel plating (electroless) provides a stable, non-oxidising surface while maintaining most of the thermal conductivity benefit. For decorative applications: clear lacquer prevents atmospheric oxidation without affecting appearance. Unprotected machined copper oxidises in days to weeks depending on humidity — if long-term bare copper surface conductivity is required, plan for plating or a controlled environment.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for copper CNC machining?**
No minimum. One copper part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. There is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for copper CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, plating requirements, or GD&T callouts appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How much does copper CNC machining cost?**
Copper CNC machining is moderately expensive relative to aluminium — primarily driven by higher material cost (copper per kg is significantly higher than aluminium), slower cycle times due to chip control requirements, and surface treatment costs if plating is required. A simple copper busbar with standard tolerances and as-machined finish costs less than a complex waveguide section with ±0.005 mm form tolerances and silver plating. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does copper compare to aluminium for thermal management parts?**
Copper's thermal conductivity (400 W/m·K for C110) is approximately 2.4× higher than aluminium 6061 (167 W/m·K). For heat spreaders and cold plates where the highest possible thermal conductivity is required — laser cooling, high-power electronics, and radar components — copper is the correct specification. Aluminium is specified where weight matters more than peak thermal conductivity, or where the conductivity difference is not significant for the application's heat density. Copper cold plates are heavier (8.96 versus 2.70 g/cm³) and more expensive, but no other common structural metal provides thermal conductivity closer to copper's figure.
