## Section 2: Why 7075 for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Aluminium 7075 — The High-Strength Choice

Aluminium 7075-T6 delivers roughly twice the yield strength of 6061-T6 — 503 MPa versus 276 MPa — at the same weight. That combination makes 7075 the correct alloy when the part needs to carry structural load, resist fatigue under cycling, or operate in a high-stress application where 6061 would deform. Aerospace secondary structures, high-load brackets, and motorsport components are the natural territory.

The higher strength comes from a different alloying system. Where 6061 is a magnesium-silicon alloy, 7075 is a zinc-magnesium-copper alloy. The zinc content is what delivers the strength, and also what creates the design constraints specific to this material.

The first constraint is weldability. 7075 cannot be fusion-welded without severe risk of hot cracking and significant strength loss in the heat-affected zone. If the assembly requires welding, 6061 is the correct alloy. If it requires bolting, bonding, or mechanical fastening, 7075 is feasible.

The second constraint is anodizing behaviour. 7075's copper and zinc content produces more variability in anodic layer colour than 6061 — particularly on cosmetic colour anodize. Type II clear anodize works consistently; colour anodize on 7075 can show batch-to-batch variation. For corrosion protection on 7075 aerospace parts, chromate conversion (Alodine, MIL-DTL-5541) is often preferred over anodize.

## Section 3: Aluminium 7075 Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** 7075 Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — 7075-T6:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 572 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 503 MPa |
| Elongation | 11% |
| Hardness | 87 HRB (Brinell) |
| Density | 2.81 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 130 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | Very Good — ~70% relative to brass C360 |
| Weldability | Poor — not recommended for fusion welding |

**Machinability notes:**

7075 machines well — better in some respects than 6061. The higher hardness produces a cleaner shear plane at the cutting edge, meaning chip formation is more predictable and surfaces tend to come off the cutter with better quality. High spindle speeds and carbide tooling are standard. Coated carbide (TiAlN) performs well on higher-volume runs where tool life matters.

Residual stress is a greater concern in 7075 than in 6061. 7075-T6 plate carries significant internal stress from the quenching step in heat treatment. Heavy stock removal — removing more than 30–40% of the billet cross-section — can release this stress and distort the part after unclamping. For precision components and complex parts with significant material removal, 7075-T7351 (over-aged and stress-relieved) is the specification that addresses this.

Corrosion resistance is lower than 6061. The copper content makes 7075 galvanically active in contact with dissimilar metals in the presence of moisture. Surface treatment is not optional for 7075 parts in service — chromate conversion or anodize is standard.

## Section 4: 7075 Tempers

**Design:** Full-width table. White background.

**H2:** Aluminium 7075 Tempers — Choosing the Right Condition

| Temper | Condition | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| **7075-T6** | Solution heat treated and artificially aged | Standard machining — maximum strength for most structural applications |
| **7075-T651** | T6 + stress-relieved by stretching | Plate and bar for precision parts — reduces distortion from heavy stock removal |
| **7075-T7351** | Overaged + stress-relieved | Improved corrosion resistance (SCC resistance), lower distortion risk, ~10% lower strength than T6 |
| **7075-T73** | Overaged — maximum SCC resistance | Applications with sustained tensile stress in corrosive environments |

T6 (or T651) is the default for maximum-strength CNC machined parts. T7351 is specified for complex precision parts or where stress corrosion cracking (SCC) resistance is required — defence and aerospace components in sustained-stress environments frequently specify T7351. Confirm the temper at quote stage.

**Aerospace and Defence**
Structural brackets, actuator fittings, wing ribs, bulkhead components. The high fatigue strength and stiffness of 7075-T6 make it standard for primary and secondary aerospace structures. CMM inspection, material traceability to heat number, and Certificate of Conformance included as standard on all aerospace orders.

**Motorsport and Automotive Performance**
Suspension uprights, wishbone brackets, transmission casings, roll cage connectors. Maximum strength-to-weight ratio is the driver. 7075-T6 is specified where 6061 has insufficient yield strength for the load envelope.

**Robotics and Automation**
Structural links, load-bearing joints, actuator housings in high-load robotic systems. Where a 6061 component requires a heavier cross-section to meet stiffness requirements, a thinner 7075 section often delivers equivalent performance at lower weight.

**Medical Devices**
Surgical instrument frames, imaging equipment housings, and structural device components. High strength with low weight suits portable and hand-held device designs. Material traceability standard.

**Electronics and Semiconductor**
Wafer handling arms, precision fixture frames, and high-load test equipment structural parts. Chromate conversion maintains conductivity where grounding paths run through the structure.

**Industrial and Tooling**
High-load fixtures, clamping systems, and structural tooling where 6061 doesn't carry the required load. 7075-T7351 preferred for tooling applications with sustained tensile stress.

## Section 10: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Aluminium 7075 CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on aluminium 7075 CNC machined parts?**
Standard aluminium 7075 CNC work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, bearing bores, sealing faces, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.4 µm surface finish is achievable on specified surfaces. Tolerance achievability depends on geometry, wall section, temper, and tool access — the engineering team assesses this during DFM review. For parts with significant stock removal in 7075-T6, distortion risk from residual stress release is flagged before cutting starts.

**Q2: What is the difference between 7075-T6, T651, and T7351?**
All three start with the same alloy composition. T6 is solution heat treated and artificially aged — it delivers peak strength (572 MPa tensile, 503 MPa yield) and is the standard specification for most machined parts. T651 adds a stress-relief step by stretching, which reduces the distortion risk when heavy material is removed from plate. T7351 is over-aged and stress-relieved: it trades roughly 10% of the T6 strength for improved resistance to stress corrosion cracking (SCC) and reduced distortion risk on complex geometry. T7351 is the standard specification for aerospace and defence parts that see sustained tensile stress in potentially corrosive environments. Specify the temper on your drawing — if unspecified, the engineering team will advise at quote stage.

**Q3: Can aluminium 7075 be welded?**
No — not reliably. 7075 is not recommended for fusion welding (MIG or TIG). The zinc-copper alloy system is highly susceptible to hot cracking during the weld thermal cycle, and the heat-affected zone loses a significant portion of the T6 temper strength. If your assembly requires welded joints, 6061 is the correct aluminium alloy. If your design uses bolted, riveted, or adhesively bonded joints, 7075 is structurally appropriate.

**Q4: What surface finishes work on aluminium 7075?**
Chromate conversion (Alodine, MIL-DTL-5541) is the most reliable corrosion protection for 7075 in aerospace applications — it adds negligible thickness, maintains electrical conductivity, and is consistent across the alloy's copper-zinc composition. Type II anodize works and gives consistent clear results; colour anodize is possible but can show batch variation due to 7075's copper content. Type III hardcoat anodize is consistent for wear surfaces. Powder coat works with proper masking of threads and precision features. Corrosion protection is not optional for 7075 in service — the copper content makes it galvanically active in the presence of moisture.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for aluminium 7075 CNC machining?**
No minimum. One 7075 part is a valid order. Single prototypes, small development batches, and production runs all receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and shipping documentation. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no order floor. If you need pricing at multiple quantities, state them in the quote notes.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for 7075 CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format — it carries full 3D geometry and is what the engineering team uses for toolpath planning and DFM review. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing alongside the STEP file when tight-tolerance callouts, GD&T requirements, or temper specifications appear on the drawing — the 2D drawing becomes the tolerance and specification authority.

**Q7: How much does aluminium 7075 CNC machining cost?**
Cost depends on geometry complexity, tolerance class, quantity, temper specification, and surface finish. A straightforward 7075-T6 bracket with ISO 2768-m tolerances and chromate conversion costs significantly less than a complex structural component in T7351 with ±0.005 mm positional callouts and hardcoat anodize. 7075 material cost is higher than 6061 — typically 20–40% more for equivalent stock. Upload your STEP file for pricing within 24 hours — not an estimate range. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does 7075 compare to 6061 for CNC machined parts — which should I choose?**
The decision comes down to load and weldability. 7075-T6 has roughly twice the yield strength of 6061-T6 (503 MPa vs 276 MPa) at a small weight and cost premium. If your part needs to carry high structural load, resist fatigue under cyclic stress, or fit within a tight envelope that 6061 can't fill at the required cross-section — 7075 is the correct choice. If the design requires welding, or if cost and anodize colour consistency are priorities, 6061 is better suited. When strength is the only criterion and nothing else constrains the choice, 7075-T6 or T651 is the standard high-strength aluminium CNC machining specification.
