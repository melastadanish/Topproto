## Section 2: Why Bronze for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Bronze — Bearing, Wear, and Corrosion Applications

Bronze is the material specified when a part must run against a steel shaft or mating surface, resist wear without lubrication degradation, and survive marine or industrial corrosive environments. Copper-tin bronzes (the classical bronze alloys) provide excellent bearing properties through the combination of copper's natural lubricity, tin's hardness contribution, and the formation of a stable oxide layer. The result is a material that prevents seizing against steel under load, runs quietly, and maintains dimensional integrity under sustained bearing stress.

The distinction from brass is important: brass (copper-zinc) is the machining and fitting material; bronze (copper-tin, copper-aluminium, or copper-silicon) is the bearing, bushing, and wear-resistant material. The overlap in common language — both are "copper alloys" and both are available in bar stock — masks a fundamental difference in application. When the specification is a bushing, a bearing housing, a worm gear, or a marine fitting, bronze is correct.

C932 (leaded tin bronze) is the most widely machined bronze for bearing applications — it combines good machinability (lead inclusions improve chip breaking) with the bearing properties of tin bronze and corrosion resistance adequate for most industrial and marine applications. C954 (aluminium bronze) adds aluminium for significantly higher strength and better corrosion resistance in seawater — used in marine hardware, pump components, and structural applications where tin bronze would be over-stressed.

Bronze machines reasonably well — machinability of C932 is approximately 70% relative to brass C360. Like copper alloys generally, chip control and ductility require attention, but bronze produces better-controlled chips than pure copper.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**Design:** Two-column icon+text grid. White background.

**H2:** Where Bronze CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Bearing and Bushing Applications**
Plain bearings, sleeve bushings, thrust washers, and journal bearings for industrial machinery, agricultural equipment, and general engineering. C932 leaded tin bronze is the standard — low friction against steel, self-lubricating under oil, and dimensionally stable under sustained load.

**Marine and Offshore Hardware**
Propeller shaft bearings, seacock bodies, through-hull fittings, and valve components in seawater service. C954 aluminium bronze for its resistance to seawater corrosion and dezincification — which makes brass and tin bronze unsuitable for sustained seawater immersion. C863 manganese bronze for heavy marine structural applications.

**Pumps and Hydraulic Equipment**
Pump impellers, bearing housings, shaft bushings, and hydraulic cylinder bushings. Bronze's combination of corrosion resistance, wear properties, and machinability makes it the standard for pump and hydraulic bearing components in water and oil service.

**Worm Gears and Power Transmission**
Bronze worm gears run against hardened steel worm shafts — the combination exploits bronze's anti-seizing characteristics and steel's hardness. C932 is the standard worm gear bronze for most power ratings.

**Industrial Valves and Fluid Control**
Valve bodies, gate valves, globe valves, and ball valve components in steam, water, and chemical service. Bronze offers corrosion resistance and machinability that makes it cost-effective for fluid control applications where stainless steel would over-specify cost.

**Architectural and Decorative Hardware**
Handles, hinges, railings, and architectural trim where bronze's warm appearance and corrosion resistance are specified. Silicon bronze (C651) for weldable decorative applications.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Bronze CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on bronze CNC machined parts?**
Standard bronze CNC work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, bearing bores, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, CMM-verified and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.4 µm is achievable as-machined on C932 bearing bores with correct tooling and parameters. Bearing bore tolerances (H7, H6, or H8 as specified) are held to the fit class on the drawing — confirm the fit class and shaft nominal at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between C932, C954, and C863 bronze?**
All three are copper alloys with different secondary alloying elements. C932 (leaded tin bronze) is the standard bearing bronze — tin provides hardness and wear resistance, lead improves machinability and acts as a dry lubricant. It is the correct specification for most plain bearings, bushings, and worm gears in industrial applications. C954 (aluminium bronze) adds aluminium for substantially higher strength (585 MPa tensile vs 240 MPa for C932) and better corrosion resistance in seawater — used for marine hardware, pump impellers, and high-load structural bushings. C863 (manganese bronze) adds zinc and manganese for high strength and marine corrosion resistance — used for heavy marine structural components. Choose C932 for bearings in industrial environments; C954 for marine and high-strength applications.

**Q3: What is the difference between bronze and brass for CNC machined parts?**
Bronze is copper alloyed with tin, aluminium, or silicon — designed for bearing, wear, and corrosion applications. Brass is copper alloyed with zinc — designed for machinability, fluid fittings, and electrical connectors. The overlap in everyday language is significant, but the engineering distinction is clear: brass is the fitting and connector material; bronze is the bearing and bushing material. Brass C360 should not be used for plain bearings against steel shafts — it seizes. C932 bronze should not be used for NPT-threaded fittings where brass is the standard. The choice of material determines the correct alloy family.

**Q4: Is bronze suitable for seawater applications?**
It depends on the grade. C932 (leaded tin bronze) has adequate corrosion resistance in fresh water and atmospheric environments but can suffer dezincification in seawater service over time — it is not the marine-grade specification. C954 (aluminium bronze) resists seawater dezincification and is the correct specification for seawater-immersed hardware — seacocks, through-hull fittings, and marine propeller shaft bearings. C863 (manganese bronze) is also marine-rated for heavy structural applications. Confirm the service environment at quote stage to ensure the correct grade is specified.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for bronze CNC machining?**
No minimum. One bronze part is a valid order. Single prototype bushings, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. There is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for bronze CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when fit class callouts, bore surface finish specifications, or GD&T requirements appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How much does bronze CNC machining cost?**
Bronze CNC machining is moderately priced — C932 material cost is modest, and machinability is good. C954 aluminium bronze material is more expensive and slightly harder to machine than C932. A simple C932 bushing with H7 bore tolerance and as-machined surface costs significantly less than a complex C954 pump housing with multiple sealed surfaces and close-tolerance bores. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does bronze compare to PTFE or polymer bearings for bushing applications?**
Bronze (C932) and PTFE or polymer bearings serve similar functions but with different performance profiles. Bronze handles higher loads, higher temperatures, and better dimensional stability under sustained load than most polymer bearing materials. PTFE and polymer bushings offer lower friction without lubrication, lighter weight, and lower cost for light-duty applications. Bronze is specified when: operating loads are high, temperature is elevated, dimensional stability under load matters, or the part is in oil-lubricated service where the oil maintains the bearing film. Polymer bearings are specified when: weight matters, dry running is required, or the load and temperature are within the polymer's rating.
