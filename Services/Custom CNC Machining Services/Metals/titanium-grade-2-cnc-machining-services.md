## Section 2: Why Titanium Grade 2 for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Titanium Grade 2 — Pure Titanium for Corrosion and Biocompatibility

Titanium Grade 2 is commercially pure titanium — the minimum alloying grade, containing over 99% titanium with controlled limits on iron, oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, and hydrogen. That purity is the source of its two most important properties: outstanding corrosion resistance and confirmed biocompatibility. Grade 2 performs in environments that defeat most other metals — seawater, strong oxidising acids, chlorine-bearing solutions, and direct body fluid contact.

The corrosion resistance comes from the stable titanium dioxide oxide layer that forms spontaneously on the surface in air. Unlike the passive layers on stainless steel, titanium's oxide layer self-heals if scratched or disrupted — it reforms immediately on exposure to air or water. This is what enables Grade 2 to outperform 316L stainless in most corrosive environments at half the weight.

Biocompatibility is the other defining property. Grade 2 is accepted per ISO 10993-1 as a metallic biomaterial — it is non-toxic, non-allergenic, and produces minimal inflammatory response in biological tissue. For medical devices, implant-contact hardware, and surgical instruments where patient contact cannot be avoided, Grade 2 is the standard first specification before engineers move to Grade 5 ELI for structural implant components.

Grade 2 is also weldable — unlike Grade 5, which requires careful process control. For assemblies combining machined and welded joints, Grade 2 enables both operations with conventional TIG welding under inert gas shielding.

## Section 3: Grade 2 Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Titanium Grade 2 Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — Grade 2 (CP Ti):**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 345 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 275 MPa |
| Elongation | 20% |
| Hardness | ~200 HB |
| Density | 4.51 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 21 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | ~40% relative to brass C360 |
| Weldability | Good — TIG under inert gas shielding |

**Machinability notes:**

Grade 2 machines more easily than Grade 5. The lower hardness and higher ductility (20% elongation versus 14% for Grade 5) reduce cutting forces, and the moderate thermal conductivity (21 W/m·K versus 7 W/m·K for Grade 5) means slightly less heat accumulation at the cutting face.

The characteristic that requires attention on Grade 2 is its high ductility — soft, ductile metals produce continuous, stringy chips that wrap around the tool rather than breaking cleanly. Chip control is managed through correct tool geometry (positive rake, sharp edge), high feed rates, and consistent coolant delivery. The same anti-dwell strategy that applies to austenitic stainless applies here: the tool must cut, not rub.

Grade 2 galls under friction. Titanium-to-titanium contact in threaded assemblies is a practical concern — thread milling rather than tapping is preferred, and dissimilar fasteners (stainless or aluminium into Grade 2 bodies) are often the better assembly design.

## Section 5: Surface Finishes for Titanium Grade 2

**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.

**H2:** Surface Finishes for Grade 2 Titanium Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Structural parts, marine hardware, evaluation prototypes |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Uniform matte texture — medical device components |
| Electropolishing | Removes ~5 µm per side | Implant-adjacent surfaces — removes machining layer, improves surface cleanliness |
| Anodize (titanium) | Negligible | Colour coding by oxide thickness — medical device part identification |
| Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Ra to 0.1 µm — implant-contact surfaces |
| Passivation | None | Available on request — titanium self-passivates; passivation cleans rather than fundamentally changes the layer |

**Surface note for Grade 2:** Titanium Grade 2 does not require passivation to achieve corrosion resistance — the native oxide layer provides this automatically. Surface treatment on Grade 2 medical parts is specified to achieve cleanliness and defined surface roughness (Ra) for device validation requirements, not to build corrosion resistance that isn't already there.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Titanium Grade 2 CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on titanium Grade 2 CNC parts?**
Standard Grade 2 work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 1.6 µm is the standard machined surface finish; electropolishing or polishing achieves Ra 0.1–0.4 µm for implant-adjacent surfaces. Grade 2's high ductility requires attention to chip control and tool condition to maintain consistent tolerances across a batch — this is assessed during DFM review.

**Q2: What is the difference between titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V)?**
Grade 2 is commercially pure titanium — maximum corrosion resistance, confirmed biocompatibility, and weldability are its defining properties. Tensile strength is 345 MPa — adequate for many applications but not for high-structural-load parts. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) adds aluminium and vanadium to achieve 950 MPa tensile strength and 880 MPa yield, making it the aerospace and motorsport structural grade. Grade 5 machines more slowly than Grade 2 and standard Grade 5 is not the implant-contact specification (Grade 5 ELI is required for that). Choose Grade 2 when biocompatibility, corrosion resistance, or weldability are the primary requirements; choose Grade 5 when structural load capacity is the priority.

**Q3: Is titanium Grade 2 biocompatible?**
Yes. Grade 2 titanium is accepted per ISO 10993-1 as a biocompatible metallic material — it is non-toxic, non-allergenic, and produces minimal inflammatory response in biological environments. It is the standard specification for medical device housings, surgical instrument components, and implant-adjacent hardware that contacts soft tissue or body fluids. For load-bearing implants where structural strength is also required, Grade 5 ELI (Extra Low Interstitial) is the standard implant-grade specification. TOP PROTO provides heat-number-traceable material certificates and surface treatment documentation (electropolishing, Ra measurement) to support your device validation.

**Q4: What surface finishes are specified for medical Grade 2 titanium parts?**
Medical Grade 2 parts typically require defined surface roughness (Ra) and, for implant-adjacent surfaces, electropolishing or polishing to remove the machined layer and achieve a clean, defined surface. Electropolishing removes approximately 5 µm per side and achieves Ra 0.1–0.4 µm while simultaneously cleaning the surface. Polishing achieves Ra down to 0.1 µm for optical-quality or implant-contact surfaces. Titanium anodizing (colour by oxide thickness control) is used for medical device part identification — colours are produced without dyes, by controlling the oxide layer thickness. Specify the required Ra value and any treatment standard on your drawing.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for Grade 2 titanium CNC machining?**
No minimum. One Grade 2 titanium part is a valid order. Single prototypes and development batches receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation as production runs. There is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for Grade 2 titanium CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, Ra surface finish specifications, implant-grade designations, or GD&T callouts appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How much does titanium Grade 2 CNC machining cost?**
Grade 2 titanium machines faster than Grade 5 but still costs significantly more than aluminium or stainless steel — typically 3–5× more than equivalent 6061 aluminium work, driven by longer cycle times (lower machinability), higher material cost, and surface treatment requirements for medical applications. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does Grade 2 titanium compare to 316L stainless steel for corrosion resistance?**
Grade 2 titanium outperforms 316L stainless in most corrosive environments. In seawater and chloride environments, Grade 2 is essentially immune to corrosion where 316L experiences pitting over time. In oxidising acids (nitric acid, chromic acid), Grade 2 is resistant where 316L would corrode. In reducing acids (hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid), both metals are attacked — neither is universally corrosion-proof. Titanium is also half the weight of 316L stainless (4.51 g/cm³ versus 8.0 g/cm³). The tradeoff is cost and machinability — 316L machines significantly faster and at lower cost. For marine, medical implant-adjacent, and electrochemical applications where corrosion resistance is the primary driver, Grade 2 titanium is the correct specification over 316L.
