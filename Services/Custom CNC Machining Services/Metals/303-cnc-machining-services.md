## Section 2: Why 303 for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** 303 Stainless Steel — The Free-Machining Grade

303 is the free-machining stainless steel. Sulphur is added to the alloy during steelmaking — it forms manganese sulphide inclusions that act as internal chip breakers. The cutting tool shears through these inclusions cleanly, producing short chips that evacuate the cut rather than wrapping around the tool. That translates directly to faster cycle times, longer tool life, and lower cost compared to 304 or 316L on equivalent geometry.

In practice, 303 machines at roughly 45% of the machinability reference of brass C360 — compared to 40% for 304 and around 40% for 316L. The difference sounds small in percentage terms, but on complex parts with many features and long cycle times, it compounds. 303 is the specification engineers reach for when the part requires stainless steel but will not be welded and does not need chloride-grade corrosion resistance.

The tradeoffs are well-defined and specific. 303 cannot be welded — the sulphur content causes hot cracking during fusion welding. 303 should not be used in marine, chloride, or highly corrosive environments — it performs well in atmospheric and mild conditions but is not the grade for parts submerged in seawater or exposed to cleaning acids. For those applications, 316L is the correct specification.

Within those constraints, 303 is used extensively for precision shafts, connector bodies, standoffs, fittings, valve components, and instrument hardware — parts where tight tolerances and good surface finish matter, and where the environment is dry, atmospheric, or mildly humid.

## Section 3: 303 Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** 303 Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — 303:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 620 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 345 MPa |
| Elongation | 50% |
| Hardness | 262 HB |
| Density | 8.0 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 16 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | ~45% relative to brass C360 |
| Weldability | Not recommended — sulphur causes hot cracking |

**Machinability notes:**

303 machines noticeably better than 304 or 316L. The manganese sulphide inclusions break the chip at the cutting face — tool engagement produces short, discrete chips rather than the long stringers that characterise 304 at the same feed rate. This is the primary practical advantage: fewer stoppages for chip clearance, lower risk of chip-re-cutting damage, and faster program cycle times.

Work hardening still occurs on 303 — it is an austenitic grade and the mechanism is the same as 304. The rate is lower because the sulphide inclusions disrupt the continuous deformation zone at the cutting face. Correct cutting parameters (high feed rate, sharp tooling, avoid dwell) still apply, but the sensitivity is lower.

Surface finish on 303 is excellent. The free-machining characteristics produce a clean shear surface, and Ra 0.8 µm or better is achievable as-machined on flat and cylindrical surfaces. Polishing or bead blasting can refine this further where cosmetic requirements are specified.

**Note on passivation for 303:** Passivation is available on 303 parts but its benefit is more limited than on 316L. The sulphide inclusions in 303 create discontinuities in the passive layer that cannot be fully restored by passivation — this is part of why 303 has lower corrosion resistance than 304 in aggressive environments. For parts in atmospheric or mildly humid environments, as-machined or bead-blasted 303 is appropriate. For parts requiring the highest corrosion resistance in the stainless family, specify 316L.

RF connector shells, SMA connector machined bodies, waveguide hardware, and precision instrument enclosures. 303 holds tight tolerances on fine threads (M2, M3) and small bores with consistent chip control.

**Instrumentation and Metrology**
Precision shafts, gauge components, micrometer anvils, and measurement fixture hardware. 303's machinability allows fine surface finishes and tight roundness tolerances on turned parts. Hardness is consistent batch to batch when material is verified against certificate.

**Hydraulic and Pneumatic Fittings**
Valve bodies, spool components, manifold fittings, and port plugs in dry or oil-based fluid service. 303 machines port features and fine threads reliably. Not suitable for hydraulic systems using water-based fluids in contaminated or marine environments — 316L for those.

**Automotive and Industrial Fasteners**
Precision bolts, studs, pins, and shoulder screws in dry industrial environments. 303's higher yield strength than 304 (345 MPa vs 205 MPa) is an advantage in fastener applications. Not for outdoor or marine fastener use.

**Consumer Products and Appliances**
Knobs, handles, trim pieces, and functional hardware requiring a stainless appearance in indoor environments. 303 machines quickly for high-volume prototype and short-run parts. Bead blast or polished finish depending on cosmetic specification.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** 303 Stainless Steel CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on 303 stainless steel CNC machined parts?**
Standard 303 work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, bearing bores, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.8 µm is the standard machined surface finish; polishing achieves Ra 0.1 µm on specified surfaces. 303's free-machining characteristics make it one of the better stainless grades for holding tight tolerances consistently across a batch — chip control is more predictable than on 304 or 316L.

**Q2: What is the difference between 303, 304, and 316L stainless steel?**
All three are austenitic stainless steels with similar chromium-nickel compositions, but with different additives that change their properties. 303 adds sulphur for free-machining capability — shorter chips, faster cycle times, lower cost — but cannot be welded and has lower corrosion resistance in aggressive environments. 304 is the general-purpose grade: weldable, good corrosion resistance, no special additives. 316L adds molybdenum for chloride and pitting resistance and uses a low-carbon variant for weld zone stability — it is the correct grade for marine, medical, and food-contact applications. Choose 303 when you need stainless without welding or corrosive environments; 304 for general weldable applications; 316L for chloride environments, medical devices, or food contact.

**Q3: Can 303 stainless steel be welded?**
No. 303 should not be fusion-welded. The sulphur content that gives 303 its free-machining properties forms low-melting-point compounds at grain boundaries during welding. Those compounds cause hot cracking in the weld and heat-affected zone, producing a structurally unsound joint. For any design with welded joints, specify 304 or 316L. If the design uses mechanical fasteners or adhesive bonding throughout, 303 is appropriate.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for 303 stainless steel parts?**
As-machined, bead blasted, brushed, polished, and black oxide are all available on 303. Passivation per ASTM A967 is available but provides less benefit on 303 than on 304 or 316L — the sulphide inclusions create discontinuities in the passive layer that passivation cannot restore. Electropolishing is not recommended for 303 as the sulphide inclusions etch unevenly, producing a non-uniform surface. For parts requiring electropolishing or maximum corrosion resistance, specify 316L. For cosmetic and functional surface treatment on 303 in atmospheric or dry environments, bead blast or polishing is appropriate.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for 303 CNC machining?**
No minimum. One 303 part is a valid order. Single prototypes, small development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and shipping documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for 303 CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish designations, or GD&T callouts appear — the 2D drawing becomes the specification authority for those requirements.

**Q7: How much does 303 stainless steel CNC machining cost?**
303 CNC machining is less expensive than equivalent 304 or 316L work because cycle times are shorter and tool life is longer. A standard 303 connector body or shaft will typically cost 15–25% less to machine than the same geometry in 304. Material cost for 303 bar stock is similar to 304. The cost advantage grows with part complexity and feature count. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours — not an estimate range. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: When should I choose 303 over aluminium for a CNC machined part?**
Choose 303 when the part needs higher hardness, greater wear resistance, or stainless corrosion resistance — and does not require low weight. Aluminium machines faster and is lighter (2.7 g/cm³ versus 8.0 g/cm³ for 303), but 303 has significantly higher hardness, better wear resistance for sliding contact surfaces, and inherent corrosion resistance without surface coating in dry environments. For precision shafts, pins, and connector bodies where weight is not a constraint and the surface needs to be hard and corrosion-resistant, 303 is the correct specification. For structural brackets where weight matters and anodizing provides adequate surface protection, aluminium is typically the right choice.
