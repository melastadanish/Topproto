## Section 2: Why Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Ti-6Al-4V — The Aerospace and High-Performance Structural Alloy

Titanium Grade 5 — designated Ti-6Al-4V — is the most widely used titanium alloy, accounting for the majority of all titanium machined globally. The 6% aluminium and 4% vanadium alloying additions raise the yield strength to 880 MPa and tensile strength to 950 MPa, while the density remains at 4.43 g/cm³. That combination — strength comparable to alloy steel at roughly half the weight — defines the application range: aerospace primary structure, high-load brackets, surgical instruments, and motorsport components where every gram of mass removed from the load path matters.

The strength-to-weight advantage is the reason Grade 5 is specified over Grade 2 for structural applications, and over aluminium where load or temperature requirements cannot be met by aluminium alloys. An aluminium 7075-T6 component at 2.81 g/cm³ and 503 MPa yield offers a better specific strength than Grade 5 for some loading conditions — but at elevated temperatures, under fatigue loading, or where corrosion resistance is required alongside structural performance, Grade 5 is the preferred specification.

Grade 5 is more demanding to machine than Grade 2 or aluminium. The alloy's low thermal conductivity (7 W/m·K) causes heat to accumulate at the cutting face rather than conduct into the workpiece. Combined with high hardness (~330 HB in the annealed condition) and chemical reactivity at temperature, this produces rapid tool wear unless cutting parameters, tooling specification, and coolant delivery are correctly managed. Cycle times for Grade 5 at equivalent features are significantly longer than for aluminium or stainless steel — this is the direct driver of higher per-part cost.

For implant-contact applications, Grade 5 ELI (Extra Low Interstitial) is required — standard Grade 5 oxygen and iron content is above the implant-grade specification.

## Section 3: Grade 5 Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Titanium Grade 5 Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V):**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 950 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 880 MPa |
| Elongation | 14% |
| Hardness | ~330 HB |
| Density | 4.43 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 7 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | ~20–25% relative to brass C360 |
| Weldability | Good — TIG under inert gas shielding |

**Machinability notes:**

Grade 5 is the most challenging of the common CNC machined metals. Thermal conductivity of 7 W/m·K (versus 167 W/m·K for aluminium 6061) means almost all heat generated at the cutting face remains in the workpiece and tool interface rather than dissipating. The alloy maintains its hardness and chemical reactivity at elevated temperature — unlike steel, which softens as it heats, titanium stays hard, and the elevated temperature makes it chemically reactive with the cutting tool at the contact zone.

The correct machining strategy: sharp, coated carbide tooling (TiAlN or AlCrN), lower cutting speeds than aluminium or stainless, high feed rates to move the heat zone through the workpiece before it accumulates, and flood coolant at high pressure continuously applied to the cutting zone. Dwell is the worst machining error on Grade 5 — a stopped or dragging tool at temperature causes rapid tool failure and potential workpiece damage.

Chip evacuation is critical. Grade 5 chips are hot and reactive — positive rake geometry produces shorter chips that clear the cut more easily. Chip entanglement in deep pockets or holes is a safety and quality risk that the machining strategy must address.

## Section 10: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Titanium Grade 5 CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on titanium Grade 5 CNC parts?**
Standard Grade 5 work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, bearing bores, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 1.6 µm is the standard machined surface finish on Grade 5; polishing or electropolishing achieves Ra 0.1–0.4 µm for surgical instrument and implant-adjacent surfaces. Grade 5's low thermal conductivity and high hardness make tool condition monitoring essential for consistent tolerances across a batch — this is managed during production and confirmed at final inspection.

**Q2: What is the difference between Grade 5 and Grade 5 ELI titanium?**
Both are Ti-6Al-4V — the same nominal alloy composition. ELI (Extra Low Interstitial) limits the content of interstitial elements: oxygen to 0.13% max (versus 0.20% for standard Grade 5), iron to 0.25% max (versus 0.30%), and nitrogen to 0.05% max. These limits improve fracture toughness and fatigue crack growth resistance in biological environments — the properties that matter for implant contact in bone and tissue. Standard Grade 5 is not the correct specification for implant-contact use; Grade 5 ELI (also designated Grade 23 per ASTM F136) is. For all non-implant aerospace, motorsport, and engineering applications, standard Grade 5 is the correct and more economical specification.

**Q3: Why is Grade 5 titanium CNC machining expensive?**
Grade 5 titanium CNC machining costs significantly more than aluminium or stainless steel work for three reasons. First, cycle time: Grade 5's thermal conductivity is 7 W/m·K versus 167 W/m·K for aluminium — heat accumulates at the cutting face, requiring lower cutting speeds and more careful parameters. Grade 5 takes roughly 5–10× longer to machine than equivalent 6061 aluminium geometry. Second, tool consumption: coated carbide tooling on Grade 5 has shorter effective life than on aluminium, increasing cost per part. Third, material cost: Grade 5 bar stock costs substantially more than aluminium per kilogram. All three add up. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours — DFM review is included.

**Q4: What is the Grade 5 ELI standard for medical implants?**
Grade 5 ELI titanium for implant-contact applications is specified per ASTM F136 (Standard Specification for Wrought Titanium-6Aluminum-4Vanadium ELI Alloy for Surgical Implant Applications). This standard sets tighter limits on oxygen, iron, nitrogen, and carbon content than standard Grade 5. It also specifies mechanical properties — yield strength minimum 795 MPa, tensile minimum 860 MPa — and surface finish requirements for implant manufacture. Orthopedic and spinal implant manufacturers routinely specify Grade 23 (the ASTM alternative designation for Grade 5 ELI) on their drawings. State the required standard on your drawing or purchase order — TOP PROTO provides material certification that traces to the applicable specification.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for Grade 5 titanium CNC machining?**
No minimum. One Grade 5 part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time and tooling cost are fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for Grade 5 titanium CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish Ra specifications, ELI grade requirements, or GD&T callouts appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How long does Grade 5 titanium CNC machining take?**
Standard Grade 5 CNC parts ship in 4–7 business days from the Dongguan facility. Complex geometry with multiple setups, deep features, or fine threads may require 5–7 business days. Lead time is confirmed per geometry and material availability at quoting — not an estimate. If your project has a specific deadline, state it when uploading your file.

**Q8: When should I use Grade 5 titanium instead of 7075 aluminium for a structural part?**
Grade 5 titanium and 7075 aluminium compete in the high-strength, low-weight design space. 7075-T6 has better specific strength at room temperature (503 MPa yield at 2.81 g/cm³ — roughly the same strength-to-weight ratio as Grade 5). Choose Grade 5 over 7075 when: the operating temperature exceeds approximately 120°C (aluminium loses strength rapidly above this, titanium does not); the part requires corrosion resistance without surface treatment; the application involves fatigue loading at high mean stress where titanium has better fatigue crack initiation resistance; or the application is implant-contact or biologically regulated. Choose 7075 over Grade 5 when weight-to-strength at room temperature is the primary criterion and cost is a constraint — 7075 machines significantly faster and at much lower cost.
