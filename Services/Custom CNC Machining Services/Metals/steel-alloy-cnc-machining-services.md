## Section 2: Why Alloy Steel for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Alloy Steel — Structural Strength at Lower Cost Than Titanium

Alloy steels — 4140, 4340, and related chromium-molybdenum grades — fill the structural performance gap between mild carbon steel and stainless steel or titanium. They combine high tensile strength (above 900 MPa in normalised condition, above 1000 MPa when heat treated), good toughness, and machinability that is significantly better than tool steel or titanium — at a material cost that is a fraction of either.

The alloy additions (chromium, molybdenum, nickel in 4340) serve two purposes. First, they increase hardenability — alloy steels respond more uniformly to quench and temper heat treatment than plain carbon steels, achieving high hardness throughout a thick cross-section rather than just at the surface. Second, they improve toughness at a given hardness level, reducing the brittleness that affects plain high-carbon steels.

4140 (chromium-molybdenum steel) is the general-purpose grade — the standard specification for shafts, gears, bolts, structural brackets, and precision components where strength above mild steel is required. 4340 adds nickel to the chromium-molybdenum composition, improving toughness and through-hardening in large cross-sections — it is the aerospace-grade alloy steel for landing gear forgings, large structural components, and high-fatigue applications.

The machining strategy depends on the condition of the stock. In the normalised or annealed condition, 4140 and 4340 machine well with standard carbide tooling at reasonable speeds. In the pre-hardened condition (28–35 HRC), feed rates and cutting speeds are reduced. Hardened and tempered (40–50+ HRC) — hard milling with CBN tooling or grinding is required.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Alloy Steel CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on alloy steel CNC machined parts?**
Standard alloy steel CNC work in normalised or annealed condition follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions. Precision features hold to ±0.005 mm, CMM-verified. In pre-hardened condition (28–35 HRC), the same tolerances are achievable with appropriate tooling and parameters. On heat-treated stock (40+ HRC), finish tolerances are achieved by grinding or hard milling, where ±0.005 mm and Ra 0.1–0.4 µm are standard. Confirm the delivery condition and machining sequence at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between 4140 and 4340 alloy steel?**
Both are chromium-molybdenum alloy steels, but 4340 adds nickel to the composition. The nickel significantly improves toughness and through-hardening — 4340 achieves high hardness uniformly in large cross-sections where 4140 would have a soft core. 4140 is the general-purpose grade — most shafts, gears, bolts, and structural components. 4340 is the aerospace and heavy engineering grade — landing gear, large shafts, and high-fatigue components where maximum fatigue strength in large cross-sections is required. Material cost for 4340 is modestly higher than 4140. Confirm which grade is specified on your drawing; if unspecified, the engineering team will ask about the application.

**Q3: Should alloy steel CNC parts be hardened before or after machining?**
The standard sequence is to machine in the normalised or annealed condition, then heat treat. This approach takes advantage of the better machinability of the soft material while achieving the final mechanical properties after the geometry is formed. Post-heat treatment finish machining — by hard milling, grinding, or wire EDM — is used for features requiring tight tolerances that cannot be maintained through the heat treatment dimensional change. P20 and some pre-hardened 4140 grades (28–35 HRC) are machined directly without post-machine heat treatment, but the sequence for your part depends on the required final hardness and tolerance — this is confirmed at DFM review.

**Q4: Is alloy steel corrosion-resistant?**
Alloy steels (4140, 4340) are not corrosion-resistant in the way that stainless steel or aluminium are. They will corrode in the presence of moisture without surface protection. For parts in service environments with any humidity or moisture contact, a surface treatment is required: black oxide with oil, electroless nickel, phosphate and paint, or zinc coating are the common options. For applications in direct water or chemical contact, stainless steel is the more appropriate material choice.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for alloy steel CNC machining?**
No minimum. One alloy steel part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. Heat treatment adds lead time and minimum batch considerations — confirm at quote if your order involves heat treatment.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for alloy steel CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when heat treatment specifications, hardness callouts, tolerance callouts, or GD&T requirements appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How much does alloy steel CNC machining cost?**
Alloy steel CNC machining is moderate in cost — material is less expensive than titanium or stainless, and machinability in the normalised condition is reasonable. Heat treatment adds both cost and lead time. Pre-hardened 4140 (28–35 HRC) eliminates heat treatment but increases cycle time. A simple 4140 normalised shaft with standard tolerances costs significantly less than a complex 4340 aerospace component machined in normalised condition, heat treated to 48 HRC, and finish ground. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does alloy steel compare to stainless steel for structural CNC parts?**
Alloy steel (4140 Q&T) achieves higher tensile and yield strength than most austenitic stainless grades at equivalent cross-section — 4140 at 48 HRC reaches 1550 MPa tensile versus 485–620 MPa for 304 or 316L stainless. However, alloy steel corrodes without surface treatment, while stainless is inherently corrosion-resistant. Alloy steel is the specification when maximum structural strength is the priority and the service environment is dry or can be protected by surface treatment. Stainless steel is the specification when corrosion resistance, biocompatibility, or cleanroom environment requirements exist. For applications where both high strength and corrosion resistance are needed, 17-4PH precipitation-hardening stainless or titanium Grade 5 are the alternatives.
