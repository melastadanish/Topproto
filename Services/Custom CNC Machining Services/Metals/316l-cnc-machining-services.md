## Section 2: Why 316L for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** 316L — The Medical and Marine Grade

316L is the molybdenum-bearing grade of austenitic stainless steel. The "L" designation means low carbon — carbon content below 0.03% — which prevents carbide precipitation at grain boundaries during welding and heat exposure. The molybdenum content (2–3%) is what sets 316L apart from 304: it provides substantially better resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion in chloride environments.

Those two properties — weldability without sensitisation and chloride resistance — make 316L the default stainless steel for medical devices, surgical instruments, food-contact equipment, marine hardware, and chemical process components. Anywhere the part encounters body fluids, saline, seawater, cleaning agents, or acidic process chemicals, 316L is the correct specification over 304.

Machinability is slightly lower than 304 due to the molybdenum content, which increases work-hardening rate and tool wear at equivalent cutting speeds. The difference is manageable with correct parameters — coated carbide tooling, consistent coolant application, and avoiding dwell at the cutting face. Cycle times for equivalent 316L and 304 parts typically differ by 10–20%.

The surface treatment expectation on 316L is higher than on most other stainless grades. Passivation per ASTM A967 is standard for medical and food-contact parts — it removes free iron deposited by tooling and restores the chromium oxide passive layer. Electropolishing reduces Ra to 0.1–0.4 µm and simultaneously passivates. Both are included in standard quoting for 316L medical and food-contact orders.

## Section 3: 316L Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** 316L Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — 316L:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 485 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 170 MPa |
| Elongation | 40% |
| Hardness | 217 HB |
| Density | 8.0 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 16 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | ~40% relative to brass C360 |
| Weldability | Excellent — low carbon prevents sensitisation |

**Machining notes:**

316L work-hardens during cutting. The machining strategy that fails on 316L is low feed rate and high spindle speed with a worn tool — the tool rubs rather than cuts, and the surface compresses and hardens ahead of the next pass. Correct approach: sharp coated carbide tooling, high feed rate relative to depth of cut, consistent coolant flooding, and tool change at schedule rather than at failure.

Thermal conductivity is low (16 W/m·K versus 167 W/m·K for aluminium 6061). Heat generated at the cutting face stays in the part rather than conducting away — coolant application is not optional, it's the primary heat management mechanism. This is why flood coolant is standard on all 316L operations.

Thread milling is preferred over tapping on 316L for critical threads — thread milling produces less work hardening in the thread form and is more controllable on tight-tolerance thread callouts.

## Section 5: Surface Finishes for 316L

**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.

**H2:** Surface Finishes for 316L CNC Machined Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Structural prototypes, internal components |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Uniform matte surface — medical device housings |
| Passivation (ASTM A967) | None | Standard on all 316L medical and food-contact orders |
| Electropolishing | Removes ~5 µm per side | Medical devices, food equipment — Ra 0.1–0.4 µm; passivates simultaneously |
| Brushed Finish | Negligible | Directional satin — panels, instrument surfaces |
| Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Ra down to 0.1 µm for optical-quality or cosmetic surfaces |

**Electropolishing vs passivation on 316L:** Both improve corrosion resistance by removing surface contamination and reinforcing the chromium oxide passive layer. Electropolishing also reduces surface roughness to Ra 0.1–0.4 µm — it removes the micro-peaks left by machining that create sites for bacterial adhesion and chemical attack. For medical implant-adjacent components and pharmaceutical process equipment, electropolishing is the standard. For general medical device housings and food equipment, passivation per ASTM A967 is sufficient.

## Section 6: Design Guidelines for 316L CNC Parts

**Design:** Table. White background.

**H2:** Design Guidelines for 316L CNC Machining

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min wall thickness | 1.0 mm | 316L is stiffer than aluminium but thin walls deflect under higher cutting forces |
| Internal corner radius | Min 1/3 of cavity depth | Tool diameter governs radius — 316L requires sharp tools to avoid work hardening in the corner |
| Thread specification | State thread class and engagement | 316L galls against stainless fasteners — specify thread class and consider anti-galling coating on mating threads |
| Electropolishing allowance | ~5 µm per side | Pre-machine to electropolishing allowance on precision fits if electropolishing is specified |
| Crevice geometry | Minimise where possible | Crevices accumulate chloride concentration even in 316L — design consideration for highly corrosive service |
| Weld joint design | Standard MIG/TIG acceptable | Low carbon content prevents sensitisation — specify heat input limits on thicker sections for critical welds |

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** 316L CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on 316L stainless steel CNC parts?**
Standard 316L work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, sealing faces, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.8 µm is the standard machined surface finish; electropolishing achieves Ra 0.1–0.4 µm. Work hardening in 316L is the primary challenge on tight features — cutting parameters are adjusted to maintain consistent material removal without surface hardening, and this is assessed during DFM review before production starts.

**Q2: What is the difference between 316L and 316 stainless steel?**
The "L" in 316L means low carbon — carbon content is limited to 0.03% maximum, compared to 0.08% in standard 316. The practical difference is weld performance. Carbon above 0.03% precipitates as chromium carbide at grain boundaries during the welding thermal cycle — this is called sensitisation and it reduces corrosion resistance in the heat-affected zone, potentially causing intergranular corrosion in service. 316L avoids sensitisation entirely, which is why it is the standard specification for all welded assemblies and for parts that undergo heat exposure in service. In machining-only applications with no welding, the mechanical properties of 316 and 316L are effectively the same.

**Q3: Is 316L stainless steel biocompatible for medical devices?**
316L meets the material requirements for many medical device applications. It is referenced in ISO 10993-1 as an acceptable metallic biomaterial and is widely used for surgical instruments, device housings, and implant-adjacent components. Biocompatibility confirmation for a specific application depends on the combination of material, surface finish, and device classification — it is not a blanket statement about the alloy. For regulated medical device projects, the material certificate, surface finish specification, and any cleaning/sterilisation validation are the buyer's responsibility. TOP PROTO provides heat-number-traceable material certificates and electropolishing and passivation records to support your validation documentation.

**Q4: What surface finish should I specify for medical 316L parts?**
Passivation per ASTM A967 removes free iron from the machined surface and restores the chromium oxide passive layer — it is the minimum standard for medical 316L parts. Electropolishing reduces Ra to 0.1–0.4 µm, removes a thin surface layer (~5 µm), and simultaneously passivates — it is the standard for implant-adjacent and pharmaceutical-contact components where surface cleanliness and smoothness are both required. Specify the surface finish and any relevant standard (ASTM A967 for passivation, or an Ra value for electropolishing) on your drawing or in the quote notes.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for 316L CNC machining?**
No minimum. One 316L part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and shipping documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for 316L CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is preferred for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish designations, passivation or electropolishing requirements, or GD&T callouts appear — the 2D drawing becomes the specification authority for those requirements.

**Q7: How much does 316L CNC machining cost?**
316L CNC machining costs more than 304 or 303 work due to slower cycle times (higher work-hardening rate), higher material cost, and typical finishing requirements (passivation or electropolishing). A simple 316L fitting with standard tolerances and passivation costs significantly less than a complex medical housing with ±0.005 mm positional callouts and electropolishing. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours — not an estimate range. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does 316L compare to 303 stainless steel for CNC machined parts?**
303 machines faster and at lower cost — the free-machining sulphur addition produces short chips and reduces cycle time by 20–30% compared to 316L. But 303 cannot be welded, has lower corrosion resistance in chloride environments, and is not suitable for medical or food-contact applications. 316L is the correct grade when corrosion resistance, weldability, or biocompatibility is required. 303 is correct when those properties are not required and machinability and cost are the priority. The choice is made at the drawing stage — if the application is medical or food-contact, 316L is specified.
