## Section 2: Why Brass for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Brass — The Benchmark for CNC Machinability

Brass is the machinability reference material. The machinability rating system used throughout precision machining defines all other metals relative to free-machining brass C360 at 100%. When machinability ratings are quoted for aluminium (~50%), stainless steel (~40%), or titanium (~20%), those percentages mean "how fast can we cut this compared to C360 brass." Brass machines faster, cleaner, and with less tool wear than almost any other structural metal.

The reasons are practical: the zinc and lead alloying additions in C360 produce short, brittle chips that break cleanly and clear the cut without wrapping or jamming. Cutting forces are low. Tool wear is minimal. Surface finishes come off the cutter excellent — Ra 0.4 µm or better as-machined on brass is achievable without post-machining finishing operations. For connectors, fittings, valves, and precision hardware that needs to come off the machine ready to use, brass is the first specification engineers reach for.

Beyond machinability, brass offers electrical and thermal conductivity, natural antimicrobial properties, excellent corrosion resistance in atmospheric conditions, and the ability to be gold-plated, nickel-plated, or polished to a bright finish. C360 is the standard machining grade. C260 (cartridge brass) is specified for parts requiring better cold-forming or welding capability. C464 (naval brass) is specified for seawater applications.

Brass is heavier than aluminium (8.5 g/cm³ versus 2.7 g/cm³) and cannot match stainless steel in strength. For applications where weight or structural load is the primary constraint, brass is not the first choice. For plumbing fittings, electrical connectors, precision valves, and decorative hardware, it is the standard.

## Section 3: Brass Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Brass C360 Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties table — C360 (Free-Machining Brass):**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 385 MPa |
| Yield Strength (0.2%) | 310 MPa |
| Elongation | 25% |
| Hardness | 80 HRB |
| Density | 8.5 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 115 W/m·K |
| Electrical Conductivity | ~26% IACS |
| Machinability Rating | 100% (the reference) |
| Weldability | Limited — lead content reduces weldability |

**Machinability notes:**

C360 sets the machining standard. Spindle speeds can be pushed to the machine's limit before heat or tool wear becomes a constraint. Feed rates 3–5× higher than stainless steel are typical. Chips are short, brittle, and clear the cut automatically — no chip wrapping, no cutting into re-entered chips. The as-machined surface is excellent across all features.

The only considerations specific to C360 in machining are: the lead content in the alloy (which creates regulatory questions in some end uses — drinking water contact, RoHS environments) and the material's tendency to produce slightly built-up edge on some tooling geometries. Uncoated carbide or high-speed steel tooling works well on brass. Coated tooling is not required and some TiN coatings can actually reduce surface quality on brass relative to sharp uncoated tooling.

For lead-free applications (plumbing under NSF 61, RoHS-compliant products), C260 or bismuth-brass grades are specified. These machine slightly less freely than C360 but are still among the most machinable metals available.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**Design:** Two-column icon+text grid. White background.

**H2:** Where Brass CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Fluid and Pneumatic Fittings**
Valve bodies, compression fittings, push-to-connect fittings, manifold blocks, and NPT/BSP threaded connectors. Brass is the default material for plumbing fittings — machinability, corrosion resistance, and thread quality make it the specification that fitting manufacturers return to consistently. Naval brass (C464) for potable water and marine applications.

**Electrical and Electronic Connectors**
Connector shells, terminals, pin contacts, and relay components. Brass's electrical conductivity (~26% IACS), ability to be gold or tin plated, and excellent machinability make it standard for connector hardware. Gold plating on contact surfaces for minimum contact resistance.

**Precision Instruments and Gauges**
Bourdon tube pressure gauges, flow meter components, valve stems, and instrument housings. Brass's machinability produces consistent thread quality and bore tolerances across high-volume precision runs. Natural antimicrobial properties are a secondary benefit for medical instrument applications.

**Decorative and Architectural Hardware**
Handles, knobs, lock components, brackets, and fixture hardware. Polished or brushed brass finishes are specified for architectural applications. Bright nickel plating provides a more durable surface than bare brass in high-touch applications.

**Automotive and Industrial Valves**
Fuel system fittings, hydraulic nipples, pneumatic valve bodies, and coolant fittings in non-corrosive service. C360 for standard service; C464 where water or mild chemical contact is expected.

**Telecommunications**
RF connector hardware, coaxial connector bodies, and waveguide fittings. Brass's electrical and RF properties, combined with plating options, make it standard for RF connector machining.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Brass CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on brass CNC machined parts?**
Brass is one of the most dimensionally stable metals to machine — C360's machinability and chip control characteristics make tight tolerances more consistent across a batch than on most other metals. Standard work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.4 µm is achievable as-machined on brass — better than the standard machined surface finish on stainless steel or titanium without additional finishing operations.

**Q2: What is the difference between brass grades C360, C260, and C464?**
C360 is free-machining brass — the highest-machinability grade, containing approximately 3% lead. It is the standard specification for most machined fittings, valves, and connector hardware. C260 (cartridge brass, 70/30 copper-zinc) has no lead and is better suited for cold-forming and welding, but machines slightly less freely than C360. C464 (naval brass) adds tin to the 60/40 copper-zinc composition — the tin provides resistance to dezincification in seawater and potable water environments. For drinking water contact (NSF 61), food contact, or RoHS-restricted applications, specify a lead-free grade (C260, bismuth-brass, or C464) on the drawing.

**Q3: Can brass CNC parts be plated?**
Yes — brass is an excellent substrate for electroplating. Gold plating is standard for electrical connector contacts (minimum contact resistance, corrosion protection). Nickel plating (electroless) provides wear and corrosion resistance with good dimensional control (5–25 µm per side). Tin plating produces a solder-compatible surface for electronic applications. Chrome plating is available for decorative and wear applications. State the plating type, thickness, and any masking requirements on your drawing — plating thickness must be accounted for on precision fits before the pre-plate machining dimensions are set.

**Q4: Is C360 brass lead-free and RoHS compliant?**
C360 contains approximately 2.5–3% lead as a free-machining additive. Standard C360 is not RoHS compliant for lead-containing components, and is not compliant with NSF 61 for potable water contact. For lead-free or RoHS-compliant applications, specify C260 (cartridge brass) or a bismuth-brass grade on the drawing. Both are available and machine well — C260 slightly less freely than C360 but still among the best-machining copper alloys. Confirm the required grade at quote stage — material verification against the correct alloy certification is part of the pre-production check.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for brass CNC machining?**
No minimum. One brass part is a valid order. Single prototypes, small batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. Brass's excellent machinability means setup time is comparatively short — per-unit cost on small quantities is lower than for harder materials.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for brass CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, thread specifications, plating requirements, or GD&T callouts appear — the drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How much does brass CNC machining cost?**
Brass CNC machining is among the lowest-cost metal machining options — fast cycle times and minimal tool wear translate to lower per-part cost than stainless, titanium, or even aluminium on many geometries. Material cost (C360 bar stock) is moderate. Plating adds cost depending on type and batch size. A simple C360 fitting with NPT threads and as-machined finish costs significantly less than an equivalent 316L stainless part. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does brass compare to aluminium for CNC machined connector and fitting parts?**
Both machine well, but for different applications. Brass is heavier (8.5 g/cm³ versus 2.7 g/cm³ for aluminium) but has better corrosion resistance in atmospheric and fluid environments without surface treatment, better electrical conductivity, and can be gold or nickel plated to MIL-spec connector standards. Aluminium is lighter and anodizes for corrosion and wear protection, but is not the standard for threaded fluid fittings (brass holds NPT and BSP thread quality better over use cycles) and is not the material for electrical connector contacts. Choose brass for fluid fittings, electrical connectors, and decorative hardware. Choose aluminium for structural brackets, enclosures, and heat sinks where weight matters.
