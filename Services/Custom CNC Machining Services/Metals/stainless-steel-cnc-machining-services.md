## Section 2: Why Stainless Steel for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Stainless Steel — Corrosion Resistance with Structural Strength

Stainless steel is the material specification when a part needs corrosion resistance, strength, and dimensional stability in a single alloy. Aluminium handles some of those requirements, but stainless handles them at higher temperature, under sustained load, and in more aggressive chemical environments. The tradeoff is cycle time and cost — stainless takes 3–5× longer to machine than aluminium at equivalent features.

The austenitic grades — 303, 304, and 316L — are the most widely machined. They offer excellent corrosion resistance, good weldability (304 and 316L), and wide availability. 303 is the free-machining grade, adding sulphur to the alloy to improve chip breaking — it's the first choice when machinability matters more than maximum corrosion resistance or weldability. 304 is the general-purpose grade. 316L is the marine and medical grade, with molybdenum content that resists chloride-induced pitting.

Precipitation-hardening grades like 17-4PH occupy a different performance envelope. Solution-annealed and then aged to Condition H900, H1025, or H1150, 17-4PH achieves yield strengths of 1170–1310 MPa — approaching tool steel territory — while retaining adequate corrosion resistance for aerospace, medical, and high-load engineering applications.

Choosing the right grade at the start avoids expensive reruns. Grade selection is part of the DFM review at TOP PROTO — the engineering team reviews your application requirements and flags grade mismatches before quoting.

## Section 3: Stainless Steel Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Stainless Steel Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties comparison table:**

| Property | 303 | 304 | 316L | 17-4PH (H900) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 620 MPa | 515 MPa | 485 MPa | 1310 MPa |
| Yield Strength | 345 MPa | 205 MPa | 170 MPa | 1170 MPa |
| Elongation | 50% | 40% | 40% | 10% |
| Hardness | 262 HB | 201 HB | 217 HB | 388 HB |
| Density | 8.0 g/cm³ | 8.0 g/cm³ | 8.0 g/cm³ | 7.78 g/cm³ |
| Machinability Rating | ~45% | ~45% | ~40% | ~30% (aged) |

**Machinability notes:**

Stainless steel work-hardens during cutting. That is the defining machining challenge across all austenitic grades: the cutting action compresses and hardens the surface ahead of the tool, and if the tool dwells or rubs rather than cuts, the next pass encounters harder material. Correct cutting parameters — high feed rates, sharp tooling, adequate coolant — prevent work hardening. Dwell is the enemy.

303 machines significantly better than 304 or 316L because sulphur forms manganese sulphide inclusions that act as chip breakers. On machining centres, 303 behaves closer to free-machining carbon steel than to 304 stainless. The tradeoff is that the sulphur reduces corrosion resistance in chloride environments and eliminates weldability — 303 cannot be welded.

316L is the slowest of the three austenitic grades. The molybdenum content increases work-hardening rate and tool wear. Coated carbide tooling, lower spindle speeds relative to 303/304, and consistent coolant application are standard on 316L operations.

17-4PH is most often machined in the annealed condition (Condition A) — softer and more manageable — then aged to the target condition after machining. Machining in H900 or H925 condition is possible but significantly increases tool wear and cycle time. Confirm the required delivery condition at quote stage.

## Section 6: Surface Finishes for Stainless Steel

**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.

**H2:** Surface Finishes for Stainless Steel Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Internal structural parts, prototype evaluation |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Uniform matte texture — cosmetic uniformity across batch |
| Brushed Finish | Negligible | Directional satin texture — panels, enclosures, instrument hardware |
| Electropolishing | Removes thin surface layer (~5 µm) | Medical devices, food equipment — Ra down to 0.1–0.4 µm; improves corrosion resistance |
| Passivation (ASTM A967) | None | Removes free iron and contamination from surface — standard for 316L medical and food parts |
| Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Ra to 0.1 µm — optical and cosmetic requirements |
| Powder Coat | +50–100 µm per side | Colour; secondary corrosion protection — less common on stainless |
| Black Oxide | Negligible | Reduces reflectivity and minor corrosion protection in dry environments |

**Passivation note:** Passivation per ASTM A967 is standard practice for 316L medical and food-contact parts. It removes free iron deposited on the surface during machining, restoring the chromium oxide passive layer. It is not the same as electropolishing — passivation cleans the surface; electropolishing smooths and brightens it.

**Design:** Table. White background.

**H2:** Design Guidelines for Stainless Steel CNC Parts

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min wall thickness | 1.0 mm recommended | Stainless deflects less than aluminium but thin walls still chatter — higher cutting forces than aluminium |
| Internal corner radius | Min 1/3 of cavity depth | Tool diameter governs minimum radius — same rule as aluminium |

| Thread engagement | Min 1.5× diameter | Stainless threads are stronger than aluminium but torque values are higher — galling risk on stainless-to-stainless assemblies |

## Section 8: Applications and Industries

**Design:** Two-column icon+text grid. White background.

**H2:** Where Stainless Steel CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Medical Devices and Surgical Instruments**
316L is the standard for surgical instruments, implant components, and device housings in contact with body fluids. Electropolishing and passivation are standard finishing steps. Heat-number-traceable material certificates with every order. Biocompatible surface finish confirmed per specification.

**Food and Beverage Equipment**
316L for anything in contact with food, cleaning chemicals, or CIP fluids. Electropolished surfaces reduce bacterial adhesion. Passivation standard before delivery. Threads and crevices minimised in the design where hygiene is the priority.

**Aerospace and Defence**
17-4PH for high-stress fasteners, actuator components, and structural parts where stainless corrosion resistance is needed alongside high strength. 303 for connector bodies, standoffs, and secondary hardware where machinability matters. CMM inspection, material traceability, and CoC standard.

**Robotics and Automation**
316L and 304 for end effectors, tool holders, and parts in washdown or chemical environments. 303 for precision-machined shafts, pins, and bushings in dry environments. Stainless used where aluminium would corrode or titanium would over-specify cost.

**Electronics and Communication**
Connector bodies, waveguide components, RF hardware, and precision enclosures. 303 for precision-machined contact surfaces; 316L for harsh-environment installations.

**Industrial and Valve Applications**
316L for valve bodies, manifolds, fittings, and fluid handling hardware exposed to process chemicals. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm on sealing faces and port diameters. Internal surface finish and passivation to specification.

## Section 10: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Stainless Steel CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on stainless steel CNC machined parts?**
Standard stainless steel CNC work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, sealing faces, bearing bores, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 0.8 µm is the standard machined surface finish on stainless; electropolishing achieves Ra 0.1–0.4 µm for medical and food-contact surfaces. Tolerance achievability depends on grade, geometry, and wall section — work-hardening behaviour in austenitic grades is assessed during DFM review and cutting parameters adjusted before production starts.

**Q2: Which stainless steel grade should I use for my CNC part?**
The choice depends on your application. 303 is the machining-optimised grade — fastest to cut, lowest cost, good corrosion resistance, but cannot be welded and is not for chloride-aggressive environments. 304 is general-purpose — good corrosion resistance, weldable, widely available. 316L adds molybdenum for chloride and pitting resistance — this is the medical, marine, and food-contact grade. 17-4PH is the high-strength grade — aged to H900 it reaches 1310 MPa tensile strength with reasonable corrosion resistance, suited to aerospace fasteners, shafts, and structural components. If the grade is unspecified on your drawing, the engineering team will ask about your application environment and loading before recommending.

**Q3: Can stainless steel CNC parts be welded?**
304 and 316L are weldable by MIG and TIG processes — standard for fabricated assemblies combining machined components. 303 cannot be welded: the sulphur content that improves machinability causes hot cracking during welding. 17-4PH can be welded but the weld zone loses precipitation-hardening response — age treatment after welding is required, and metallurgical review is recommended for structural welds. Confirm weldability requirements at quote stage so the correct grade is specified from the start.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for stainless steel parts?**
Standard: as-machined, bead blasted, brushed, polished, black oxide. Medical and food-contact: passivation per ASTM A967 (standard on 316L medical orders), electropolishing to Ra 0.1–0.4 µm. Passivation removes free iron from the machined surface and restores the chromium oxide passive layer — it does not alter dimensions. Electropolishing removes approximately 5 µm per side and simultaneously polishes and passivates. Powder coat and paint are available on stainless but are less common than on aluminium. State surface treatment requirements on your drawing or in the quote notes.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for stainless steel CNC machining?**
No minimum. One stainless steel part is a valid order. Single prototypes, small development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and shipping documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for stainless steel CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing PDF alongside the STEP file when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish designations, passivation requirements, or GD&T callouts appear — the 2D drawing becomes the specification authority for those requirements.

**Q7: How much does stainless steel CNC machining cost?**
Stainless steel CNC machining costs 3–5× more than equivalent aluminium work, driven by longer cycle time (work hardening demands conservative parameters and more frequent tool changes), higher material cost, and finishing processes like passivation or electropolishing. A simple 316L bracket with standard tolerances and passivation costs significantly less than a complex 17-4PH component with ±0.005 mm callouts and electropolishing. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours — not an estimate range. DFM review is included with the quote at no additional charge.

**Q8: How does stainless steel CNC machining compare to stainless steel sheet metal fabrication?**
CNC machining starts from solid bar or plate and produces parts with tight tolerances, internal features (pockets, bores, threads), and complex 3D geometry. Sheet metal fabrication starts from flat sheet and produces thin-wall structures — enclosures, brackets, flanges — through laser cutting and bending. CNC machining is correct when wall thickness, feature depth, thread engagement, or dimensional tolerance exceeds what sheet metal can achieve. Sheet metal is correct when the design is thin-wall and flat surfaces are acceptable. Many stainless assemblies use both: CNC machined bodies with sheet metal covers or flanges from one facility at TOP PROTO.
