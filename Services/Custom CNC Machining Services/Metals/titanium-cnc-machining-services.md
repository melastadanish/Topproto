## Section 2: Why Titanium for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** Titanium — When Strength, Weight, and Corrosion Resistance All Matter

Titanium is specified when no other material satisfies all three requirements simultaneously: high strength, low weight, and complete corrosion resistance. Aluminium gives you weight and corrosion resistance but is softer. Stainless steel gives you strength and corrosion resistance but is heavy. Titanium delivers both — at density of 4.5 g/cm³ versus 8.0 g/cm³ for stainless steel — making it the standard material for aerospace primary structure, medical implants, and high-performance engineering applications where every gram of material and every degree of biocompatibility matters.

The two grades that cover most CNC machined titanium applications are Grade 2 and Grade 5. Grade 2 (commercially pure) is the corrosion resistance and formability grade — biocompatible, weldable, and used in medical, chemical, and marine applications. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is the structural grade — the most widely used titanium alloy, adding aluminium and vanadium to achieve yield strengths above 880 MPa while remaining significantly lighter than steel.

The price of those properties is machinability. Titanium machines poorly compared to aluminium or free-machining stainless. It is chemically reactive at high temperatures — heat generated at the cutting face accelerates tool wear, and without correct tooling and coolant strategy, tool life on titanium is short. Cycle times for equivalent titanium and 6061 aluminium geometry differ by 5–10×. This is the direct driver of cost: machined titanium is expensive because it takes significantly longer per feature.

TOP PROTO's engineering team assesses titanium DFM at quote stage — tool access, coolant paths, fixturing strategy, and thermal management are all part of the pre-production review before any titanium job enters the queue.

## Section 3: Titanium Properties and Machinability

**Design:** Two-column. Light grey background. Properties table left, machinability notes right.

**H2:** Titanium Mechanical Properties and Machining Behaviour

**Properties comparison — Grade 2 vs Grade 5:**

| Property | Grade 2 (CP Ti) | Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) |
|---|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 345 MPa | 950 MPa |
| Yield Strength | 275 MPa | 880 MPa |
| Elongation | 20% | 14% |
| Hardness | ~200 HB | ~330 HB |
| Density | 4.51 g/cm³ | 4.43 g/cm³ |
| Thermal Conductivity | 21 W/m·K | 7 W/m·K |
| Machinability Rating | ~40% (CP Ti) | ~20–25% (Ti-6Al-4V) |

**Machining notes:**

Titanium's combination of low thermal conductivity, high strength at temperature, and chemical reactivity at the cutting face creates the classic titanium machining problem: heat accumulates at the tool tip, the material remains strong even when hot, and the tool wears or fractures quickly without the correct parameters and coolant strategy.

Grade 2 machines better than Grade 5 — lower hardness and higher ductility mean lower cutting forces. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is significantly harder and more demanding. Sharp coated carbide tooling (TiAlN or AlCrN coating), low cutting speeds, high feed rates, and flood coolant are the standard approach. The strategy is to move the tool fast through the material before heat can build — slow, rubbing cuts generate far more heat and wear the tool faster than aggressive, fast feeds.

Titanium also work-hardens during cutting, and the chips are prone to fire hazard when dry machining — this is why coolant is not optional on titanium. The chips must be cleared continuously. Specific fire risk management applies in titanium machining facilities.

## Section 6: Surface Finishes for Titanium

**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.

**H2:** Surface Finishes for Titanium CNC Machined Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Structural evaluation, fixturing hardware |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Uniform matte texture — aerospace and medical components |
| Passivation | None | Removes machining contamination — not always specified on titanium as native oxide layer is self-healing |
| Electropolishing | Removes ~5 µm per side | Medical implant-adjacent parts — improves corrosion resistance and removes surface layer |
| Anodize (Type II — titanium) | Negligible | Colour coding by thickness (not dye) — used in medical devices for part identification |
| Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Ra to 0.1 µm — implant surfaces, optical-quality requirements |

**Titanium surface note:** Titanium forms a native titanium dioxide oxide layer spontaneously in air, which is the source of its excellent corrosion resistance and biocompatibility. This passive layer is self-healing — unlike stainless steel, titanium typically does not require passivation treatment after machining. Anodizing titanium produces colour by controlling oxide layer thickness (not by dye), making it useful for colour-coded identification of implants and surgical instruments.

## Section 7: Design Guidelines for Titanium CNC Machining

**Design:** Table. White background.

**H2:** Design Guidelines for Titanium CNC Parts

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min wall thickness | 1.0 mm (confirm per geometry) | Titanium is stiffer than aluminium — but thin sections still deflect under high cutting forces |
| Internal corner radius | Min 1/3 of cavity depth | Tool diameter constraint — same as all metals, but larger radii preferred to reduce tool stress on titanium |
| Hole depth | Max 5× diameter for standard drill | Deep holes in titanium require peck drilling and careful coolant delivery — flag depths >5D |
| Thread method | Thread milling preferred | Titanium galls easily against titanium fasteners — thread milling is more controllable than tapping; specify thread class |
| Avoid sharp re-entrant angles | Fillets where possible | Stress risers in titanium are more critical — sharp corners in high-stress parts are a structural concern |
| Grade 5 ELI for implants | State on drawing | Standard Grade 5 is not implant-grade — ELI (Extra Low Interstitial) is required for implant contact |
| Coolant access | Review with engineering | Internal channels and blind features that restrict coolant flow require specific machining strategy |

## Section 10: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** Titanium CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on titanium CNC machined parts?**
Standard titanium CNC work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.01 mm on linear dimensions for most features. Precision features, bearing bores, and positional callouts hold to ±0.005 mm, confirmed by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing revision. Ra 1.6 µm is the standard machined surface finish on titanium; polishing achieves Ra 0.1 µm for implant surfaces. Tolerance achievability on titanium depends on geometry, wall section, and coolant access — these are assessed during DFM review before cutting starts.

**Q2: What is the difference between titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5?**
Grade 2 is commercially pure titanium — minimal alloying additions, maximum corrosion resistance, biocompatible, weldable, and used in medical implant-contact applications, chemical processing, and marine environments. Yield strength is 275 MPa — adequate for many applications but not for high-stress structural parts. Grade 5 is Ti-6Al-4V — an aluminium and vanadium alloyed titanium with yield strength of 880 MPa and tensile strength of 950 MPa. Grade 5 is the aerospace and high-performance structural grade. It machines more slowly than Grade 2 (lower machinability rating) and requires greater care with cutting parameters and coolant. If the part is implant-contact, Grade 2 or Grade 5 ELI is the specification; if it's structural aerospace or motorsport, Grade 5 is correct.

**Q3: Why does titanium CNC machining cost more than aluminium or stainless steel?**
Titanium costs more to machine because it takes significantly longer per feature. Titanium's low thermal conductivity (7 W/m·K for Grade 5 versus 167 W/m·K for aluminium) means heat accumulates at the cutting face rather than conducting into the workpiece. Combined with titanium's high chemical reactivity at temperature, this accelerates tool wear rapidly. The response is lower cutting speeds, higher feed rates, and flood coolant — all of which increase cycle time relative to aluminium. Tool consumption per unit is higher. Material cost is also higher than aluminium or stainless. Grade 5 titanium parts typically cost 5–10× more than equivalent aluminium parts. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q4: Is titanium biocompatible for medical devices?**
Grade 2 and Grade 5 titanium are both recognised as biocompatible metallic materials per ISO 10993-1. Grade 2 is the standard specification for implant-contact hardware where maximum biocompatibility is the priority. Grade 5 ELI (Extra Low Interstitial) is used for structural implant components where strength is also required — ELI limits oxygen and iron content to levels that improve fracture toughness in biological environments. Standard Grade 5 is not the correct specification for implant-contact use. Biocompatibility of a specific device depends on the combination of material, surface finish, and device classification — TOP PROTO provides material certificates and surface finish documentation to support your device validation.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for titanium CNC machining?**
No minimum. One titanium part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and shipping documentation. Per-unit cost on single pieces is higher because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, and titanium-specific tooling costs amortise less across fewer parts, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for titanium CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format for all 3D geometry. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, implant-grade designations, or GD&T callouts appear — the 2D drawing becomes the specification authority.

**Q7: How long does titanium CNC machining take?**
Standard titanium CNC parts ship in 4–7 business days from the Dongguan facility. Grade 5 parts with complex geometry, multiple setups, or fine thread features may require 5–7 business days. Lead time is confirmed per geometry and material availability at quoting — not an estimate. If your timeline is tight, state it when uploading your file.

**Q8: How does titanium compare to stainless steel for CNC machined parts?**
Titanium offers a significantly better strength-to-weight ratio than stainless steel: Grade 5 achieves 950 MPa tensile at 4.43 g/cm³, versus 485–620 MPa tensile for austenitic stainless at 8.0 g/cm³. For aerospace, medical, and performance applications where weight matters alongside strength, titanium is the correct material. Stainless steel machines faster and costs significantly less — for parts where weight is not a constraint and corrosion resistance is the main driver, stainless steel is typically the better value. 316L stainless is also the standard medical material for non-implant applications; titanium is reserved for implant contact and high-structural-load applications.
