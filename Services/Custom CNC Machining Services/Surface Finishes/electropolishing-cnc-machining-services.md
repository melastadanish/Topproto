## Section 2: What Is Electropolishing

**H2:** Electropolishing — Electrochemical Material Removal for Surface Improvement

Electropolishing is the reverse of electroplating: instead of depositing metal onto the surface, electropolishing removes metal from the surface by anodic dissolution in an electrolyte bath (typically phosphoric/sulphuric acid for stainless steel). When current flows, metal ions are removed preferentially from surface peaks (high points), where current density is highest, while valleys are protected by a diffusion layer. The result is a progressive levelling of surface micro-roughness — peaks are dissolved faster than valleys, smoothing the surface.

The key outcomes of electropolishing are: surface smoothing (Ra improvement), removal of surface contamination and free iron, and creation of a chrome-enriched passive layer. As the surface is dissolved, carbon, sulphur, and iron-rich inclusions are preferentially removed relative to chromium — the remaining surface is enriched in chromium oxide, which provides superior corrosion resistance compared to the as-machined or passivated surface. This is particularly significant for 316L and 304 stainless steel in chloride-containing environments: electropolished 316L has measurably better pitting resistance than passivated 316L at equivalent thickness.

Dimensional change from electropolishing must be designed for. The process removes 5–25 µm from all surfaces — including bores, external diameters, threads, and flat faces. For a precision bore electropolished to 15 µm total removal, the bore diameter increases by approximately 15 µm (7.5 µm on each bore wall). For precision components, specify the post-electropolish dimension as the final dimension on the drawing — TOP PROTO will machine to pre-electropolish dimensions accounting for material removal. Threads in stainless steel parts should be electropolished before threading or masked — electropolishing removes thread profile and reduces thread class.

Ra improvement from electropolishing depends on the starting surface condition. A typical as-machined 316L surface at Ra 1.6 µm can be electropolished to Ra 0.4–0.8 µm in a standard cycle. Starting from a ground or fine-machined surface at Ra 0.4–0.8 µm, electropolishing can achieve Ra 0.1–0.2 µm. Electropolishing improves the ratio of peaks to valleys rather than uniformly removing material — starting from a polished surface allows the best electropolished finish.

## Section 5: Applications

**H2:** Where Electropolished CNC Parts Are Used

**Medical Devices** — Surgical instrument components, catheter fittings, implant-adjacent parts, and fluid path components requiring the smoothest possible stainless steel surface. Electropolished 316L reduces surface micro-roughness that harbours bacteria and biofilm. The chrome-enriched surface resists corrosion from body fluids and sterilisation media. Electropolishing is the standard surface treatment for medical-grade stainless steel fluid contact components.

**Pharmaceutical and Bioprocess Equipment** — Valves, manifolds, and fluid path fittings in pharmaceutical manufacturing equipment. Smooth surface (Ra <0.4 µm) reduces bacterial adhesion and facilitates CIP (clean-in-place) cleaning validation. Electropolished 316L is the standard for pharmaceutical fluid contact stainless steel per industry guidance.

**Food Processing Equipment** — Food contact valves, fittings, and equipment where smooth, cleanable stainless steel surfaces are required. Similar cleanability requirements to pharmaceutical applications — electropolishing reduces surface microstructure that traps food particles and bacteria.

**Semiconductor Equipment** — Ultra-clean fluid path components in semiconductor process tools. Electropolished stainless steel surfaces have lower particle generation and outgassing compared to as-machined surfaces — critical in ultra-clean process environments.

**Aerospace and Defence** — Corrosion-critical stainless steel components where enhanced passive layer integrity is specified. Electropolishing provides a chrome-enriched surface with superior salt spray corrosion resistance versus passivated stainless.

## Section 7: FAQ

**H2:** Electropolishing — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much material does electropolishing remove from CNC parts?**
Standard electropolishing removes 5–25 µm from all surfaces — the exact amount depends on cycle time, current density, temperature, and the alloy being processed. A standard medical-grade cycle typically removes 10–15 µm per surface. For a precision bore, this means the bore diameter increases by 20–30 µm total (10–15 µm on each bore wall). For precision stainless steel components, state the post-electropolish dimension as the final callout on the drawing — the engineering team will machine to the pre-EP dimension accounting for predicted material removal. Confirm the target removal at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between electropolishing and passivation for stainless steel?**
Passivation (ASTM A967) is a chemical cleaning process — it removes free iron contamination and accelerates passive layer formation without removing substrate material. Zero dimensional change. Electropolishing (ASTM B912) is an electrochemical material removal process — it removes 5–25 µm of substrate from all surfaces, smoothing the surface and creating a chrome-enriched passive layer with superior corrosion resistance. Electropolishing causes dimensional change. Passivation is the standard post-machining treatment when dimensional change is unacceptable. Electropolishing is specified when surface smoothing, improved cleanability (Ra <0.4 µm), or superior corrosion resistance are required, with dimensional change designed for.

**Q3: What Ra surface finish is achievable with electropolishing?**
Ra improvement from electropolishing depends on the starting surface condition. From an as-machined surface at Ra 1.6 µm, electropolishing achieves Ra 0.4–0.8 µm in a standard cycle. From a finely machined or pre-polished surface at Ra 0.4–0.8 µm, electropolishing achieves Ra 0.1–0.2 µm. Electropolishing levels surface peaks rather than uniformly removing material — starting from a finer surface gives the best electropolished result. If Ra 0.2 µm or better is the requirement, machine the part to Ra 0.4 µm before electropolishing.

**Q4: Is electropolishing compatible with threaded stainless steel parts?**
Electropolishing removes material from all surfaces, including thread flanks — this changes thread pitch diameter and can shift the thread class. For threaded stainless steel parts that will be electropolished, threads should be electropolished before threading (machine to EP allowance, EP the blank, then thread) or threads should be masked during EP. Masking threads adds setup cost but preserves the thread class. Confirm thread treatment at quote stage — the engineering team will advise based on thread tolerance and EP depth.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for electropolished CNC parts?**
No minimum. Electropolishing is applied to any order that specifies it, including single prototypes. There is no batch minimum — one part is a valid order.

**Q6: What information is needed to quote electropolished CNC stainless parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. 2D drawing specifying: stainless grade, target Ra or material removal (state which dimension is pre- or post-EP), masking requirements for threads or precision features, and any applicable standard (ASTM B912). Note whether parts are for medical, pharmaceutical, or food contact applications — these require specific documentation.

**Q7: How much does electropolishing add to the cost of CNC stainless steel parts?**
Electropolishing adds a surface finishing charge above passivation cost — it is more expensive than passivation due to longer process time, controlled electrolyte bath, and dimensional measurement before and after. Parts requiring thread masking or detailed fixturing add setup cost. For medical and pharmaceutical applications where Ra <0.4 µm is required, electropolishing is the standard and its cost is part of the component specification. Upload your STEP file and drawing for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does electropolishing compare to mechanical polishing for stainless steel surfaces?**
Electropolishing and mechanical polishing (abrasive polishing to Ra 0.4 µm or better) are both used to smooth stainless steel surfaces, but they work differently. Mechanical polishing removes material with abrasives — it can smear and embed abrasive particles in the surface (particularly in softer stainless grades), and it produces a directional grain. Electropolishing removes material electrochemically — it is non-directional, removes surface contamination and free iron simultaneously, and produces a chrome-enriched surface rather than a mechanically deformed one. For medical and pharmaceutical applications, electropolishing is preferred over mechanical polishing because it produces a cleaner, chrome-enriched surface without the risk of embedded abrasive particles. For applications requiring visible grain direction (decorative brushed finish), mechanical polishing is used instead.
