## Section 2: What Is Passivation

**H2:** Passivation — Restoring the Chromium Oxide Passive Layer

Stainless steel's corrosion resistance comes from a thin, self-healing chromium oxide (Cr₂O₃) passive layer on the surface — approximately 1–5 nm thick. This layer forms spontaneously when chromium in the alloy is exposed to oxygen, and it re-forms if damaged. The problem with machined stainless steel is that the machining process introduces free iron contamination: cutting tool fragments, iron from the machine fixtures, and metallic debris from the machining process deposit on the stainless steel surface. These free iron particles corrode rapidly in humid or wet environments, causing visible rust spots on what should be a corrosion-resistant surface.

Passivation removes this free iron contamination and accelerates formation of the chromium oxide passive layer. The process immerses the part in an acid bath (nitric acid or citric acid) that dissolves the free iron without attacking the stainless steel substrate. After passivation, the cleaned surface is exposed to air, and the chromium oxide passive layer reforms uniformly across the entire surface. The result is a stainless steel surface with its full designed corrosion resistance — free of contamination from the machining process.

ASTM A967 is the standard that governs passivation of stainless steel. Nitric acid processes (ASTM A967 Types 1–5) have been the traditional method — nitric acid is effective at dissolving free iron and accelerating passive layer formation. Citric acid passivation (ASTM A967 Type 8) is an increasingly preferred alternative: citric acid is less hazardous than nitric acid, environmentally safer, and produces an equivalent or better passive layer on most stainless steel grades. Both processes produce a passivated surface verified by testing (water wetting, copper sulphate test, or humidity chamber per ASTM A967 test methods).

Passivation produces no dimensional change — it is a chemical cleaning process, not a deposition or material-removal process. The passive layer itself is nanometres thick — invisible to measurement and with no effect on part dimensions or tolerances. For precision stainless steel parts where dimensional change from a surface treatment is not acceptable, passivation is the correct specification.

## Section 7: FAQ

**H2:** Passivation — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Does passivation change the dimensions of stainless steel CNC parts?**
No. Passivation is a chemical cleaning process that removes free iron contamination and restores the chromium oxide passive layer. The passive layer itself is nanometres thick — below any measurement instrument's resolution and with no effect on part tolerances, bore diameters, or surface finish. For precision stainless steel parts where dimensional change from a surface treatment is unacceptable, passivation is the correct post-machining treatment.

**Q2: What is the difference between nitric acid and citric acid passivation?**
Both nitric acid (ASTM A967 Types 1–5) and citric acid (ASTM A967 Type 8) passivation processes remove free iron contamination and restore the chromium oxide passive layer on stainless steel. Nitric acid is the traditional process — fast and effective but produces hazardous nitric oxide fumes and generates regulated waste requiring specialist disposal. Citric acid is an increasingly preferred alternative — less hazardous, environmentally safer waste, and produces equivalent or better passive layer on most stainless grades in most applications. The finished part from either process has equivalent corrosion resistance when tested per ASTM A967 methods. TOP PROTO uses citric acid passivation as the standard process and nitric acid where specified by customer drawing or standard.

**Q3: What is the difference between passivation and electropolishing for stainless steel?**
Passivation is a chemical cleaning process — it removes free iron contamination and accelerates passive layer formation without removing stainless steel substrate. Electropolishing is an electrochemical material removal process — it removes a controlled layer of substrate (typically 5–25 µm) from all surfaces, smoothing the surface and simultaneously creating a chrome-enriched surface with superior corrosion resistance. Passivation produces zero dimensional change; electropolishing changes dimensions. Passivation is the standard post-machining treatment for stainless steel parts that require their machined dimensions preserved. Electropolishing is specified when surface smoothing (Ra improvement), superior corrosion resistance, or reduced surface micro-roughness for cleaning purposes are required, with dimensional change accounted for in the design.

**Q4: Is passivation required for all stainless steel CNC machined parts?**
Passivation is not always required — stainless steel is inherently corrosion-resistant and will re-form its passive layer naturally on exposure to air over time. However, for applications in corrosive environments (marine, food contact, medical, pharmaceutical, and chemical process), passivation after machining is strongly recommended. Without passivation, free iron contamination from machining can cause visible rust spots on the surface — locally compromising the corrosion resistance and potentially causing product contamination. For any stainless steel part specified for food contact, medical use, or corrosive environment, passivation is the standard post-machining specification.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for passivated CNC parts?**
No minimum. Passivation is applied to any order that specifies it, including single prototypes. There is no batch minimum for surface finishing — one part is a valid order.

**Q6: What information is needed to quote passivated CNC stainless steel parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. 2D drawing specifying: stainless steel grade, passivation process (ASTM A967, Type 2 nitric or Type 8 citric, or specify if customer-preference), and any verification testing requirements (water wetting, copper sulphate, or humidity chamber). State if passivation certificate is required for regulatory documentation.

**Q7: How much does passivation add to the cost of CNC stainless steel parts?**
Passivation adds a modest surface finishing charge to the base machining cost. The process cost is lower than electropolishing, plating, or coating. Passivation of standard stainless steel parts (303, 304, 316L) is straightforward and economical. Parts requiring passivation certification documentation (ASTM A967 compliance certificate) add a documentation fee. Upload your STEP file and drawing for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: What stainless steel grades benefit most from passivation?**
All machined stainless steel grades benefit from passivation — free iron contamination is introduced by machining regardless of grade. 316L benefits most in corrosive environments: the molybdenum in 316L gives pitting resistance in chloride environments, but only when the passive layer is intact. Passivation after machining ensures the full 316L corrosion advantage is realised in service. 303 stainless has sulphur inclusions that can cause localised corrosion even after passivation — passivation is still standard practice but 303 should not be specified for applications requiring the highest corrosion resistance (use 316L instead). 17-4PH benefits from passivation after heat treatment to restore the passive layer disturbed by the HT process.
