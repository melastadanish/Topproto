## Section 2: What Is Nickel Plating

**H2:** Nickel Plating — Hardness, Corrosion Resistance, and Uniform Coverage

Nickel plating deposits a nickel layer onto the substrate surface — either by electrochemical deposition (electrolytic nickel) or by autocatalytic chemical reduction (electroless nickel). Both processes produce a nickel coating that increases surface hardness, improves corrosion resistance, provides a barrier between the substrate and corrosive environment, and creates a hard, wear-resistant surface. The choice between electroless and electrolytic determines the coating uniformity, hardness achievable, and application suitability.

Electroless nickel (EN) is the more commonly specified process for precision CNC machined parts. The key advantage is uniform coating thickness: because EN deposition is driven by a chemical reaction rather than current density, the coating deposits uniformly across all surfaces regardless of geometry — inside bores, on recesses, sharp internal corners, and complex features receive the same coating thickness as external faces. For parts with internal features, threads, or complex geometry where electrolytic plating would produce uneven buildup at corners and thin coverage in recesses, electroless nickel is the correct specification.

Electroless nickel also contains phosphorus (typically 4–12% P depending on the bath formulation), which affects hardness and corrosion resistance. Medium-phosphorus EN (6–9% P) provides good general corrosion resistance and achieves 45–55 HRC as-plated. High-phosphorus EN (10–12% P) provides better corrosion resistance (particularly in acidic environments) and is amorphous — it resists cracking under thermal cycling. Low-phosphorus EN (2–4% P) achieves the highest hardness as-plated (60–65 HRC) and can be heat-treated to 70 HRC, but has lower corrosion resistance than high-phosphorus grades. After heat treatment at 400°C, electroless nickel approaches tool steel hardness — this is the process for hardening electroless nickel on tool steel and die parts.

Electrolytic nickel deposits are thicker, brighter, and ductile — used for decorative applications, electrical contact surfaces, and as underlayers before chrome plating. Electrolytic nickel does not plate uniformly on complex geometry — buildup is higher at edges and lower in recesses. It is the specification for high-brightness decorative nickel, connector plating, and applications where appearance rather than uniform coverage is the primary requirement.

## Section 3: Electroless vs Electrolytic Nickel

**H2:** Electroless Nickel vs Electrolytic Nickel

| | Electroless Nickel (EN) | Electrolytic Nickel |
|---|---|---|
| Deposition mechanism | Autocatalytic chemical reduction | Electrochemical (current-driven) |
| Coverage uniformity | Uniform on all surfaces including bores | Uneven — thick at edges, thin in recesses |
| Phosphorus content | 2–12% P | None (pure Ni) |
| Hardness (as-plated) | 45–65 HRC depending on P content | 140–200 HV (~15–20 HRC) |
| Hardness (heat-treated) | Up to 70 HRC at 400°C | Not significantly increased by HT |
| Corrosion resistance | Excellent (high-P grades best) | Good |
| Typical thickness | 5–50 µm | 5–500 µm |
| Best for | Precision parts, complex geometry, wear and corrosion | Decorative, electrical contacts, pre-chrome undercoat |

## Section 6: Applications

**H2:** Where Nickel Plated CNC Parts Are Used

**Tooling and Dies** — Tool steel dies, moulds, and jig components plated with electroless nickel for wear resistance and release properties. Heat-treated EN to 70 HRC on die surfaces approaches hard chrome alternative. Release properties of nickel surface reduce galling and material sticking in injection moulding and stamping applications.

**Precision Mechanical Components** — Gears, shafts, bushings, and structural components requiring a hard, wear-resistant surface on complex geometry. Electroless nickel's uniform coverage ensures consistent protection on all surfaces including bores and recesses.

**Aerospace and Defence** — Steel and aluminium components requiring corrosion protection with a hard, wear-resistant surface. EN replaces cadmium plating in many aerospace applications where cadmium's toxicity is a concern. Electroless nickel on aluminium provides a hard surface layer that allows the light weight of aluminium with the surface performance of a harder material.

**Electronics and Connectors** — Electrolytic nickel as undercoat before gold plating on connectors, switch contacts, and electronic components. Nickel provides a barrier layer that prevents copper migration into gold and provides a hard substrate for the gold topcoat.

**Oil and Gas** — Downhole tool components and valve parts in corrosive process environments. High-phosphorus electroless nickel resists H₂S-containing environments where lower-phosphorus or electrolytic grades would corrode.

**Medical Device Components** — Aluminium and steel medical device parts requiring a hard, cleanable surface. Electroless nickel on aluminium housings provides surgical instrument hardness with aluminium weight.

## Section 8: FAQ

**H2:** Nickel Plating — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much does nickel plating change the dimensions of CNC machined parts?**
Electroless nickel plating at 12.5 µm deposits uniformly — each external surface grows 12.5 µm, each bore decreases 25 µm in diameter (12.5 µm on each bore wall). For precision parts, state the post-plate dimension as the final callout on the drawing. TOP PROTO will machine to the pre-plate dimension accounting for the expected coating thickness. For threads, either plate before threading (machine to allow for coating, plate blank, then thread) or mask threads during plating — plating over threads changes thread class. Confirm the dimensional strategy at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between electroless and electrolytic nickel plating?**
Electroless nickel (EN) uses a chemical autocatalytic reaction to deposit nickel — the coating thickness is uniform across all surfaces regardless of geometry. EN contains phosphorus (2–12%), which provides hardness (up to 70 HRC after heat treatment) and corrosion resistance. Electrolytic nickel uses electrical current to deposit nickel — thickness varies with current density and geometry, producing buildup at edges and thin coverage in recesses. Electrolytic nickel is softer, brighter, and ductile — used for decorative finishes, electrical contacts, and as undercoat before chrome. For precision CNC machined parts with complex geometry, bores, and tight dimensional requirements, electroless nickel is the correct specification. Electrolytic nickel is specified for decorative and electrical applications.

**Q3: What hardness is achievable with electroless nickel plating?**
As-plated electroless nickel hardness depends on phosphorus content: low-phosphorus EN (2–4% P) achieves 60–65 HRC as-plated; medium-phosphorus (6–9% P) achieves 45–55 HRC; high-phosphorus (10–12% P) achieves 38–45 HRC but has the best corrosion resistance. Heat treatment at 400°C for 1 hour increases EN hardness significantly: low-phosphorus EN reaches up to 70 HRC after HT — approaching hard chrome and harder than most tool steels. This makes heat-treated EN a viable alternative to hard chrome on steel and aluminium tooling components. Confirm the target hardness and phosphorus content at quote stage.

**Q4: Is electroless nickel plating suitable for aluminium CNC parts?**
Yes — electroless nickel on aluminium is a well-established process used in aerospace, electronics, and general engineering. Adhesion requires a zincate pre-treatment: the aluminium is immersed in a zinc solution that deposits a thin zinc layer, providing an adherent base for the EN deposit. Without zincate, EN adhesion to aluminium is unreliable. After zincate and EN plating, the aluminium surface has the hardness and wear resistance of the nickel coating — this is the basis for using EN-plated aluminium in applications that would otherwise require steel or titanium for surface hardness. Confirm aluminium grade and pre-treatment at quote stage — not all aluminium alloys respond equally to zincate.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for nickel plated CNC parts?**
No minimum. Nickel plating is applied to any order that specifies it, including single prototypes. There is no batch minimum — one part is a valid order.

**Q6: What information is needed to quote nickel plated CNC parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. 2D drawing specifying: substrate material and grade, plating type (EN or electrolytic nickel), phosphorus content for EN (or specify hardness target), coating thickness, masking requirements for threads and precision features, post-plate dimensional callouts (not pre-plate), and any applicable standards (ASTM B733 for EN, ASTM B689 for electrolytic).

**Q7: How much does nickel plating add to the cost of CNC parts?**
Nickel plating adds a surface finishing charge above the base machining cost. Electroless nickel costs more than black oxide or passivation due to the chemical bath, pre-treatment steps, and XRF thickness measurement. Parts requiring masking of threads or precision features add setup cost. Heat treatment of EN after plating adds an additional operation. For applications requiring wear resistance, corrosion protection, and dimensional uniformity on complex parts, electroless nickel is well-established and its cost is part of the component specification. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does electroless nickel compare to hard chrome for CNC parts?**
Electroless nickel and hard chrome are both hard, wear-resistant coatings for steel and other substrates. Hard chrome achieves higher hardness as-applied (70–72 HRC) and has excellent wear resistance — but chrome plating is an electrolytic process that deposits unevenly on complex geometry, leaving thin areas on bores and recesses. Hexavalent chromium (Cr⁶⁺) used in hard chrome plating is regulated under REACH and RoHS — its use is restricted and alternatives are required in many applications. Electroless nickel (heat-treated to 70 HRC) is the most common hard chrome alternative — it provides equivalent or similar hardness, uniform coverage on all surfaces, and uses trivalent chromium-free chemistry. For new designs requiring a hard, wear-resistant coating, electroless nickel is the preferred specification over hard chrome in most jurisdictions.
