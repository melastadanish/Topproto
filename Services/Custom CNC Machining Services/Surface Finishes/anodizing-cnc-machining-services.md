## Section 2: What Is Anodizing

**H2:** Anodizing — Controlled Oxide Growth, Not a Coating

Anodizing is an electrochemical process that converts the surface of aluminium into aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) — a hard, corrosion-resistant ceramic layer. Unlike plating, which deposits a foreign material onto the surface, anodizing grows the oxide layer from the aluminium itself: approximately 50% of the layer thickness grows outward, and 50% grows into the substrate. The result is an oxide layer that is metallurgically bonded to the aluminium — it cannot peel, chip, or delaminate.

Type II anodizing (sulphuric acid anodize, standard anodize) produces a layer 5–25 µm thick. The oxide layer is porous after anodizing and is sealed using hot deionised water or nickel acetate sealing — the sealing step closes the pores, increases corrosion resistance, and fixes any dye colour. Type II anodize is the standard specification for corrosion protection, cosmetic colour, and general-purpose aluminium protection. It is compatible with most aluminium alloys and is the finish specified for the majority of commercial, aerospace interior, and consumer electronics aluminium parts.

Type III anodizing (hard anodize, hardcoat) uses a lower temperature sulphuric acid bath and higher current density to produce a denser, thicker oxide layer — 25–100 µm, with typical specification at 25–50 µm. Hard anodize is significantly harder than standard Type II — the oxide layer achieves 400–600 HV (approximately 60–70 HRC equivalent), compared to 200–300 HV for Type II. Type III is the specification for wear-resistant surfaces, sliding interfaces, hydraulic cylinder bores, and applications where abrasion resistance is the primary requirement.

Dimensional change with anodizing must be understood for precision parts. The oxide layer grows approximately 50% outward and 50% into the substrate — a 25 µm hard anodize layer adds approximately 12.5 µm to each external surface and removes approximately 12.5 µm from the substrate. For a precision bore, hard anodize tightens the bore by approximately 25 µm total (12.5 µm on each bore wall). For precision components, specify the post-anodize dimension on the drawing — the engineering team will machine to the pre-anodize dimension accounting for the expected growth.

## Section 6: Applications

**H2:** Where Anodized CNC Aluminium Parts Are Used

**Aerospace Interiors** — Seat hardware, brackets, panel fittings, and structural clips in aircraft interiors. Type II anodize for corrosion protection and colour identification. Aluminium's weight advantage over steel makes anodized aluminium standard in aerospace interior applications.

**Consumer Electronics** — Enclosures, bezels, and structural frames in smartphones, laptops, and portable devices. Type II anodize with custom colour for cosmetic finish and scratch resistance.

**Medical Device Housings** — Aluminium housings and panels for non-implant medical equipment. Type II anodize for cleanability and corrosion resistance in hospital environments.

**Hydraulic and Pneumatic Components** — Cylinder bores, valve bodies, and manifold channels in hydraulic and pneumatic systems. Type III hard anodize for wear resistance against seals and pistons.

**Robotics and Automation** — Structural aluminium parts, guide rails, and end-effector components. Anodize for corrosion protection and surface hardness improvement on sliding interfaces.

**Tooling and Fixturing** — Jig plates, fixture bodies, and tooling components. Hard anodize to reduce galling and improve surface life of aluminium tooling in repeated-use applications.

## Section 8: FAQ

**H2:** Anodizing — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What is the dimensional change from anodizing on precision CNC parts?**
Anodizing grows the oxide layer approximately 50% outward and 50% into the substrate. For Type II standard anodize at 15 µm total thickness, each external surface grows approximately 7.5 µm outward. For a precision bore anodized to Type II at 15 µm, the bore diameter decreases by approximately 15 µm (7.5 µm on each bore wall). For Type III hard anodize at 50 µm total, each surface grows 25 µm outward and a bore decreases 50 µm total. For precision parts, state the post-anodize dimension as the final dimension on the drawing — TOP PROTO will machine to pre-anodize dimensions accounting for predicted growth. Confirm the exact layer thickness specification at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between Type II and Type III (hard anodize)?**
Type II standard anodize produces a 5–25 µm oxide layer with 200–300 HV hardness — suitable for corrosion protection, colour, and general surface improvement. Type III hard anodize produces a 25–100 µm layer (typically 25–50 µm specified) at 400–600 HV — the specification for wear-resistant surfaces, sliding interfaces, and hydraulic bore protection. Hard anodize is darker (grey-black as grown) and limits colour options. Hard anodize costs more due to longer process time and lower-temperature bath requirements. For most general corrosion-protection and cosmetic applications, Type II is correct. For wear surfaces and sliding components, specify Type III.

**Q3: Can anodized aluminium parts be re-anodized?**
Yes — anodize can be stripped (using caustic soda or chromic acid) and re-anodized. Stripping removes the oxide layer and a very small amount of substrate. Re-anodizing is used to correct colour discrepancies, repair damaged anodize, or re-process rejected parts. Each strip-and-re-anodize cycle removes a small amount of substrate material — typically 5–10 µm — which must be accounted for in the dimensional budget. Confirm re-anodize requirements at quote stage.

**Q4: What aluminium alloys anodize best?**
6061 and 6063 aluminium (6000-series, Mg-Si alloys) anodize most consistently — they produce uniform, clear layers with good colour response. 7075 (7000-series, Zn-Mg-Cu alloy) anodizes well but the high zinc content causes colour variability — dark anodize colours (black) are more consistent on 7075 than light colours. 2024 (2000-series, Cu alloy) anodizes with some difficulty due to high copper content — colour is less consistent and Type III is more challenging. Cast aluminium alloys generally anodize with less uniform appearance than wrought alloys. Confirm alloy at quote stage for colour-critical applications.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for anodized CNC parts?**
No minimum. Anodizing is applied to every aluminium order that specifies it, including single prototypes. There is no batch minimum for anodizing — one part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats and specifications are needed to quote anodized CNC parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. 2D drawing with: anodize type (Type II or Type III), layer thickness specification, colour (if specified), sealing type, masking requirements for threads or precision features, and any applicable standard (MIL-A-8625 Type II or III). Note which surfaces are critical dimensions that need to account for anodize growth.

**Q7: How much does anodizing add to the cost of CNC aluminium parts?**
Anodizing adds a surface finishing charge to the base machining cost. Type II anodize adds a modest cost to the part. Type III hard anodize adds more due to longer processing and controlled bath conditions. Colour anodizing (dye bath) adds cost over clear anodize. Parts requiring precise masking of threads, bores, or mating features add setup cost for the masking operation. Upload your STEP file and drawing for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: What is the corrosion resistance of anodized aluminium?**
Type II sealed anodize (hot DI water or nickel acetate sealed) passes 336+ hours in salt spray testing per ASTM B117 on 6061 and 6063 aluminium. Unsealed anodize has significantly lower corrosion resistance — sealing is standard for all corrosion-protection anodize specifications. Type III hard anodize has higher inherent corrosion resistance due to thicker, denser oxide. Anodized aluminium is not suitable for continuous immersion in strong acids, bases, or salt solutions — the oxide layer provides atmospheric and splash corrosion protection, not chemical immersion resistance.
