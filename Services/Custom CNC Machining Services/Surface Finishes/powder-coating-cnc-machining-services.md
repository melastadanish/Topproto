## Section 2: What Is Powder Coating

**H2:** Powder Coating — Durable Thermosetting Polymer Finish

Powder coating applies a dry thermosetting polymer powder to the part surface electrostatically and then cures it in an oven at 160–200°C. The curing process causes the powder to melt, flow, and cross-link into a continuous, hard, durable film. The result is a coating significantly thicker (60–120 µm) and more durable than conventional liquid paint (typically 20–40 µm). Powder coating is not applied from a solvent carrier — it is a solid powder, which means no volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are emitted during application.

The mechanical properties of powder coating are its key advantage over liquid paint. The cross-linked polymer film is harder, more impact-resistant, and more abrasion-resistant than liquid paint coatings of equivalent thickness. Powder coating passes direct impact tests (ASTM D2794) that liquid paint fails, and shows superior resistance to chipping, scratching, and peeling in service. For painted aluminium and steel parts subject to handling, outdoor exposure, or mechanical contact, powder coating outperforms liquid paint in durability and service life.

Dimensional change from powder coating must be designed for. The 60–120 µm coating adds to all coated surfaces — 60–120 µm on each face, meaning a bore decreases by 120–240 µm in diameter (60–120 µm on each bore wall). For precision parts, mask bores, threads, and precision features before powder coating. Threads coated with powder coating will not accept fasteners — masking threads is standard practice for all threaded CNC parts that are powder coated. The engineering team reviews masking requirements at DFM stage.

Powder coating is applied to conductive substrates only — aluminium, steel, and other metals. It cannot be applied directly to most plastics without specialist pre-treatment. It also requires oven curing at 160–200°C — this temperature excludes parts with heat-sensitive inserts, rubber seals installed before coating, or sensitive electronic components. These items must be installed after powder coating.

## Section 7: FAQ

**H2:** Powder Coating — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much does powder coating affect the dimensions of CNC machined parts?**
Powder coating adds 60–120 µm to each coated surface. For a bore that is powder coated, the bore diameter decreases by 120–240 µm (60–120 µm on each bore wall). For external features, each face grows 60–120 µm. For precision CNC parts, mask threads and precision bores before powder coating to protect critical dimensions. State post-coat dimensions on the drawing for any unmasked precision feature — the engineering team will machine pre-coat to allow for the expected coating thickness. Confirm coating thickness specification at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between powder coating and anodizing for aluminium CNC parts?**
Anodizing converts the aluminium surface to aluminium oxide — the layer is integral to the aluminium, cannot peel, and changes dimensions by only 5–25 µm. Powder coating deposits a separate polymer layer (60–120 µm) that can be mechanically damaged or chipped. Anodizing is the preferred finish for precision aluminium parts where dimensional change must be controlled, where transparency of the finish is required, and where the anodized layer's integral nature is important. Powder coating is preferred when: a wide range of opaque colours are required (anodize colours are more limited), thicker film build for impact resistance is required, or UV stability without special UV-grade anodize is needed. Choose anodizing for precision, transparent, and tight-tolerance applications. Choose powder coating for opaque colour, impact resistance, and outdoor UV-exposed applications.

**Q3: Can powder coated parts have threads and precision bores?**
Yes — but threads and precision bores must be masked before powder coating. Powder coating over threads will block them — fasteners will not engage, and attempting to run a tap through powder-coated threads strips the coating and produces an unreliable thread. Standard practice is to plug all threaded holes with masking plugs or spiral-wound masking paper before powder coating. Precision bores are similarly masked with rubber plugs. State all features requiring masking on the 2D drawing — the engineering team confirms the masking plan at DFM stage.

**Q4: What colours are available in powder coating?**
Standard RAL colours are available — the RAL system covers thousands of colours from whites through blacks, all primary and secondary colours, and metallic effects. The most common specifications are: RAL 9005 (jet black), RAL 9010 (pure white), RAL 7016 (anthracite grey), RAL 5012 (light blue), and custom colours matched to customer samples. Finishes include gloss, semi-gloss, matte, and textured (wrinkle, orange peel, sand texture). Custom RAL colours can be matched for production runs — confirm colour and finish specification at quote stage.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for powder coated CNC parts?**
No minimum. Powder coating is applied to any order that specifies it, including single prototypes. Parts requiring masking have a setup cost per part, which means powder coat on single prototypes is more expensive per unit than in batches, but is a valid order. There is no minimum batch size.

**Q6: What information is needed to quote powder coated CNC parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. Specify on the drawing or in the quote request: substrate material and grade, powder type (polyester, epoxy, or polyurethane), colour (RAL number or description), finish (gloss, matte, or texture), coating thickness, masking requirements for threads and precision features, and pre-treatment requirements (iron phosphate or bead blast).

**Q7: How much does powder coating add to the cost of CNC parts?**
Powder coating adds a surface finishing charge to the base machining cost. Parts requiring extensive masking (many threads, precision bores) add setup cost for the masking operation. Single-part prototype powder coating is more expensive per unit than batch processing due to setup costs. Powder coating costs more than bead blasting or passivation but is generally less expensive than anodizing for thick-film applications. Upload your STEP file and drawing for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does powder coating compare to liquid paint for CNC machined parts?**
Powder coating and liquid paint both apply a polymer finish to metal parts, but powder coating provides significantly better mechanical performance. Powder coat film at 60–120 µm is 2–4× thicker than typical liquid paint. The cross-linked thermoset film is harder (2H–4H vs H–2H pencil hardness), more impact-resistant, and more abrasion-resistant than liquid paint. Powder coat has zero VOC emissions during application — an environmental advantage over solvent-based liquid paint. Liquid paint is used when: very thin film (<40 µm) is required, the part cannot withstand cure temperatures (160–200°C), complex colour blending or gradient effects are needed, or very large parts that cannot fit in a powder coat oven are specified. For standard metal part finishing, powder coating outperforms liquid paint in durability and is the standard industrial specification.
