## Section 2: What Is Bead Blasting

**H2:** Bead Blasting — Uniform Matte Texture by Controlled Abrasive Impact

Bead blasting propels spherical glass beads at the part surface under controlled air pressure. The spherical beads create a uniform, matte, satin-like surface texture by impacting and lightly deforming the surface — not cutting or removing significant material. The result is a consistent, non-directional surface finish that eliminates machining tool marks, reduces gloss, and provides a cosmetically uniform appearance across all machined faces.

The key distinction from other abrasive processes: bead blasting uses spherical media (not sharp-edged grit), which peens rather than cuts the surface. The result is a smoother, more uniform finish than sandblasting (which uses sharp angular grit and produces a rougher, more directional texture). Bead blasting is the correct specification when the requirement is a clean, matte, cosmetically consistent finish.

Dimensional impact is negligible for most engineering applications. The material removal from bead blasting is typically less than 5 µm — far less than the tolerance of most machined features. Bead blasting also introduces a slight compressive residual stress at the surface (shot peening effect), which can improve fatigue life. For very tight tolerance features (±0.01 mm or tighter), masking is recommended.

Bead blasting is frequently applied as a pre-treatment before anodizing aluminium. The blasted surface texture carries through the anodize process — an anodized part over a bead-blasted substrate has a matte, satin appearance rather than the semi-gloss of anodize over an as-machined surface. This is the standard specification for consumer electronics aluminium enclosures where the anodized-over-bead-blasted finish is the cosmetic reference.

## Section 7: FAQ

**H2:** Bead Blasting — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much material does bead blasting remove from CNC machined parts?**
Bead blasting with spherical glass bead media removes less than 5 µm of material in most applications — negligible for standard engineering tolerances. The process peens rather than cuts the surface. For features with tolerances tighter than ±0.025 mm, specify masking on the drawing so those surfaces are protected during blasting. Threads, precision bores, and sealing faces are typically masked before bead blasting to preserve their dimensional integrity.

**Q2: What is the difference between bead blasting and sandblasting?**
Bead blasting uses spherical glass beads — they peen the surface, producing a smooth, uniform matte texture (Ra 1.6–3.2 µm) without directional grain. Sandblasting uses sharp angular grit (typically aluminium oxide or silicon carbide) — the sharp edges cut and abrade the surface, producing a rougher, more directional texture (Ra 3.2–6.3 µm or coarser). Bead blasting is the specification for cosmetic finishes, pre-anodize treatment, and matte surface on precision parts. Sandblasting is used for aggressive surface preparation and applications requiring a coarser texture. TOP PROTO uses glass bead media as standard — angular grit media is available on request.

**Q3: Does bead blasting affect part dimensions or tolerances?**
Material removal from bead blasting is less than 5 µm — below the tolerance band of most CNC machined features. Bead blasting does not meaningfully affect part dimensions for standard tolerance work. For very tight tolerances (±0.01 mm or tighter), mask those features before blasting as a precaution. Specify masking requirements on the drawing — the engineering team will confirm whether masking is required for your part's critical features.

**Q4: Is bead blasting compatible with all metals?**
Bead blasting is compatible with aluminium, stainless steel, titanium, steel alloys, brass, and copper. Softer metals (copper, soft aluminium alloys) require attention to media selection and pressure to avoid media embedment — glass bead particles can become embedded in very soft surfaces. The engineering team confirms media selection at DFM stage for softer materials. Bead blasting is not typically applied to plastics in precision machining applications.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for bead blasted CNC parts?**
No minimum. Bead blasting is applied to any order that specifies it, including single prototypes. There is no batch minimum for surface finishing — one part is a valid order.

**Q6: What information is needed to quote bead blasted CNC parts?**
STEP file for the 3D geometry. Specify on the drawing or in the quote request: the target surface finish (Ra or appearance specification), media type (glass bead standard), masking requirements for critical features, and whether bead blasting is a standalone finish or pre-treatment before anodize.

**Q7: How much does bead blasting add to the cost of CNC parts?**
Bead blasting adds a modest surface finishing charge to the base machining cost. Parts requiring masking of threads or precision features add setup cost for the masking operation. Bead blasting is one of the lowest-cost surface finishing options — less than anodizing, powder coating, or plating. Upload your STEP file and drawing for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: Can parts be bead blasted and then anodized?**
Yes — bead blast followed by anodize is the standard process sequence for premium aluminium enclosures. The bead-blasted surface texture carries through the anodize process, producing the characteristic matte-satin anodized appearance. The bead blast step must be done before anodize (not after), as anodizing after blasting preserves the matte texture. Specify both operations on the drawing: "bead blast (glass bead) + Type II anodize [colour]" is the standard callout for this finish combination.
