## Section 2: Why Acrylic for CNC Machining

**H2:** Acrylic — Optical Clarity and UV Stability

Acrylic (PMMA — polymethyl methacrylate) is the transparent plastic specified for optical clarity and UV stability. At 92% visible light transmission, acrylic transmits more light than glass (typically 90–91%) and more than polycarbonate (88–90%). Unlike polycarbonate, acrylic is UV-stable without additives — it does not yellow under prolonged outdoor UV exposure. For display cases, architectural panels, signage, and any outdoor transparent application where the part must remain clear over time, acrylic is the specification over polycarbonate.

Brittleness is the defining limitation of acrylic and the key distinction from polycarbonate. Acrylic is significantly more brittle than polycarbonate — it shatters under impact rather than deforming. Notch sensitivity is high; acrylic will crack from stress concentrations at internal corners and holes under impact or even thermal cycling. For safety-critical transparent applications where the part may be struck or stressed, polycarbonate is the correct specification. Acrylic is the specification for optical quality, scratch resistance, and UV stability where impact risk is not the primary constraint.

Machinability is good — acrylic machines cleanly with appropriate tools and parameters, producing good surface finish. The key machining requirements are: sharp tooling (dull tools heat the material and cause melting and chipping), adequate chip clearance (chips must not re-weld to the cut surface), and correct feeds and speeds to avoid generating heat. Acrylic is susceptible to stress cracking from solvents — avoid contact with ketones, esters, and chlorinated solvents during machining and post-processing. Acrylic also generates static charges during machining that attract dust particles — relevant for optical surfaces.

Cast acrylic and extruded acrylic have different properties that affect machining. Cast acrylic (cell cast or continuous cast) has lower residual stress and better optical uniformity — it is the preferred grade for precision and optical applications. Extruded acrylic has slightly higher residual stress and more pronounced directional properties — it is lower cost and suitable for general structural applications.

## Section 5: Surface Finishes for Acrylic

**H2:** Surface Finishes for Acrylic CNC Machined Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Structural acrylic where optical clarity on machined faces is not required |
| Flame Polishing | Removes minimal surface layer | Fast optical finish on edges; not suitable for dimensional faces |
| Diamond Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Optical-quality faces — Ra 0.1 µm achievable; clearest result |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Frosted/diffuse appearance; eliminates machining marks on non-optical faces |
| UV Coating | Negligible | Enhanced UV protection for extended outdoor service |

**Optical polishing note:** As-machined acrylic is not optically clear on machined faces — tool marks scatter light. For optical-quality transparency on CNC machined faces, diamond polishing through progressive stages is required. Flame polishing is faster but less precise dimensionally and less consistent for flat surfaces — best suited to edge finishing. Specify which faces require optical finish on the drawing.

**Chemical restriction:** No contact with ketones (acetone, MEK), esters (ethyl acetate), or chlorinated solvents — all cause stress cracking in acrylic. IPA is acceptable for cleaning in limited concentrations.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Acrylic CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on acrylic CNC machined parts?**
Standard acrylic CNC work achieves ±0.05 mm on most features. Precision dimensions hold ±0.025 mm with appropriate fixturing and cast acrylic stock. Acrylic's moisture absorption is low (0.2%) — comparable to polycarbonate — so in-service dimensional change from humidity is minimal. The more significant dimensional concern with acrylic is residual stress in extruded stock, which can cause warping after material removal; cast acrylic has lower residual stress and is preferred for precision parts. Brittle behaviour also means that thin sections and sharp internal corners must be designed out — they will crack during machining at tight tolerances.

**Q2: What is the difference between cast and extruded acrylic?**
Cast acrylic (cell cast or continuous cast) is produced by casting liquid monomer into moulds or between glass plates and polymerising — the result is a material with lower residual stress, higher molecular weight, better optical uniformity, slightly higher mechanical properties (72 MPa tensile vs 65 MPa for extruded), and superior machinability for precision parts. Extruded acrylic is produced by melt extrusion — it has higher residual stress, more directional properties, and slightly lower mechanical performance, but it is lower cost and available in a wider range of thicknesses. For optical windows, precision machined parts, and display applications, specify cast acrylic. For general structural applications where cost matters more than precision, extruded acrylic is acceptable.

**Q3: How does acrylic compare to polycarbonate for CNC machined transparent parts?**
Acrylic and polycarbonate are both transparent engineering plastics but with different performance profiles. Acrylic has 92% light transmission vs 88–90% for polycarbonate — acrylic is slightly clearer. Acrylic is UV-stable without additives — polycarbonate yellows without UV coating. Acrylic is harder and more scratch-resistant than polycarbonate. However, acrylic is brittle and shatters under impact; polycarbonate is essentially unbreakable and deforms rather than fractures. Acrylic cannot be used in safety-critical applications where impact resistance is required — machine guards, safety shields, and structural enclosures require polycarbonate. Choose acrylic for displays, optical windows, signage, and UV-exposed applications where clarity and scratch resistance matter. Choose polycarbonate where the part may be struck or must not shatter.

**Q4: Can acrylic be polished to optical quality after CNC machining?**
Yes — CNC machined acrylic can be polished to optical quality using diamond polishing and flame polishing techniques. As-machined acrylic faces have tool marks that scatter light (Ra approximately 1.6 µm) and are not optically clear. Diamond polishing through progressive stages achieves Ra 0.1 µm or better — comparable to optical glass in terms of transmission loss at the surface. Flame polishing is faster for edges but less precise dimensionally — suitable for edge clarity, not for flat precision windows. Specify on the drawing which faces require optical polishing. Optical polishing adds cost and lead time relative to as-machined structural finish.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for acrylic CNC machining?**
No minimum. One acrylic part is a valid order. Material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation are the same standard regardless of quantity. Single prototypes and development samples are routine.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for acrylic CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) preferred. Also IGES, SolidWorks, CATIA, Parasolid, DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings. Attach 2D drawing when optical surface specifications, tolerance callouts, or material grade (cast vs extruded) specifications appear. Note which faces require optical polishing on the drawing.

**Q7: How much does acrylic CNC machining cost?**
Acrylic material cost is low — among the least expensive transparent engineering plastics. Cast acrylic costs more than extruded but is required for precision and optical applications. The main cost drivers for acrylic are: optical polishing (adds significant cost for any face requiring clarity), and the need for correct tooling and parameters (sharp tooling, slow feeds) to avoid chipping or cracking. A simple structural acrylic panel with as-machined faces is economical. A precision optical window in cast acrylic with polished faces costs significantly more. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does acrylic compare to glass for precision transparent components?**
Acrylic and glass both provide high optical clarity, but their mechanical properties differ fundamentally. Acrylic weighs approximately half as much as glass (density 1.19 g/cm³ vs ~2.5 g/cm³ for glass), is significantly easier to machine (glass requires diamond grinding; acrylic cuts with conventional tooling), and is far more impact-resistant than glass (though still brittle relative to polycarbonate). Glass has better scratch resistance, better chemical resistance to solvents and acids, and better temperature resistance (acrylic HDT is 84–96°C vs glass which is non-deforming to 500°C+). Glass also has marginally better optical properties for precision optics. Choose acrylic for lightweight transparent components, complex geometries, prototype optical windows, and any application where CNC machining ease and weight reduction matter. Choose glass for scratch-critical surfaces, chemical-resistant windows, and high-temperature applications.
