## Section 2: Why Polycarbonate for CNC Machining

**H2:** Polycarbonate — Impact Resistance and Optical Clarity

Polycarbonate (PC) is the engineering plastic specified when the combination of optical transparency and structural integrity is required. No other common engineering plastic approaches polycarbonate's balance of light transmission (88–90% visible light, comparable to glass), impact resistance (250× that of glass by some measures, significantly higher than acrylic), and the ability to machine to tight tolerances without cracking. For sight glasses, light guides, enclosure windows, and transparent housings that must survive impact rather than simply be clear, polycarbonate is the material specification.

Impact resistance is polycarbonate's defining mechanical property. Polycarbonate is essentially unbreakable in most practical engineering situations — it will deform and yield under extreme impact rather than fracture. This distinguishes it sharply from acrylic (PMMA), which is clearer in optical terms but brittle: acrylic shatters; polycarbonate deforms. For components that may be struck, dropped, or stressed in service, polycarbonate is the transparent plastic specification; acrylic is the display and cosmetic application plastic.

Machinability is moderate. Polycarbonate machines cleanly at appropriate speeds but is prone to stress cracking from solvents — cutting fluids and cleaning agents containing ketones, esters, or aromatic solvents attack polycarbonate and cause crazing or cracking. Machining uses dry cutting or water-based coolants only. Polycarbonate also has a tendency to melt and gum on the cutting edge at excessive feed rates — maintaining correct speeds and chip clearance prevents this. Residual internal stress in polycarbonate sheet and rod stock can cause warping after machining — stress-relieved or annealed stock is preferred for precision parts.

The limits of polycarbonate are also defined. UV resistance is poor without UV-stabilising additives or coatings — polycarbonate yellows with prolonged outdoor UV exposure. Chemical resistance is limited — ketones, esters, aromatic solvents, and strong bases attack polycarbonate. Not suitable for continuous service above 120°C. For applications within these limits and requiring transparency with structural integrity, polycarbonate is the correct specification.

## Section 5: Surface Finishes for Polycarbonate

**H2:** Surface Finishes for Polycarbonate CNC Machined Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| As Machined | None | Structural PC where appearance is not the primary requirement |
| Polishing | Removes minor surface layer | Optical surfaces, sight glasses, windows requiring maximum clarity |
| Bead Blasted | Negligible | Frosted/diffuse appearance on non-optical faces |
| UV Coating | Negligible | Outdoor-service polycarbonate — prevents UV yellowing |

**Optical surface note:** As-machined polycarbonate is not optically clear — machining marks scatter light. For optical-quality transparency, progressive polishing to Ra 0.1 µm or better is required. Specify optical surface finish requirements on the drawing — this adds significant cost compared to structural-grade as-machined finish.

**Chemical restriction:** No solvent-based cleaners or coatings on polycarbonate. No acetone, MEK, toluene, or similar solvents — these cause stress cracking and crazing. IPA (isopropanol) in low concentrations is acceptable for cleaning machined polycarbonate parts.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**H2:** Where Polycarbonate CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Optical Windows and Sight Glasses** — Transparent windows for pressure vessels, machine guards, and inspection ports. Polycarbonate's impact resistance is the specification over glass or acrylic when the window must survive shock or pressure without shattering. Polished to optical quality for maximum clarity.

**Enclosure Panels and Display Covers** — Transparent covers and panels for electronic enclosures, instrument displays, and control panel windows. Combines visibility with structural integrity and impact resistance.

**Medical Device Housings** — Transparent housings and covers for diagnostic equipment, fluid management systems, and laboratory instruments. Low moisture absorption (0.35%) provides better dimensional stability than nylon for enclosed electronics. Sterilisable by EtO and gamma — not autoclave (120°C HDT is the limit for short cycles only).

**Lighting Components** — Light diffusers, reflector housings, and light guide components. Polycarbonate's light transmission and ability to machine complex geometry makes it the specification for CNC-machined lighting components.

**Robotics and Automation** — Transparent sensor guards, end-effector covers, and cable management components where visibility into the mechanism is required for monitoring or adjustment.

**Safety Shields and Machine Guards** — Transparent guards and shields for CNC machines, presses, and automated equipment. Impact resistance is the critical property — guards are designed to contain projectiles, which requires PC not acrylic.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Polycarbonate CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on polycarbonate CNC machined parts?**
Standard polycarbonate CNC work achieves ±0.05 mm on most features. Precision bores and positional callouts hold ±0.025 mm with appropriate fixturing and stress-relieved stock. Polycarbonate's moisture absorption is 0.35% — moderate, and lower than nylon, so dimensional change from humidity in service is limited. The more significant dimensional concern with polycarbonate is residual stress in raw stock — machining removes material and can release internal stresses, causing warping. For precision polycarbonate parts, annealed or stress-relieved stock reduces this effect. Confirm tolerance requirements at quote stage.

**Q2: What is the difference between polycarbonate and acrylic for CNC machined parts?**
Polycarbonate and acrylic (PMMA) are both transparent engineering plastics, but they have fundamentally different mechanical properties. Polycarbonate has impact resistance roughly 30× higher than acrylic at equivalent thickness — polycarbonate deforms under impact; acrylic shatters. Acrylic has slightly better optical clarity (92% light transmission vs 88–90% for PC) and better UV resistance. Acrylic is harder and more scratch-resistant than polycarbonate. Acrylic is also more chemically resistant to some solvents. Choose polycarbonate when impact resistance is required — safety shields, machine guards, and structural transparent enclosures. Choose acrylic for cosmetic displays, signage, and optical applications where scratch resistance and UV stability matter more than impact performance.

**Q3: Can polycarbonate parts be sterilised for medical applications?**
Polycarbonate is compatible with EtO (ethylene oxide) and gamma radiation sterilisation. It is not suitable for steam autoclave sterilisation — the HDT of 120°C is marginal for autoclave temperatures (121–134°C), and repeated sterilisation cycles cause dimensional instability and optical degradation. For medical devices requiring autoclave sterilisation, specify PEEK, which withstands repeated autoclave cycles without dimensional change. For single-use or EtO/gamma-sterilised medical devices, polycarbonate is acceptable. Confirm sterilisation method and cycle parameters at quote stage.

**Q4: What surface finish is achievable on polycarbonate CNC machined parts?**
As-machined polycarbonate achieves Ra 1.6 µm structurally — adequate for opaque structural faces. As-machined surfaces on polycarbonate are not optically clear — machining marks scatter light. For optical clarity, progressive polishing to Ra 0.1 µm or better is required, typically through multiple polishing stages. Specify on the drawing which faces require optical finish and which are structural. Do not use solvent-based polishing compounds on polycarbonate — use water-based or mechanical polishing only.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for polycarbonate CNC machining?**
No minimum. One polycarbonate part is a valid order. Material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation are the same standard regardless of quantity. Single prototypes and development parts are routine orders.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for polycarbonate CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) preferred. Also IGES, SolidWorks, CATIA, Parasolid, DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings. Attach 2D drawing when optical surface specifications, tolerance callouts, or material grade specifications appear on the drawing.

**Q7: How much does polycarbonate CNC machining cost?**
Polycarbonate is a moderately priced engineering plastic — higher than ABS, similar to nylon, lower than PEEK or Ultem. Optical-grade polycarbonate costs more than standard structural grade. The main cost driver for polycarbonate is surface finish: structural as-machined parts are economical; optical-quality polished parts require additional polishing operations that add cost. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does polycarbonate compare to PEEK for transparent structural applications?**
Polycarbonate and PEEK serve different application tiers. Polycarbonate is transparent (88–90% light transmission) — PEEK is opaque. For any application requiring transparency, polycarbonate is the specification; PEEK is not an option. Where opacity is acceptable, PEEK has significantly better temperature resistance (HDT 160°C unfilled vs 120°C for PC), better chemical resistance, and biocompatibility for autoclave applications — at 5–10× the material cost of polycarbonate. Choose polycarbonate for transparent structural parts, sight glasses, and enclosures in normal service temperature range. Choose PEEK for high-temperature, aggressive chemical, or autoclave-sterilisation applications where transparency is not required.
