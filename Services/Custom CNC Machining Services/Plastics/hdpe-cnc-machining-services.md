## Section 2: Why HDPE for CNC Machining

**H2:** HDPE — Chemical Resistance, Low Cost, and FDA Compliance

HDPE (high-density polyethylene) is the workhorse engineering plastic for applications requiring broad chemical resistance, impact resistance, low friction against food products and process fluids, and the lowest possible material cost. Its chemical resistance to acids, bases, solvents, and most industrial chemicals is broad — not as wide as PTFE, but suitable for most common industrial chemical environments. Its FDA compliance in standard food-contact grades makes it the default for food processing wear pads, cutting boards, guide rails, and material handling components. Its low density (0.95–0.97 g/cm³) makes it among the lightest engineering plastics — lighter than nylon, Delrin, or polycarbonate.

Dimensional precision is the key limitation of HDPE and must be understood before specifying it for tight-tolerance applications. HDPE has a high coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE approximately 12–13 × 10⁻⁵/°C — similar to PTFE and higher than most engineering plastics) and creeps under sustained load. HDPE is not the material for precision fits, close-tolerance bores, or applications where dimensions must be maintained under compressive load. For applications requiring dimensional stability in service, Delrin (POM) is the better engineering plastic choice even though it costs more. HDPE is specified when chemical resistance, food safety, low cost, and impact resistance are the primary requirements — not precision.

Machinability is easy — HDPE machines with low cutting forces and is not abrasive on tooling. The challenge is the material's softness and tendency to deform under the cutting tool rather than cutting cleanly, similar to PTFE. Sharp tools, correct feeds, and adequate workpiece support are required. HDPE generates long, stringy chips that must be managed to avoid rewinding on the cutter. The material's waxy surface does not accept adhesives, coatings, or paints without surface activation — the as-machined HDPE surface is the final surface for virtually all applications.

HDPE is self-lubricating with a low surface energy — food products, liquids, and many materials do not adhere to its surface. This property, combined with FDA compliance, makes it the standard material for food processing wear surfaces, packaging equipment guides, and marine components where corrosion is the problem with metal alternatives.

## Section 6: Design Guidelines for HDPE CNC Parts

**H2:** Design Guidelines for HDPE CNC Machining

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Avoid tight tolerance fits | Use clearance fits; allow for CTE | HDPE changes dimensions significantly with temperature — pressed or interference fits will fail or loosen with thermal cycling |
| Thread callouts | Metal inserts for all load-bearing threads | HDPE threads strip easily under load — inserts are required for any joint subject to torque or sustained load |
| Wall thickness | Min 1.5 mm; prefer 3+ mm for structural walls | HDPE is soft and flexible — thin walls deform under cutting forces and service loads |
| Specify food-contact grade | State on drawing if FDA compliance required | Standard and food-contact grades may look identical — grade must be called out for regulatory compliance |
| Sharp tooling | Required — flagged at DFM | Dull tooling causes HDPE to smear and deform rather than cut cleanly |
| Sustained load | Do not design for press fits under sustained load | HDPE creeps under compressive stress — dimensions change over time under constant load |

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** HDPE CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on HDPE CNC machined parts?**
Standard HDPE CNC work achieves ±0.1 mm on most features. Precision dimensions hold ±0.05 mm with sharp tooling and careful fixturing. HDPE is not the choice for close-tolerance engineering fits — its high coefficient of thermal expansion (approximately 12–13 × 10⁻⁵/°C) means machined dimensions at room temperature differ significantly from service dimensions at elevated temperature. HDPE also creeps under sustained compressive load, so press fits and tight bore tolerances will change in service. For precision fits, use Delrin (POM) which has far better dimensional stability. Specify HDPE when chemical resistance, food safety, and cost are the primary requirements rather than dimensional precision.

**Q2: Is HDPE FDA-compliant for food and pharmaceutical contact?**
HDPE in food-contact grade complies with FDA 21 CFR 177.1520 for repeated use food contact. It is one of the most widely used plastics in food processing — cutting boards, conveyor guides, and wear strips in direct food contact. For pharmaceutical applications, food-contact HDPE grade provides the regulatory documentation base, but pharmaceutical contact applications may require additional validation testing depending on the device and application class. TOP PROTO provides material certificates identifying the specific grade and compliance status. Standard HDPE and food-contact HDPE may appear identical — always specify FDA-grade on the drawing if regulatory compliance is required.

**Q3: How does HDPE compare to Delrin for CNC machined industrial components?**
HDPE and Delrin (POM) are both low-cost engineering plastics used in similar applications — guides, pads, bushings, and structural components. Delrin has significantly better dimensional stability: lower CTE, better creep resistance, and tighter achievable tolerances. Delrin also has higher tensile strength (68 MPa vs 26–33 MPa for HDPE) and better machinability for precision parts. HDPE has broader chemical resistance (unaffected by some acids and bases that attack Delrin), lower material cost, lower density (lighter parts), and FDA food-contact compliance in standard grades. Choose Delrin when precision, dimensional stability, and strength matter. Choose HDPE when broad chemical resistance, lowest cost, lowest weight, or FDA food-contact compliance are the primary specifications.

**Q4: What surface finish is achievable on HDPE CNC machined parts?**
As-machined HDPE achieves Ra 3.2 µm on most surfaces with standard tooling. Ra 1.6 µm is achievable with sharp tooling and fine parameters. HDPE's waxy, low-surface-energy surface does not accept standard coatings, adhesives, or paints without surface activation (flame treatment, corona treatment). For HDPE assemblies, mechanical fasteners, screws with metal inserts, or thermal welding are the standard joining approaches — adhesive bonding is not reliable without specialist surface preparation.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for HDPE CNC machining?**
No minimum. One HDPE part is a valid order. Material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation are the same standard regardless of quantity. HDPE's low material cost makes single prototype orders economical.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for HDPE CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) preferred. Also IGES, SolidWorks, CATIA, Parasolid, DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings. Attach 2D drawing when FDA-grade specification, tolerance callouts, or material grade notes appear. Note the service environment (chemical exposure, temperature range, food contact requirements) in the quote request.

**Q7: How much does HDPE CNC machining cost?**
HDPE is among the least expensive CNC plastic machining options — very low material cost and excellent machinability keep cycle times short. Simple wear pads, guide rails, and spacers in HDPE are economical. FDA food-contact grade costs marginally more than standard HDPE due to material certification requirements. HDPE is often specified primarily for cost where other engineering plastics would over-specify performance requirements. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: What chemicals is HDPE resistant to?**
HDPE has broad chemical resistance: resistant to most dilute and concentrated acids (except oxidising acids such as concentrated nitric and sulphuric above certain concentrations), most alkalis and bases, alcohols, ketones, aliphatic hydrocarbons, and common industrial solvents. It is not resistant to aromatic hydrocarbons (benzene, toluene, xylene), halogenated solvents (chloroform, methylene chloride), or strong oxidising agents at elevated temperature. For critical chemical applications, confirm compatibility against the specific chemical and concentration at quote stage — chemical resistance can be concentration- and temperature-dependent. A chemical compatibility list for HDPE against common industrial chemicals is available on request.
