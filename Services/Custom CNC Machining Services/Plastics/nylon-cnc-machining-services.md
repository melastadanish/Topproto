## Section 2: Why Nylon for CNC Machining

**H2:** Nylon — Impact Resistance and Self-Lubrication

Nylon (polyamide) is the engineering plastic specified for impact resistance, self-lubrication, and chemical resistance to oils, fuels, and aliphatic solvents. Where Delrin (POM) is the precision-first plastic — low moisture absorption, dimensional stability above all — nylon is the toughness-first plastic. Nylon's elongation before fracture is typically 30–60% versus 25% for Delrin. It absorbs impact energy through deformation rather than shattering. In applications with intermittent overload, shock loading, or vibration, nylon survives where stiffer materials crack.

Moisture absorption is the defining tradeoff of nylon and must be understood before specifying it for close-tolerance applications. PA6 absorbs 2.5–3.5% moisture by weight at equilibrium in ambient conditions; PA66 absorbs 2.0–2.5%. Both are significantly higher than Delrin (<0.2%). Each percent of moisture absorption causes approximately 0.2–0.3% linear dimensional change in nylon — meaning a 100 mm bore machined to H7 fit in a dry shop environment will grow after installation in a humid or water-contact application. For precision fits, design to the expected in-service dimension, not the ambient-condition machined dimension.

Machinability is good. Nylon machines cleanly, produces good surface finish, and is not demanding on tooling. However, nylon's tendency to absorb ambient moisture during machining and storage means parts should be measured for critical dimensions after moisture equilibration if tight tolerances in service are the specification. Glass-filled grades (PA6-GF30, PA66-GF30) are abrasive and wear tooling faster than unfilled nylon.

Self-lubrication sets nylon apart from most engineering plastics. Nylon's crystalline structure and surface properties allow it to run against steel and other metals in low-to-medium load, low-speed applications without external lubrication. MoS2-filled nylon extends this further — the molybdenum disulphide filler is a solid lubricant that improves dry-running performance and reduces wear rate.

## Section 6: Design Guidelines for Nylon CNC Parts

**H2:** Design Guidelines for Nylon CNC Machining

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture allowance | Design bore diameters to in-service size, not ambient-condition size | Nylon absorbs 2.5–3.5% moisture — bores machined tight in dry conditions will grow in humid service |
| Specify grade on drawing | State PA6, PA66, GF30, or MoS2 explicitly | Grade affects moisture absorption, dimensional stability, and machinability |

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**H2:** Where Nylon CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Gears and Drive Components** — Spur gears, helical gears, rack sections, and drive cams in light-to-medium load applications. Nylon (especially MoS2-filled) runs against steel or nylon mating gears quietly and without lubrication. Self-lubricating property reduces maintenance requirements versus metal gears in dry-running systems.

**Bushings and Wear Pads** — Sleeve bushings, guide pads, and wear strips in conveyor systems, agricultural machinery, and general industrial equipment. Self-lubricating nylon outperforms bronze in water-contact applications where an oil film cannot be maintained.

**Structural Housings and Brackets** — Lightweight enclosures, cable management components, and structural brackets. PA66-GF30 for load-bearing structural parts requiring stiffness and dimensional stability.

**Fluid and Chemical Handling** — Valve bodies, manifold blocks, and pump components in water, oil, and aliphatic solvent service. Chemical resistance adequate for most common industrial fluids. Not suitable for strong acids, bases, or aromatic solvents.

**Automotive Components** — Under-hood components, clips, brackets, and fluid system parts. PA66-GF30 heat resistance (HDT ~250°C at 1.8 MPa) suits higher-temperature automotive environments. Standard OEM specification for polymer structural components.

**Consumer and General Engineering** — Fixture plates, jig components, guide rails, and non-conductive structural parts. Nylon's combination of machinability, toughness, and cost makes it the default engineering plastic when precision dimensional stability is not the primary constraint.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Nylon CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on nylon CNC machined parts?**
Standard nylon CNC work achieves ±0.05 mm on most features. Precision bores hold ±0.025 mm with appropriate fixturing. The critical caveat for nylon is moisture absorption: PA6 absorbs 2.5–3.5% moisture at equilibrium, PA66 absorbs 2.0–2.5%. This corresponds to a linear dimensional change of approximately 0.2–0.3% per percent moisture absorbed. For precision bores and close fits, specify the in-service humidity condition at quote stage — tolerances should be designed to the in-service dimension, not the ambient-condition machined dimension. Glass-filled grades (GF30) reduce moisture absorption to 1.0–2.0% and provide better dimensional stability for close-tolerance work.

**Q2: What is the difference between PA6 and PA66 nylon?**
PA6 (polycaprolactam) and PA66 (polyhexamethylene adipamide) are both engineering nylons with similar property profiles but different base chemistry. PA6 has slightly higher impact resistance, better machinability, and lower cost — it is the default for general-purpose nylon CNC parts. PA66 has higher stiffness (85 MPa tensile vs 75 MPa for PA6), better heat resistance, and slightly lower moisture absorption. PA66 is specified where higher operating temperature, greater stiffness, or marginally better dimensional stability justifies the modestly higher cost. For most structural, gear, and bushing applications, PA6 is correct and more economical. Glass-filled variants (GF30) of both grades significantly improve stiffness and reduce moisture absorption compared to unfilled grades.

**Q3: Can nylon be used in wet or water-contact applications?**
Yes — nylon is used in water-contact applications including conveyor bushings, pump components, and food processing equipment. Chemical resistance to water, oils, and aliphatic solvents is good. The critical consideration is dimensional change from moisture absorption: nylon absorbs water and dimensions change with moisture content. Parts machined in ambient conditions will grow when exposed to water or humid environments. For precision fits in water-contact applications, design to the expected in-service wet dimension, use glass-filled grades (lower moisture absorption), or specify Delrin (POM) if dimensional stability is the primary requirement.

**Q4: What surface finish is achievable on nylon CNC machined parts?**
As-machined Ra 1.6 µm is standard on nylon. Ra 0.8 µm is achievable with fine machining parameters. Polishing achieves Ra 0.4 µm or better for gear tooth flanks and bearing surfaces. Nylon does not accept most standard adhesives or paints without surface activation — for bonding nylon assemblies, mechanical fasteners, snap fits, press fits, or ultrasonic welding are the standard approaches. MoS2-filled nylon has a grey-black coloration compared to natural nylon (white or cream).

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for nylon CNC machining?**
No minimum. One nylon part is a valid order. Material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation are the same standard regardless of quantity. Single prototypes and development samples are routine.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for nylon CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) preferred. Also IGES, SolidWorks, CATIA, Parasolid, DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings. Attach 2D drawing when tolerance callouts, moisture-condition specifications, or fit class specifications appear. For close-tolerance nylon parts, note the intended service humidity on the drawing or in the quote request.

**Q7: How much does nylon CNC machining cost?**
Nylon is one of the lowest-cost precision plastic machining options — lower material cost than PEEK, Ultem, or Delrin, and good machinability keeps cycle times low. PA6 is lower cost than PA66; glass-filled grades cost more due to higher material price and increased tool wear. A standard PA6 gear or bushing is significantly less expensive than equivalent Delrin, PEEK, or aluminium work. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does nylon compare to Delrin for CNC machined parts?**
Nylon and Delrin (POM) are both common engineering plastics for gears, bushings, and structural components, but they serve different application priorities. Delrin has significantly lower moisture absorption (<0.2% vs 2.5–3.5% for PA6) — giving it superior dimensional stability in varying humidity and making it the default for precision fits and close-tolerance bores. Nylon has higher impact resistance and elongation before fracture — making it better for parts subject to shock loading or intermittent overload. Nylon's self-lubricating properties in wet environments make it better than Delrin in water-contact bearing applications. Choose Delrin for precision fits, gears requiring tight dimensional tolerances, and applications where moisture variation matters. Choose nylon for impact-critical parts, water-contact bearings, and applications where toughness is the primary specification.
