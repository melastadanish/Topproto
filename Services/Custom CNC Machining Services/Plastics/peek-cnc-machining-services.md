## Section 2: Why PEEK for CNC Machining

**Design:** Two-column. White background. Text left, image right.

**H2:** PEEK — The High-Performance Engineering Polymer

PEEK (polyether ether ketone) sits at the top of the engineering polymer performance pyramid. Continuous service temperature to 250°C — no common engineering polymer handles higher sustained heat. Chemical resistance across nearly all organic solvents, acids, bases, and sterilisation media including autoclaving, EtO, and gamma radiation. Biocompatible per ISO 10993. Mechanical properties approaching metal at elevated temperatures. These properties make PEEK the specification when no other polymer can satisfy the application.

The thermal performance is the first differentiator. Standard engineering plastics — ABS, nylon, polycarbonate — have heat deflection temperatures in the range of 80–130°C. PEEK's HDT is 160°C (unfilled) and up to 300°C in carbon-filled or glass-filled grades. For components in continuous high-temperature service, near heat sources, or in repeated autoclave sterilisation cycles, PEEK is the polymer specification where others fail.

Chemical resistance is the second. PEEK does not solvate in most aggressive process chemicals. It resists concentrated sulphuric acid (under certain conditions), HF acid (at low concentrations), and a wide range of organic solvents that attack nylon, Delrin, and polycarbonate. For chemical process equipment, semiconductor wet-bench hardware, and medical devices sterilised by aggressive agents, PEEK's chemical stability is the enabling property.

The cost is the tradeoff. PEEK material costs significantly more than engineering plastics like Delrin or nylon — raw material price is typically 5–10× higher than Delrin per kilogram. Machining cost is also higher because PEEK generates abrasive chips that wear tooling faster than softer plastics. PEEK is specified where those costs are justified by the application requirements — not as a substitute for Delrin on standard applications.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**Design:** Two-column icon+text grid. White background.

**H2:** Where PEEK CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Medical Devices and Implants**
Spinal implant trial inserts, surgical guide components, catheter hubs, and device housings that require repeated autoclave sterilisation. Unfilled PEEK is biocompatible per ISO 10993 and radiolucent — it does not interfere with imaging. PEEK CF30 for structural spinal cages and implants requiring higher stiffness.

**Semiconductor and Electronics Manufacturing**
Wafer handling fixtures, process chamber components, wet-bench hardware, and precision alignment components in aggressive chemical environments (HF, H2SO4, solvents). PEEK unfilled resists most wet process chemicals where metal or standard plastics would corrode or solvate.

**Aerospace**
Primary and secondary structural components where low weight and high temperature are both required. PEEK GF30 and CF30 replace metal in certain aerospace applications — lighter than aluminium at equivalent stiffness in the right geometry. CMM inspection and material traceability standard.

**Oil and Gas**
Valve components, seals, and downhole tool components exposed to high temperature, high pressure, and H2S-bearing environments. PEEK's chemical resistance and mechanical properties at elevated temperature (continuous to 250°C) suit demanding downhole service.

**Bearings and Tribological Applications**
PEEK CF30 for bearing cages, thrust washers, and wear pads. The carbon fibre filler reduces coefficient of friction and improves wear resistance. Used in semiconductor and food processing equipment where metal bearings would contaminate the product.

**Chemical and Process Equipment**
Pump components, valve seats, manifold fittings, and heat exchanger baffles in contact with aggressive process chemicals. PEEK's solvent resistance makes it an alternative to PTFE where higher strength is required.

## Section 9: FAQ

**Design:** Accordion. White background.

**H2:** PEEK CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on PEEK CNC machined parts?**
Standard PEEK CNC work achieves ±0.05 mm on most features. Precision bores and positional callouts can achieve ±0.025 mm with appropriate fixturing and toolpath strategy. PEEK is dimensionally more stable than many engineering plastics — its low moisture absorption (0.1% vs 1.5% for nylon) and relatively low coefficient of thermal expansion reduce in-service dimensional change. For tight-tolerance PEEK parts, annealing before final machining reduces residual stress movement. Confirm tolerance requirements at quote stage — the engineering team assesses feasibility per geometry.

**Q2: What is the difference between unfilled PEEK, PEEK GF30, and PEEK CF30?**
Unfilled PEEK provides the best chemical resistance and biocompatibility — it is the specification for medical, semiconductor, and chemical process applications. PEEK GF30 (30% glass fibre) has higher stiffness and strength, better creep resistance, and HDT up to 300°C — at the cost of lower chemical resistance (glass fibres can be attacked in HF and some strong bases) and higher tool wear during machining. PEEK CF30 (30% carbon fibre) has the highest stiffness of the three, low coefficient of friction, and slight electrical conductivity — used for bearing surfaces and structural applications. CF30 generates conductive carbon dust during machining, which requires specific handling. Specify the grade on your drawing; if unsure, the engineering team will advise based on your application.

**Q3: Is PEEK biocompatible for medical device applications?**
Unfilled PEEK is referenced in ISO 10993-1 as a biocompatible polymeric material. It is non-toxic, non-reactive in biological environments, radiolucent (transparent to X-ray), and compatible with autoclave, EtO, and gamma radiation sterilisation methods. It is used in spinal implants, surgical guides, and implant-adjacent components. As with all medical device materials, biocompatibility confirmation for a specific device depends on the application, contact type, and device classification — TOP PROTO provides material certificates and traceability to support your validation documentation. Filled grades (GF30, CF30) have different biocompatibility profiles and must be confirmed separately.

**Q4: Can PEEK parts be autoclaved?**
Yes — unfilled PEEK retains its mechanical properties and dimensional stability through repeated autoclave sterilisation cycles (134°C steam under pressure). This is one of the key reasons it is specified for surgical instrument components and reusable device hardware. PEEK GF30 and CF30 may absorb slightly more moisture after repeated sterilisation cycles, which can affect dimensional precision in critical fits — confirm the grade for autoclave applications requiring tight tolerances.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for PEEK CNC machining?**
No minimum. One PEEK part is a valid order. Single prototypes, development batches, and production runs receive the same material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation. PEEK's high material cost makes per-unit cost on small quantities significant, but there is no order floor.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for PEEK CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) is the preferred format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings are also accepted. Attach your 2D drawing when tight-tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, or material biocompatibility notes appear.

**Q7: How much does PEEK CNC machining cost?**
PEEK CNC machining is among the most expensive plastic machining options — driven by high raw material cost (PEEK is 5–10× more expensive per kg than Delrin), faster tool wear (especially on filled grades), and lower cutting speeds relative to softer plastics. A simple unfilled PEEK component costs significantly more than equivalent Delrin or nylon work. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours. DFM review is included at no additional charge. If cost is the primary constraint, Delrin or nylon should be considered if the application requirements allow it.

**Q8: How does PEEK compare to Delrin for CNC machined plastic parts?**
PEEK and Delrin are both precision engineering plastics, but they serve different performance tiers. Delrin (POM) has HDT of 100°C and good machinability at moderate cost — it is the standard precision plastic for gears, bushings, and structural components in normal service conditions. PEEK has HDT of 160°C (unfilled) to 300°C (filled), significantly better chemical resistance, and biocompatibility for medical applications — at 5–10× the material cost of Delrin. Choose Delrin when the application is within its service envelope and cost matters. Choose PEEK when operating temperature exceeds 100°C, the chemical environment attacks Delrin, autoclave sterilisation is required, or biocompatibility demands it.
