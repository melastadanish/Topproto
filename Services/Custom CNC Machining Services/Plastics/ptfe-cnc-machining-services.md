## Section 2: Why PTFE for CNC Machining

**H2:** PTFE — Chemical Resistance and the Lowest Friction of Any Engineering Solid

PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) is the material specified when the application requires universal chemical resistance, the lowest possible friction, and a wide temperature range in a single material. No other solid engineering material has a lower static coefficient of friction than PTFE (approximately 0.04 against steel — lower than ice on ice). No common engineering polymer has wider chemical resistance: PTFE is unaffected by virtually all industrial acids, bases, solvents, and oxidising agents at temperatures up to 260°C continuous. The fluorine-carbon bond in PTFE is one of the strongest in organic chemistry — it is what makes PTFE chemically inert across environments that destroy every other polymer.

Dimensional stability is the critical limitation of PTFE and must be understood before specifying it for precision applications. PTFE has a high coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) — approximately 10–12 × 10⁻⁵/°C, roughly 5–10× that of aluminium and significantly higher than most engineering plastics. PTFE also creeps (cold flows) under sustained compressive load — a PTFE seal or gasket under bolt load will compress over time as the material flows. For precision fits and close-tolerance applications, PTFE's dimensional change with temperature and load must be factored into the design. Filled grades (glass-filled, carbon-filled) significantly reduce creep and CTE — improving PTFE's dimensional behaviour for structural applications.

Machinability is good in terms of cutting forces — PTFE machines easily and requires relatively low cutting forces. However, PTFE's softness and tendency to creep under cutting forces creates a different challenge: the material can deform under the cutting tool rather than cut cleanly, particularly at very tight tolerances. Sharp tools, correct feeds, and adequate support of the workpiece are required. PTFE is not abrasive on tooling. Chips are continuous and stringy in unfilled grades — chip management is required. Filled grades (particularly glass-filled) produce better-controlled chips.

The functional properties of PTFE — chemical inertness, non-stick surface, and self-lubrication — mean the material rarely requires any surface treatment. The as-machined PTFE surface is the functional surface for virtually all applications. Coatings do not adhere to PTFE; PTFE itself is the non-stick coating reference material.

## Section 7: Applications and Industries

**H2:** Where PTFE CNC Machined Parts Are Used

**Seals and Gaskets** — PTFE rod seals, V-packing, lip seals, and flat gaskets for chemical process equipment, hydraulic systems, and food processing machinery. Universal chemical resistance and low friction are the specification properties. PTFE seals run against stainless steel, aluminium, and hard chrome without lubrication.

**Chemical Process Equipment** — Valve seats, valve bodies, pump components, manifold liners, and reactor vessel fittings in contact with aggressive chemicals (concentrated acids, bases, oxidising agents, solvents). PTFE is unaffected by virtually every common industrial chemical at temperatures up to 260°C.

**Bearings and Bushings** — Filled PTFE (glass or carbon-filled) for plain bearings, thrust washers, and guide pads in chemical or food processing environments where lubricant contamination is prohibited. PTFE's self-lubricating property eliminates the need for oil or grease.

**Laboratory and Pharmaceutical Equipment** — Tubing connectors, valve inserts, and fittings in laboratory and pharmaceutical fluid systems. Virgin PTFE is FDA-compliant and suitable for pharmaceutical process contact. Inert to extraction by solvents — does not leach into process fluids.

**Food Processing Equipment** — Valve components, guide rails, conveyor pads, and bearing elements in food processing lines. Complies with FDA 21 CFR 177.1550 for food contact. Non-stick surface prevents product adhesion.

**Electrical Insulation** — Insulators, stand-offs, and wire guides in high-frequency and high-voltage electrical applications. PTFE has one of the lowest dielectric constants of any solid material — suitable for RF and microwave frequency applications.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** PTFE CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can you hold on PTFE CNC machined parts?**
Standard PTFE CNC work achieves ±0.05 mm on most features. Precision dimensions hold ±0.025 mm with appropriate fixturing and sharp tooling. Two factors must be considered for tight-tolerance PTFE: first, PTFE's coefficient of thermal expansion (10–12 × 10⁻⁵/°C) is high — machined dimensions at room temperature will differ from service dimensions at elevated temperature, and this must be accounted for in precision designs. Second, PTFE creeps under sustained compressive load — seals and structural parts under bolt preload will change dimension over time. For precision PTFE parts, state the service temperature and any sustained load conditions at quote stage so the engineering team can advise on dimensional strategy.

**Q2: What is the difference between virgin PTFE and filled PTFE grades?**
Virgin PTFE (unfilled) provides the widest chemical resistance, lowest friction (CoF approximately 0.04 against steel), and FDA food-contact compliance — it is the specification for chemical process equipment, seals in aggressive chemical environments, and pharmaceutical/food contact applications. Filled PTFE grades add fillers (glass fibre, carbon, MoS2) to improve mechanical behaviour — filled grades have lower creep under compressive load, better dimensional stability with temperature, and higher compressive strength. The tradeoff is reduced chemical resistance (glass fibres are attacked by HF and strong bases), loss of FDA food-contact suitability (in most filled grades), and slightly higher friction. Specify virgin PTFE for chemical and food/pharma applications; specify filled PTFE for bearings, valve seats, and structural components where dimensional stability under load is required.

**Q3: Is PTFE FDA-compliant for food and pharmaceutical contact?**
Virgin PTFE complies with FDA 21 CFR 177.1550 for food contact applications. It is widely used in food processing valves, conveyors, and bearings. For pharmaceutical applications, virgin PTFE's inertness (it does not leach extractables or absorb process fluids) makes it a preferred material for fluid path components. Filled PTFE grades — glass-filled, carbon-filled — do not carry the same FDA food-contact clearance and should not be specified for food or pharmaceutical contact without verification of the specific filled grade's compliance status. Confirm FDA compliance requirements at quote stage; TOP PROTO provides material certificates identifying grade and base resin for regulatory documentation.

**Q4: What surface finish is achievable on PTFE CNC machined parts?**
As-machined PTFE achieves Ra 1.6 µm on most surfaces — the natural surface of machined PTFE is smooth and functions as the sealing or bearing surface in virtually all applications. Polishing to Ra 0.4 µm or better is achievable for precision sealing surfaces. No coating or plating adheres to PTFE without specialist surface activation (chemical etching or plasma treatment). For standard CNC machined PTFE parts, the as-machined surface is the finished surface — no post-process surface treatment is required or typically possible.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for PTFE CNC machining?**
No minimum. One PTFE part is a valid order. Material verification, dimensional inspection, and documentation are the same standard regardless of quantity. Single prototype seals, development samples, and production runs are all accepted.

**Q6: What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for PTFE CNC quotes?**
STEP (.step / .stp) preferred. Also IGES, SolidWorks, CATIA, Parasolid, DXF, DWG, and PDF drawings. Attach 2D drawing when seal groove dimensions, tolerance callouts, or material grade specifications appear. For seal components, include mating shaft or housing dimensions in the quote request so the engineering team can review fit and creep allowance.

**Q7: How much does PTFE CNC machining cost?**
PTFE material cost is moderate — higher than nylon or Delrin, lower than PEEK. The main machining cost driver is the requirement for sharp tooling and careful setup to avoid deforming the soft material during cutting. Simple rotational parts (rod seals, gaskets, bushings) are economical. Complex multi-axis PTFE parts with tight tolerances require more careful setup and fixturing. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does PTFE compare to Delrin for seal and bushing applications?**
PTFE and Delrin (POM) serve different functions. PTFE's defining properties are chemical resistance and the lowest friction of any solid — it is specified for seals in aggressive chemical environments, pharmaceutical contact, and ultra-low-friction bearing applications. Delrin has far better dimensional stability under load (lower CTE, essentially no creep at normal loads) and much higher tensile strength (68 MPa vs 25 MPa for PTFE) — making it the correct specification for precision gears, close-tolerance bores, and structural parts that must hold dimensions under mechanical load. Choose PTFE for seals, chemical process fittings, and applications where friction or chemical resistance is the primary specification. Choose Delrin for gears, precision bores, and structural components where dimensional stability and mechanical strength matter.
