## What Is Bead Blasting?

Bead blasting — also called glass bead blasting — propels fine spherical glass bead media at a part surface under controlled blast pressure. The impact of the beads peens the surface uniformly, removing machining marks, tool lines, and surface irregularities while leaving a consistent matte texture. Ra roughness after bead blasting runs 3.2–6.3µm depending on bead media shot size and blast pressure.

Unlike abrasive blasting with angular media, glass bead media is spherical — it compresses and smooths the surface rather than cutting into it. The result is a uniform satin-matte appearance that looks the same across the entire part, not just on flat faces. ### Surface Profile and Ra Value

Ra — arithmetical mean roughness — measures surface texture as the average deviation of the surface from its mean line. As-machined CNC parts typically sit at Ra 3.2µm on standard milling passes. Bead blasting at standard parameters produces Ra 3.2–6.3µm — a slightly coarser texture than a fine machined surface, but one that is uniform across all faces rather than showing directional tool marks. Finer bead media and lower blast pressure produce the lower end of the Ra range; coarser media and higher pressure produce the upper end. Specify your Ra target at quoting stage if the finish tolerance matters for your application.

### Bead Blasting as Pre-treatment

Bead blasting before anodizing creates a uniform, matte pre-surface that produces consistent colour uptake across the entire part. Without pre-blasting, anodizing can highlight the directional lines left by machining — visible even after dyeing and sealing. Before powder coating or painting, bead blasting creates a surface profile that improves mechanical adhesion of the coating to the substrate. In both cases, bead blasting is applied first, then the secondary finish follows on the same order at TOP PROTO.

## Bead Blasting Process Steps

**Step 1 — Part inspection:** Parts are checked for burrs, sharp edges, and surface defects before blasting. Burrs are deburred first — blasting does not remove burrs, it rounds them slightly without eliminating them.

**Step 2 — Masking:** Threads, precision bores, sealing faces, and any feature where surface roughness must stay at the machined value are masked before the blast cabinet. Specify masked features on your drawing or at quoting stage.

**Step 3 — Media selection:** Shot size and blast pressure are set for the material and target Ra. Stainless steel and titanium parts use dedicated glass bead media — separate from media used on aluminium and steel — to prevent cross-contamination and iron transfer onto passive surfaces.

**Step 4 — Blasting:** Parts are processed in the blast cabinet at controlled pressure. Blast distance, angle, and duration are controlled to produce consistent Ra roughness across all accessible surfaces. Internal features that can be reached by the blast nozzle are treated; blind features that cannot be reached are left at machined finish and noted on the inspection record.

**Step 5 — Media removal and cleaning:** Residual glass bead media is removed by air blowing and brushing. For stainless steel and titanium parts, additional cleaning confirms no glass bead residue remains in crevices before passivation or packaging.

**Step 6 — Inspection:** Ra measurement on representative surfaces. Visual check for uniform matte appearance, absence of tool marks, and masking seal integrity.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is bead blasting and what does it do to a machined part?**

Bead blasting propels spherical glass bead media at a CNC machined part surface under controlled blast pressure. The impact peens the surface uniformly, removing machining marks, tool lines, and directional patterns left by milling or turning. The result is a consistent satin-matte finish at Ra 3.2–6.3µm across the entire part. Because glass bead media is spherical rather than angular, it compresses the surface rather than cutting into it — producing a smoother, more uniform texture than angular abrasive blasting at equivalent roughness values.

**Q2: What materials can be bead blasted?**

Bead blasting works on most metals — aluminium, steel, stainless steel, titanium, copper, and brass are all compatible. Some engineering plastics (ABS, nylon, PEEK) accept bead blasting; softer plastics (PP, PTFE) are not suitable because the media embeds into or deforms the surface. Stainless steel and titanium require dedicated glass bead media — separate from media used on aluminium and steel — to prevent iron particle transfer onto passive surfaces, which would cause corrosion. Specify your material at quoting stage and TOP PROTO will confirm compatibility and media selection.

**Q3: What Ra surface finish does bead blasting produce?**

Bead blasting at TOP PROTO produces Ra 3.2–6.3µm. The exact value depends on bead media shot size and blast pressure — finer media at lower pressure produces Ra closer to 3.2µm; coarser media or higher pressure produces Ra closer to 6.3µm. For comparison, standard CNC milling produces Ra 3.2µm with directional tool marks; bead blasting at the same Ra value removes those marks and creates a non-directional uniform texture. Specify your Ra target at quoting if the roughness value matters for your application.

**Q4: Does bead blasting change part dimensions?**

Dimensional change from bead blasting is sub-micron — negligible on standard wall thicknesses and machined features. The glass bead peening action compresses the surface rather than removing material. For very thin walls (under 0.5mm) or precision ground surfaces, blast pressure is reduced or blasting is excluded — flagged at DFM review. Masking prevents the blast reaching critical bores, threads, and sealing faces where any surface change would affect function. Specify masked features on your drawing.

**Q5: Can bead blasting be combined with other finishes?**

Yes — bead blasting is often applied as pre-treatment before anodizing, powder coating, or painting. Pre-anodize bead blasting produces a uniform matte surface that accepts anodize dye consistently without highlighting machining patterns. Before powder coating, bead blasting creates a surface profile that improves mechanical adhesion. Before passivation on stainless steel, bead blasting removes surface oxidation and creates a clean substrate. All combinations can be ordered together — specify the process sequence at quoting stage and TOP PROTO coordinates both steps.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local bead blasting shop?**

A local shop handles blasting as a standalone process — your parts travel to them and return before any subsequent finishing. That handoff introduces handling risk and delays the secondary process. TOP PROTO coordinates bead blasting with machining and any secondary finish (anodizing, passivation, powder coat) on the same order at the Dongguan facility. Dedicated media per material family prevents cross-contamination that a shared-media shop may not control. DFM review at quoting identifies geometry issues — blind holes that trap media, thin walls needing adjusted blast pressure — before machining begins.
