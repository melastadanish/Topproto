## What Is Anodizing?

Anodizing is an electrochemical surface treatment that converts the outer layer of an aluminium part into a dense aluminium oxide — the anodic oxide layer — through controlled immersion in a sulphuric acid bath. The layer forms from the parent material itself, not deposited on top of it, so adhesion is permanent. It cannot peel or flake like a sprayed coating.

TOP PROTO applies anodizing at its Dongguan, China facility to aluminium grades 5052, 6061, and 7075. Two process variants are available: Type II (standard anodizing) and Type III (hard anodizing). Each serves different applications — Type II for corrosion resistance and colour; Type III for wear surfaces and mechanical durability.

### Type II Anodizing — Corrosion Resistance and Colour

Type II anodizing produces a coating thickness of 5–25µm. The porous oxide layer accepts dyeing before sealing — colour options (clear, black, and custom) are achievable in the same process run. Sealing closes the pores after dyeing, converting the surface to a stable, corrosion-resistant finish. Masking is available where threads, sealing faces, or tight-tolerance bores must remain bare metal.

### Type III Hard Anodizing — Wear Resistance

Type III runs in a colder, more concentrated sulphuric acid bath at higher current density. This grows a thicker, denser anodic oxide layer — 25–150µm — with hardness reaching HV 400. Dimensional change is predictable: roughly half the coating thickness grows outward, the other half converts inward. For precision bores or shafts, specify the post-anodize final dimension on your drawing and TOP PROTO will machine the pre-anodize dimension to hit the post-coating target.

## Anodizing Process Steps

**Step 1 — Pre-treatment and degreasing:** Parts are cleaned with alkaline degreasing solution to remove machining oils, coolant residue, and handling contamination. Surface prep quality here determines coating adhesion throughout the entire process.

**Step 2 — Masking:** Threads, sealing faces, press-fit bores, and any feature where dimensional or electrical continuity must be preserved are masked before bath entry. Call out masked features on your drawing or in quoting notes.

**Step 3 — Etching (where specified):** An alkaline etch removes the natural oxide skin and creates a uniform surface for the anodic layer to form on. Etched parts show a matte satin appearance before anodizing begins.

**Step 4 — Sulphuric acid bath:** Parts hang on titanium racks and are immersed in the sulphuric acid bath. Direct current drives anodic oxide layer formation. Bath temperature, current density, and dwell time control coating thickness — Type III runs colder and at higher current to build the denser, thicker layer.

**Step 5 — Dyeing (Type II, where specified):** The porous oxide layer accepts organic dye by immersion. Black and custom colours are available. Dye depth depends on coating thickness — thicker coatings hold more dye and produce richer colour.

**Step 6 — Sealing and inspection:** Hot deionised water sealing closes the pores and locks the dye. Coating thickness is measured using eddy-current gauge. Visual inspection checks colour consistency, masking seal integrity, and surface quality before shipment.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is anodizing and what does it do to an aluminium part?**

Anodizing converts the outer surface of an aluminium part into aluminium oxide — the anodic oxide layer — by passing direct current through the part while it is immersed in a sulphuric acid bath. The result is a hard, corrosion-resistant ceramic layer that bonds permanently to the substrate because it forms from the parent material itself. Unlike paint or plating, it cannot peel. Type II produces 5–25µm for corrosion resistance and colour. Type III builds to 25–150µm at hardness up to HV 400 for wear-critical surfaces.

**Q2: What aluminium grades are compatible with anodizing?**

Anodizing works on wrought aluminium alloys — the grades TOP PROTO machines most often are 5052, 6061, and 7075. All three anodize well, with 6061 giving the most consistent, bright finish. Grade 7075 produces a naturally darker tone due to its zinc content. Grade 2024 is possible but its high copper content reduces corrosion performance after anodizing. Anodizing is not compatible with stainless steel, titanium, steel, copper, brass, or plastics — those materials require different surface treatment processes.

**Q3: How thick is the anodizing coating, and which type should I specify?**

Type II coating thickness runs 5–25µm. Choose Type II when you need corrosion resistance, colour, or a protective cosmetic surface. Type III hard anodize builds to 25–150µm at hardness up to HV 400. Choose Type III when the surface will experience sliding wear, abrasion, or aggressive chemical exposure. For precision parts, specify the post-anodize final dimension on your drawing — TOP PROTO will machine to the pre-anodize dimension calculated to hit your target after the coating grows.

**Q4: Does anodizing change part dimensions?**

Yes — and the dimensional change is predictable. Roughly half the coating thickness grows outward from the original surface; the other half converts inward. A 25µm Type III coating adds approximately 12.5µm to each exposed surface. For non-critical surfaces this is negligible. For bores, shafts, and tight-fitting features, specify your post-anodize dimension and let TOP PROTO calculate the pre-anodize machined target. Masking prevents dimensional change on threaded features and critical sealing surfaces.

**Q5: What colour options are available with anodizing?**

Type II anodizing accepts dye before sealing. Standard options are clear (natural silver-grey aluminium tone), black, and custom colours including blue, red, gold, and green — confirm custom colours at quoting stage. Type III hard anodize has limited dye uptake at greater thicknesses — black is the most reliable option. Colour consistency within a batch is high. Batch-to-batch variation is normal for anodizing — if exact colour matching across multiple orders is critical, specify a colour reference standard at quoting.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO's anodizing compare to using a separate local finishing house?**

A separate finishing house introduces a handoff — machined parts travel, get re-handled, and return before you see the finished product. Every transfer adds risk of surface damage, dimensional measurement uncertainty, and lead time extension. TOP PROTO coordinates machining and anodizing on the same order, calculates pre-anodize dimensions on your behalf, and ships the finished part as a single delivery. DFM review at quoting stage catches anodizing-specific geometry issues before metal is cut — a separate finisher sees the part after machining and has no input on those decisions.
