## What Is Electroplating?

Electroplating is an electrodeposition process. The part (cathode) and an anode material — the plating metal — are immersed in a bath of dissolved metal salts (electrolyte). Direct current drives metal ions from the anode solution to deposit uniformly onto the cathode surface. Plating thickness is controlled by current density — the amperes per square centimetre applied to the part — and by immersion time. Both are adjusted per part geometry to hit the specified thickness.

Different plating metals serve different engineering functions. Zinc plating provides sacrificial corrosion protection — zinc corrodes preferentially, protecting the steel substrate underneath. Chrome plating produces a hard surface (up to 900 HV in hard chrome applications) for wear resistance. Gold plating gives reliable low-contact-resistance electrical conductivity on connector pins and contact surfaces. Copper plating is used as an undercoat to improve adhesion of subsequent plating layers, or for electrical conductivity applications.

Substrate preparation — substrate prep — is critical. Bath chemistry and current density are dialled per the substrate material; plating onto steel requires different bath parameters than plating onto copper or brass. Masking protects features that must remain unplated — threads, precision bores, and mating surfaces specified on the drawing are masked before the part enters the bath. Specify masked features at the quoting stage so adhesion and dimensional allowances are confirmed during the DFM review.

## Process Steps

1. **Part review and masking specification** — Drawing review confirms which surfaces receive plating, which are masked, and what dimensional allowance is needed for plating thickness.
2. **Substrate preparation** — Parts are degreased, alkaline-cleaned, and acid-activated to remove oxides and machining residue. Clean substrate is the foundation of adhesion in electroplating.
3. **Masking** — Threads, precision bores, and close-tolerance faces are masked per the quoting stage review. Masking material is selected for the bath chemistry used.
4. **Strike layer (where required)** — Stainless steel and aluminium substrates receive a thin strike layer (copper or nickel) to establish adhesion before the primary plating metal is deposited.
5. **Plating bath** — Part enters the electrolytic bath at the confirmed current density. Immersion time and current are controlled to build to the specified thickness.
6. **Post-plate treatment** — Zinc-plated parts typically receive a chromate passivation layer for additional corrosion protection and colour (clear, yellow, or black chromate). Gold and silver parts receive a final rinse.
7. **Inspection** — Plating thickness is measured by X-ray fluorescence (XRF) or magnetic gauge. Adhesion, appearance, and dimensional checks on critical features complete the inspection.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is electroplating and what does it do to a metal part?**

Electroplating uses direct electrical current to deposit a thin layer of metal — zinc, chrome, gold, copper, or silver — onto a substrate part. The part acts as the cathode in an electrolytic bath of dissolved metal salts, and ions deposit uniformly onto the surface as current flows. The plating layer adds corrosion resistance (zinc sacrificial protection on steel), wear resistance (hard chrome up to 900 HV), or electrical conductivity (gold on connector contacts). Plating thickness is controlled by current density and immersion time.

**Q2: What materials can be electroplated?**

Steel, copper, and brass are the primary substrates for electroplating. Mild steel and alloy steel are the standard base for zinc and chrome plating. Copper and brass accept gold and silver plating directly with good adhesion. Stainless steel can be plated using a copper or nickel strike layer to establish adhesion — confirm feasibility at quoting stage. Aluminium is possible with a zincate pre-treatment — confirm per alloy. Titanium and plastics are not compatible with standard electroplating.

**Q3: How thick is the electroplating layer?**

Thickness depends on the plating metal and the application. Zinc plating for corrosion protection builds to 5–25µm. Decorative chrome runs 0.25–0.5µm over a nickel undercoat. Hard chrome for wear surfaces reaches 12.5–125µm. Gold plating for electrical contacts typically runs 0.1–2.5µm. Copper plating as an undercoat runs 5–25µm. Each application is confirmed at quoting stage, and thickness is measured and documented per part on the inspection report.

**Q4: Does electroplating affect part dimensions?**

Yes — the plated layer adds thickness to all unmasked surfaces. Zinc at 5–25µm adds 5–25µm per face. Hard chrome at 25–125µm adds a significant amount that must be machined off or accounted for in the as-machined geometry. Specify your plating type and thickness at the quoting stage — the DFM review confirms which faces need masking and what machining allowance is required for faces where final dimension must be held.

**Q5: What plating options are available?**

TOP PROTO offers zinc, chrome (decorative and hard), gold, silver, and copper plating. Zinc is most common for corrosion protection on machined steel parts. Hard chrome is available for wear-resistant tooling and die surfaces. Gold and silver are available for electrical contacts and connector components. Copper is available as an undercoat or standalone plating. Chromate passivation — clear, yellow, or black — is available over zinc for additional corrosion resistance and colour.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local plating shop for my machined parts?**

A local plating shop applies the specified process — but the DFM review, masking confirmation, and post-plate dimensional verification typically do not happen unless you explicitly request them and coordinate between two suppliers. The DFM review at quoting stage covers masking locations, strike layer requirements for difficult substrates, machining allowances for plating thickness, and geometry feasibility for internal passages. You receive a single shipment with plating thickness records and a full inspection report.
