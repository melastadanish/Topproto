## What Is Electropolishing?

Electropolishing is an electrochemical polishing process. The part is connected as the anode in an electrolytic bath — typically a phosphoric and sulphuric acid mixture — and a direct current dissolves metal ions from the surface. Microscopic peaks dissolve faster than valleys because current density concentrates at high points. The result is a flattened, smoothed surface — Ra roughness improves by approximately 50% without abrasive contact.

The process removes 5–10µm of surface material uniformly across the part. That material removal is not selective; it applies to all exposed surfaces unless masked. For precision-machined features with tight tolerances, the dimensional change of 5–10µm per face must be accounted for in the machined geometry. Parts intended for electropolishing should be machined 5–10µm oversize on faces where final dimension matters.

Beyond surface smoothing, anodic dissolution removes embedded contaminants, free iron particles, and machining-induced surface stresses. The resulting surface is chemically clean, with a passive oxide layer reformed in the acid bath — which is why electropolished stainless steel shows improved corrosion resistance and is accepted in medical device and food contact applications requiring biocompatibility and cleanability.

## Process Steps

1. **Part review and masking specification** — Features requiring dimensional preservation are identified. Parts intended for electropolishing should be machined 5–10µm oversize on critical tolerance faces.
2. **Pre-cleaning and degreasing** — Parts are alkaline-cleaned to remove machining oils, cutting fluid residue, and surface contaminants that would cause uneven dissolution.
3. **Fixturing and immersion** — Parts are fixtured to ensure electrical contact and immersed in the phosphoric and sulphuric acid electrolytic bath.
4. **Anodic dissolution** — Direct current is applied. Microscopic surface peaks dissolve faster than valleys — Ra measurement improves as the surface flattens uniformly.
5. **Water rinse and neutralisation** — Parts are rinsed thoroughly and neutralised to halt the electrochemical reaction. The passive oxide layer reforms during this stage.
6. **Inspection** — Ra is measured by contact profilometer. Dimensional checks confirm material removal is within the 5–10µm specification. Visual inspection confirms surface uniformity.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is electropolishing and what does it do to a stainless steel part?**

Electropolishing is an electrochemical polishing process where the stainless steel part acts as the anode in an acid electrolytic bath. Direct current dissolves metal ions from the surface, with microscopic peaks dissolving faster than valleys because current concentrates at high points. The result is a measurably smoother surface — Ra roughness improves by approximately 50%, so a part at Ra 1.6µm as machined reaches approximately Ra 0.8µm after electropolishing. The process also removes embedded free iron particles, reforms the passive oxide layer, and produces a clean surface suited to medical, food contact, and semiconductor applications.

**Q2: What materials are compatible with electropolishing?**

Stainless steel is the primary substrate for electropolishing — grades 303, 304, 316L, and 17-4PH all process well. Grade 316L is preferred for medical and food contact applications where biocompatibility documentation is required. Aluminium can be electropolished using specialised bath chemistry with variable results — confirm at quoting stage. Mild steel, titanium, and copper-based alloys are not compatible with the standard phosphoric and sulphuric acid process.

**Q3: How much material does electropolishing remove?**

The process removes 5–10µm of surface material per exposed face. That removal is uniform and applies to all surfaces in contact with the electrolyte. Precision-machined features — bores, threads, mating faces — should be machined 5–10µm oversize when electropolishing is specified, to preserve final dimensions after the process. The DFM review at quoting stage confirms which faces require machining allowance.

**Q4: Does electropolishing affect part dimensions?**

Yes — 5–10µm is removed from each exposed face. For most non-precision surfaces, that change is insignificant. For close-tolerance bores, journal surfaces, and thread flanks, the removal must be accounted for in machined geometry. Specify electropolishing at the quoting stage — not after machining — so the DFM review can confirm allowances before the part is cut.

**Q5: What surface finish improvement can I expect?**

Ra roughness improves by approximately 50% — a part machined to Ra 1.6µm reaches approximately Ra 0.8µm after electropolishing. Starting roughness affects the result: very rough surfaces (Ra >3.2µm) show proportional improvement but may not reach Ra 0.8µm in a single electropolishing cycle. Electropolishing cannot correct deep tool marks or machining defects that mechanical polishing would require abrasives to address. The improvement is consistent across internal surfaces and passages that abrasive polishing cannot reach.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local electropolishing shop for my machined parts?**

A local electropolishing shop processes parts without knowledge of the machined geometry or tolerance requirements. The 5–10µm removal is applied without a prior review of which faces have tight tolerances, which passages are blind, or how the part was machined. TOP PROTO reviews these details at quoting stage — the DFM covers machining allowance for electropolishing, identifies features that need masking, and confirms grade compatibility before the part goes into production.
