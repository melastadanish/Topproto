## What Is Passivation?

Passivation is a chemical treatment that removes free iron and surface contamination from stainless steel and titanium parts, then allows the native passive oxide layer — chromium oxide on stainless steel — to reform fully on the cleaned surface. Machining, grinding, and handling all embed free iron particles into the surface. Left untreated, those particles corrode and create rust spots, even on stainless steel. Passivation eliminates them.

The process uses either citric acid or nitric acid bath chemistry. Citric acid passivation is now the more common choice — lower toxicity, equivalent performance to nitric acid bath treatment, and compliance with both ASTM A967 and AMS 2700. Nitric acid bath passivation remains available for specifications that require it. TOP PROTO applies both at its Dongguan, China facility.

### What Passivation Does and Does Not Do

Passivation restores the native passive oxide layer — the thin chromium-rich film that gives stainless steel its corrosion resistance. This layer is damaged by machining and handling. Passivation cleans the surface and allows that layer to reform fully. It does not plate, coat, or add any material to the surface. That is why dimensional change is zero — passivation is a cleaning and surface restoration process, not a deposition process.

Passivation does not remove heavy surface contamination such as heat tint from welding, deep corrosion, or scale. Those require mechanical or electrochemical pre-treatment before passivation. Call out any heat-affected zones or surface conditions on your drawing at quoting stage.

## Passivation Process Steps

**Step 1 — Pre-cleaning and degreasing:** Parts are cleaned with alkaline degreaser to remove machining oil, coolant, and handling contamination. This stage is critical — any residual oil prevents acid contact with the metal surface and causes incomplete passivation.

**Step 2 — Masking (where required):** Features that must not contact the acid bath — bonded inserts, sealed assemblies, or co-processed components of incompatible materials — are masked before treatment.

**Step 3 — Acid bath immersion:** Parts are immersed in citric acid or nitric acid bath at the specified concentration and temperature. Citric acid passivation runs at approximately 20–65°C; nitric acid bath at approximately 20–55°C. Dwell time depends on the stainless steel grade and the specification being met.

**Step 4 — Rinse:** Parts are rinsed thoroughly in deionised water to remove all acid residue. Incomplete rinsing leaves acid trapped in crevices, which causes corrosion rather than preventing it.

**Step 5 — Drying and passive layer formation:** Parts are dried and held in air for the passive oxide layer to reform on the cleaned surface. Chromium in the steel reacts with atmospheric oxygen to form the protective chromium oxide film.

**Step 6 — Verification testing:** Salt spray resistance testing or copper sulphate test confirms free iron removal and passive layer integrity. Results logged per batch.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is passivation and what does it do to stainless steel?**

Passivation is a chemical treatment that removes free iron particles embedded in the stainless steel surface during machining, grinding, and handling, then allows the native passive oxide layer — chromium oxide — to reform fully on the cleaned surface. Free iron causes rust spots on stainless steel even though the bulk alloy is corrosion-resistant. Passivation eliminates those particles using citric acid or nitric acid bath chemistry per ASTM A967 or AMS 2700. The result is a stainless steel surface with full corrosion resistance restored, without any coating applied or any material removed.

**Q2: What materials are compatible with passivation?**

Passivation applies to stainless steel alloys and titanium. At TOP PROTO the most common grades processed are stainless steel 304, 316L, 17-4PH, and Grade 303, plus titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5. Mild steel and carbon steel have no passive layer to restore and are not suitable. Aluminium, copper, brass, and plastics are not applicable — those materials require different surface treatments. If your assembly mixes stainless steel with aluminium, passivation for the stainless components and anodizing for the aluminium can be coordinated on the same order.

**Q3: How does passivation affect part dimensions?**

Passivation produces zero dimensional change. No material is added to the surface, and the chemical removal of free iron particles is at the nanometre level — below any measurable dimensional effect. This makes passivation the correct choice for precision stainless steel parts where thread engagement, bore fits, and sealing faces must hold their machined dimensions. If dimensional verification is required, a pre- and post-passivation inspection report is available on request — specify at quoting stage.

**Q4: Does passivation change the surface appearance or roughness of a part?**

Passivation does not visually alter a machined stainless steel surface or change its Ra value. The surface looks the same before and after. If you want a matte surface before passivation, bead blasting can be applied first. If you want a directional brushed grain, that is applied before passivation. Passivation is always the final chemical step — applied after any mechanical finishing, never before.

**Q5: What is the difference between ASTM A967 and AMS 2700?**

Both ASTM A967 and AMS 2700 are passivation specifications for stainless steel. ASTM A967 is the broader general industry standard, covering citric acid and nitric acid bath methods with acceptance testing requirements. AMS 2700 is the aerospace-sector passivation specification, also covering both acid types with tighter process documentation requirements. If your drawing calls out a specific standard, specify it at quoting stage — TOP PROTO processes to both. If no standard is called out, ASTM A967 is applied by default.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local finishing house for passivation?**

A local finishing house handles passivation as a standalone process — your machined parts travel to them, get treated, and return. Every handoff adds risk of surface contamination, re-introduction of free iron from handling, and schedule delay. TOP PROTO passivates at the same Dongguan facility that machines the parts, maintaining surface integrity from machining through to final treatment. Mixed-material orders — stainless steel passivation alongside aluminium anodizing — are handled on the same order without splitting across suppliers. DFM review at quoting catches geometry issues before machining, not after the part arrives at a finishing house.
