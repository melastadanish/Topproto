## What Is Powder Coating?

Powder coating is a dry finishing process. Electrostatically charged powder particles — a blend of resin, pigment, and curing agent — adhere to a grounded metal substrate before passing through a cure oven at 160–200°C. The heat fuses and cross-links the powder into a continuous film 60–120µm thick. No solvents are involved, so the coating cures without running or dripping, and layer thickness stays consistent across flat faces and edges.

The resulting film is harder and more impact-resistant than liquid paint at comparable thickness. Adhesion relies on surface preparation — adhesion primer and media blasting or chemical pre-treatment removes mill scale, machining oils, and oxide layers before the powder is applied. Poor pre-treatment is the primary cause of adhesion failure, which is why substrate preparation and coating application must be controlled in the same workflow.

Powder coating adds 60–120µm to each coated face. For precision-machined features — threaded holes, bearing bores, close-tolerance faces — masking before coating is standard practice. Specify masked features at the quoting stage so clearances are held to your drawing.

## Powder Coating vs Liquid Paint — Key Differences

| | Powder Coating | Liquid Paint |
|---|---|---|
| Application method | Electrostatic spray (dry powder) | Brush, roller, or spray gun (wet) |
| Coating thickness | 60–120µm | 25–75µm (single coat) |
| Solvent use | None | Solvent carrier required |
| Impact resistance | High — flexible cross-linked film | Lower — more prone to chipping |
| Edge coverage | Good — powder wraps edges | Poor — liquid sags at edges |
| Colour options | Full RAL range | Full RAL + Pantone |
| Rework | Strip and re-coat | Sand and repaint |
| Best for | Steel and aluminium structural parts | Multi-substrate or very thin parts |

## Process Steps

1. **Part receipt and drawing review** — Masked features and dimensional requirements are confirmed against your drawing before any surface preparation begins.
2. **Pre-treatment** — Parts are media blasted or chemically cleaned to remove machining oils, oxide layers, and mill scale. Adhesion primer is applied where the substrate requires it.
3. **Masking** — Threaded holes, bearing seats, and close-tolerance surfaces are masked to the dimensions specified at quoting stage.
4. **Electrostatic spray** — Charged powder particles are applied to the grounded part. Coating thickness builds uniformly across flat faces and internal radii.
5. **Oven cure** — Parts cure at 160–200°C for 10–20 minutes. The powder cross-links into a hard, continuous film.
6. **Inspection** — Coating thickness is measured by dry film gauge (DFT). Adhesion, colour, and appearance are checked against your specification before shipping.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is powder coating and what does it do to a metal part?**

Powder coating is a dry finishing process where electrostatically charged powder — resin, pigment, and curing agent — adheres to a grounded metal part and cures in an oven at 160–200°C. The heat cross-links the particles into a continuous film 60–120µm thick. You get a hard, impact-resistant surface with consistent colour across the full part and corrosion protection that liquid paint at the same thickness cannot match. No solvents means no drips, no runs, and no solvent residue on the finished part.

**Q2: What materials are compatible with powder coating?**

Steel and aluminium are the primary substrates — both accept powder coating well with correct pre-treatment and adhesion primer. Aluminium grades 6061 and 7075 are both compatible. Stainless steel is possible with mechanical surface roughening, but adhesion is less reliable — confirm per part at quoting stage. Titanium and plastics are not compatible: titanium has adhesion problems at cure temperature, and most engineering plastics cannot survive 160–200°C.

**Q3: How thick is the powder coating layer?**

Standard coating on machined steel and aluminium builds to 60–120µm. The target within that range depends on the application — corrosion-heavy environments typically specify 80–100µm, while cosmetic parts may use 60–80µm. Coating thickness is uniform across flat faces. Edges and internal radii may vary by ±10µm. Every part is measured by calibrated dry film gauge and the reading is recorded on the inspection report.

**Q4: Does powder coating affect part dimensions?**

Each coated face gains 60–120µm. For most structural parts, that addition is insignificant. For precision-machined features — threaded bores, bearing seats, press-fit surfaces — masking before coating is required. Specify which features need masking at the quoting stage. The DFM review confirms masking locations before production starts, so there are no dimensional surprises after coating.

**Q5: What colour and finish options are available?**

TOP PROTO's powder coating covers the full RAL colour standard in three finish types: matte, gloss, and satin. Any RAL code can be specified. Custom colour matching against a Pantone reference or a supplied physical sample is available on request — lead time may extend by 1–2 days for colour-matched batches. Textured finishes such as fine texture and hammer tone are available — confirm at quoting stage.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local powder coating shop for my machined parts?**

A local coating shop receives your part with no knowledge of its critical features. Masking decisions are made by the operator — not by a DFM engineer reviewing your drawing. TOP PROTO applies powder coating at the same facility where your part was machined, so the DFM review at quoting stage confirms masked features, dimensional allowances, and surface prep requirements before the part is touched. You get one accountable supplier for machining and finishing — not two separate shops to coordinate between.
