## What Is Nickel Plating?

Nickel plating deposits a thin layer of nickel metal onto a machined part to improve surface hardness, wear resistance, and corrosion protection. Two distinct processes are available: electroless nickel plating and electrolytic nickel plating. Each uses different bath chemistry and produces different coating characteristics — the right choice depends on part geometry and the properties your application requires.

### Electroless Nickel Plating — Uniform Thickness on Complex Geometry

Electroless nickel — also called EN plating — deposits nickel without electrical current. The bath chemistry drives the reaction, which means coating thickness builds uniformly across the entire part surface regardless of geometry. Inside bores, blind holes, undercuts, and complex external profiles all receive the same thickness. This is the critical advantage of electroless nickel: thickness uniformity on parts that electrolytic plating cannot reach evenly.

Electroless nickel also contains phosphorus from the bath chemistry. Phosphorus content — typically 5–12% by weight — directly controls hardness and corrosion resistance. High-phosphorus electroless nickel (10–12%) gives the best corrosion resistance. Low-phosphorus (2–5%) achieves higher as-deposited hardness. Heat treatment after plating to approximately 400°C increases hardness further, reaching HV 500 — comparable to hard chrome.

### Electrolytic Nickel Plating — Ductility and Conductivity

Electrolytic nickel plating uses direct current to drive deposition. The coating is bright, ductile, and has excellent electrical conductivity. Thickness is less uniform than electroless on complex geometry — features shielded from the anode receive thinner deposits. For flat surfaces, flanges, and simple geometries this is not a concern. Electrolytic nickel is often applied as a decorative or functional base coat before chrome or gold plating.

## Nickel Plating Process Steps

**Step 1 — Surface inspection and substrate prep:** Parts are inspected for surface defects, burrs, and machining marks. Substrate prep determines adhesion quality — surfaces must be clean, consistent, and free of heat-affected zones before plating begins.

**Step 2 — Degreasing and cleaning:** Alkaline degreaser removes machining oil, coolant, and handling contamination. Electroless nickel plating is particularly sensitive to substrate cleanliness — any residue prevents the autocatalytic reaction from starting correctly.

**Step 3 — Activation (substrate-dependent):** Aluminium parts receive zincate pre-treatment to prepare the surface for nickel adhesion. Stainless steel receives an activation etch to open the passive oxide layer. Steel proceeds directly after cleaning.

**Step 4 — Masking:** Threads, sealing faces, press-fit bores, and electrical contact surfaces that must remain uncoated are masked before bath entry. Specify masked features on your drawing or in the quoting notes.

**Step 5 — Plating bath:** Parts are immersed in the electroless or electrolytic nickel bath. Bath temperature, pH, and chemical concentrations control deposition rate and phosphorus content. Dwell time controls coating thickness.

**Step 6 — Rinse, dry, and inspection:** Parts are rinsed in deionised water, dried, and inspected. Coating thickness is measured using magnetic induction or eddy-current gauge depending on substrate. Adhesion is confirmed by cross-hatch test. Dimensional check on critical features confirms post-plate dimensions.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is nickel plating and what does it do to a machined part?**

Nickel plating deposits a metallic nickel layer onto a CNC machined part to improve surface hardness, wear resistance, and corrosion protection. Two processes are available: electroless nickel, which uses bath chemistry to deposit uniformly across all geometry, and electrolytic nickel, which uses direct current and deposits most thickly on line-of-sight surfaces. Coating thickness runs 5–25µm. Electroless nickel with high phosphorus content (10–12%) gives the best corrosion resistance and, after heat treatment at 400°C, reaches hardness up to HV 500 — comparable to hard chrome.

**Q2: What materials are compatible with nickel plating?**

Nickel plating is compatible with steel, stainless steel, aluminium, copper, and brass. Steel is the standard substrate with excellent adhesion. Aluminium requires zincate pre-treatment to build adhesion between the aluminium surface and the nickel layer. Stainless steel requires an activation etch to open the passive oxide film before plating. Copper and brass plate well with minimal pre-treatment. Titanium is possible but requires specialised pre-treatment — confirm at quoting stage. Plastics are not compatible with standard electroless or electrolytic nickel plating.

**Q3: How thick is the nickel plating coating?**

Coating thickness runs 5–25µm for both electroless and electrolytic nickel at TOP PROTO. The key difference between the two processes is thickness uniformity — electroless nickel builds to ±1–2µm across all surfaces including internal bores and undercuts. Electrolytic nickel builds thicker on exposed surfaces and thinner on shielded geometry, with variation of ±5–10µm depending on part complexity. For precision parts, specify the post-plate dimension on your drawing. TOP PROTO will machine to the pre-plate dimension that hits your target after the coating builds.

**Q4: Does nickel plating affect part dimensions?**

Yes — nickel plating adds material to the surface, so dimensions change by the coating thickness. A 10µm coating adds 10µm to each plated surface — 20µm on a bore diameter or shaft diameter. For non-critical surfaces this is typically negligible. For precision bores, shafts, and mating faces, specify the post-plate finished dimension on your drawing. Masking prevents coating on threads and sealing faces where dimensional interference would affect function. Specify masked features at quoting stage.

**Q5: What is the difference between electroless and electrolytic nickel plating?**

Electroless nickel uses bath chemistry — an autocatalytic reaction — to deposit nickel without electrical current. This produces uniform coating thickness across all part geometry regardless of complexity, making it the right choice for internal bores, complex 3D profiles, and precision features. Electrolytic nickel uses direct current, which deposits thicker on surfaces facing the anode and thinner on shielded features. Electrolytic nickel is brighter and more ductile, and is often used as a decorative base coat or for simple flat geometry. Electroless nickel is harder and more corrosion-resistant, particularly at high phosphorus content.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local nickel plating shop?**

A local plating shop handles finishing as a standalone service — your machined parts travel to them, get plated, and return. Every transfer risks surface contamination between machining and plating, which directly affects adhesion. TOP PROTO machines and plates at the same Dongguan facility, maintaining controlled surface conditions from cutting to coating. Substrate-specific pre-treatment for aluminium, stainless, and steel is handled correctly per material. DFM review at quoting identifies geometry that needs masking or dimensional compensation before machining — a standalone plating shop has no input on part design decisions.
