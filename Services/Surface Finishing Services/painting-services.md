## What Is Industrial Painting for Metal Parts?

Industrial painting on machined metal parts is a multi-step wet coating process. Surface preparation removes machining oils, oxides, and contaminants. A primer is applied first — primer adhesion is the bond between the substrate and the topcoat system, and it determines the long-term durability of the entire finish. The topcoat goes on over the cured primer in the specified colour, sheen level, and film thickness.

Dry film thickness (DFT) — the thickness of the fully cured paint film — is specified and measured per part. Primer runs 25–40µm. Topcoat adds another 40–80µm. Total system DFT typically falls in the 60–120µm range, similar to powder coating. Unlike powder coating, liquid paint can be applied to all metal substrates including stainless steel and complex curved surfaces where electrostatic spray would not wrap uniformly.

Colour matching is specified by RAL code or Pantone reference. A supplied physical sample or reference part can also be used for colour matching when an exact code is not available. Masking protects threaded holes, bearing bores, and close-tolerance surfaces — specify masked features at the quoting stage so the DFM review confirms clearances before production starts.

## Process Steps

1. **Part review and masking specification** — Drawing review confirms masked features, colour specification, DFT target, and whether the substrate requires a specialised primer.
2. **Surface preparation** — Parts are degreased and either mechanically blasted or chemically etched to remove oxides, machining oils, and surface contamination. Surface preparation quality determines primer adhesion.
3. **Masking** — Threaded bores, bearing surfaces, and close-tolerance features are masked per the drawing review. Masking material is selected to withstand the primer and topcoat cure process.
4. **Primer application** — Primer is sprayed to 25–40µm DFT and allowed to cure. Primer creates the adhesion bond between substrate and topcoat — this layer must be correct before topcoat goes on.
5. **Topcoat application** — Topcoat is sprayed in the specified colour and sheen to 40–80µm DFT over the cured primer. Multiple coats may be required for deep or saturated colours.
6. **Inspection** — DFT is measured at multiple points per part by film gauge. Colour is checked against RAL swatch or Pantone chip. Adhesion is tested by cross-cut per ISO 2409. Dimensional checks on masked features complete the inspection.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is industrial painting for metal parts and how does it differ from spray paint?**

Industrial painting on machined metal parts is a two-stage process: primer followed by topcoat. The primer creates a chemical and mechanical bond between the metal substrate and the topcoat — without it, the topcoat delaminates under service conditions. Spray paint skips the primer stage or uses a combined one-coat product with limited adhesion and durability. Industrial primer and topcoat systems are applied to specified dry film thickness, tested for adhesion by cross-cut per ISO 2409, and colour-matched to RAL or Pantone standard. The result is a finish with documented performance — not a cosmetic coat.

**Q2: What materials can be painted?**

All common machined metals accept painting with the correct primer. Mild steel, alloy steel, aluminium, stainless steel, copper, brass, and titanium are all compatible. Aluminium requires an etch primer for adhesion. Stainless requires an adhesion primer, ideally over a mechanically roughened surface. Mild steel accepts standard epoxy primer with good results after blast or chemical pre-treatment. Cast iron is compatible but requires thorough surface prep to prevent rust bleed-through. Some engineering plastics can be painted with an adhesion promoter — confirm per material at quoting stage.

**Q3: How thick is the painted coating?**

A standard primer and topcoat system applies 25–40µm primer and 40–80µm topcoat, for a total system DFT of 60–120µm. Total DFT depends on the specified coating system, the colour (deep or saturated colours may require additional topcoat passes), and the application environment. Every part is measured by film gauge and the DFT is recorded per part on the inspection report.

**Q4: Does painting affect part dimensions?**

Each painted face gains the total system DFT — typically 60–120µm. For most structural and cosmetic parts, that addition is not dimensionally significant. For close-tolerance features — threaded bores, bearing seats, locating surfaces — masking before painting is required. Specify masked features at the quoting stage so the DFM review confirms clearances and the correct masking approach before production starts.

**Q5: What colour options are available?**

TOP PROTO matches to the full RAL colour standard and to Pantone references. Any RAL code in matte, satin, or gloss finish can be specified. Pantone-matched colours are available for brand-critical specifications. Physical sample matching — where you supply a reference part or colour chip — is also available. For colour-matched batches, lead time may extend by 1–2 days to confirm the match before full production begins.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local paint shop for my machined parts?**

A local paint shop applies primer and topcoat — but the substrate-specific primer selection, masking confirmation, and post-paint dimensional verification are not typically part of the service unless you specify and chase each point separately. TOP PROTO reviews your part drawing at the quoting stage, confirms the correct primer for your substrate, identifies masked features, and documents DFT per part. Your machined part goes from the cutting tool to the paint booth to your delivery address without leaving the facility on standard orders. You receive one inspection report covering machining and finishing.
