## What Is Brushed Finish?

A brushed finish is a mechanical surface treatment that applies a consistent directional grain to a metal surface using an abrasive belt or abrasive pad run in one direction. The abrasive removes surface irregularities — including milling tool marks, burr remnants, and surface oxidation — while leaving a fine parallel line pattern known as the grain direction. Ra measurement after brushing runs 0.8–1.6µm depending on grit sequence used.

The process is cosmetic-first. Where bead blasting produces a non-directional matte texture, brushed finish produces a controlled linear grain — the kind seen on consumer electronics, aerospace interior panels, and precision instrument housings. ### Grit Sequence and Ra Control

Ra — arithmetical mean roughness — describes how far the surface texture deviates from its mean line on average. A standard CNC milled surface sits at Ra 3.2µm with directional tool marks. Brushed finish at Ra 0.8–1.6µm produces a finer, more refined texture using progressively finer abrasive grit. The grit sequence — for example, 180 grit followed by 320 grit — controls final Ra. Coarser starting grits remove deeper machining marks; finer final grits bring the Ra down and define the grain appearance. Specify your Ra target at quoting if the finish tolerance matters for your application.

### Grain Direction — Functional and Cosmetic Considerations

Grain direction is not only a cosmetic choice. On stainless steel parts used in food, pharmaceutical, or medical environments, grain direction affects cleanability — longitudinal grain runs in the direction that a cleaning wipe travels, reducing particle trapping. On aluminium panels and housings, grain direction is typically specified relative to the long axis of the part for visual consistency. Critical features — sealing faces, threaded holes, precision bores — are masked so the abrasive does not reach them.

## Brushed Finish Process Steps

**Step 1 — Part inspection:** Parts are checked for burrs, deep tool marks, and surface defects before brushing begins. Deep gouges that exceed the abrasive's material removal depth are flagged — brushing is a finishing process, not a repair process.

**Step 2 — Masking:** Threads, precision bores, sealing faces, and any feature where Ra must remain at the machined value are masked using tape or plugs. Masking critical features is standard — call them out on your drawing or at quoting stage.

**Step 3 — Abrasive belt selection:** Grit sequence is selected based on the target Ra and the as-machined surface condition. Coarser grit removes machining marks; the final grit defines the grain fineness and Ra value. Aluminium and stainless steel use separate abrasive belts — preventing metal transfer between materials.

**Step 4 — Brushing:** The abrasive belt or pad is run in one controlled direction across the part surface. Grain direction is maintained consistently — all passes run parallel. Flat faces, edges, and accessible curved surfaces are covered in the same direction.

**Step 5 — Ra measurement:** Surface roughness is measured using a contact profilometer along and across the grain direction. Ra across the grain (perpendicular to grain direction) is higher than Ra along the grain — specify which orientation matters for your application.

**Step 6 — Inspection and secondary process:** Visual inspection checks grain consistency, absence of cross-hatching, and masking seal integrity. If anodizing or passivation follows, parts proceed directly to the next process at the Dongguan facility without additional handling.

## FAQ

**Q1: What is a brushed finish and what does it do to a machined part?**

A brushed finish is a mechanical surface treatment that uses an abrasive belt or pad — run in one controlled direction — to produce a consistent linear grain on a metal surface. The process removes machining tool marks, milling lines, and surface irregularities, replacing them with a fine parallel grain at Ra 0.8–1.6µm. The result is a satin-matte linear texture that looks consistent across the entire part, rather than showing the directional arc and feed marks left by CNC milling. Brushed finish is a cosmetic-first process — it improves surface appearance and slightly reduces Ra, without adding or removing measurable material from the part.

**Q2: What materials can receive a brushed finish?**

Brushed finish at TOP PROTO is available for CNC machined aluminium (6061, 7075, 5052) and stainless steel (304, 316L). Aluminium brushes consistently with a clean grain definition. Stainless steel brushes well and is common in medical and food-contact applications where grain direction affects cleanability. Mild steel is not recommended — it oxidises after brushing without a secondary coating, which re-contaminates the surface. Titanium, copper, brass, and plastics are not offered for brushed finish at TOP PROTO. If your material is not on this list, confirm compatibility at quoting stage.

**Q3: What Ra surface finish does brushed finish achieve?**

Brushed finish produces Ra 0.8–1.6µm depending on the grit sequence applied. A 320-grit final pass produces Ra closer to 0.8µm. A coarser final grit produces Ra closer to 1.6µm. For comparison, standard as-machined CNC milling produces Ra 3.2µm with directional tool marks — brushed finish at Ra 0.8–1.6µm is finer and non-directional in appearance relative to milling patterns. Specify your Ra target at quoting if the value matters dimensionally or functionally. Ra is measured along and across the grain direction — specify which orientation applies to your drawing callout.

**Q4: Does brushed finish affect part dimensions?**

Dimensional change from brushed finish is sub-micron — negligible on standard machined wall thicknesses and surface features. The abrasive removes only the peaks of surface roughness. Threads, precision bores, sealing faces, and tight-tolerance features are masked before brushing to prevent the abrasive from reaching them. Specify masked features on your drawing. If any post-brush Ra measurement is required as part of your inspection record, it is available on request — specify at quoting stage.

**Q5: Can brushed finish be combined with anodizing?**

Yes — and the combination is one of the most common multi-step finishes at TOP PROTO. Brushed grain direction is visible through clear or coloured anodize on aluminium. The sequence is: machine → brush → anodize. Bead blasting before anodizing produces a matte, non-directional texture; brushing before anodizing produces a linear grain that shows through the anodize layer. Both are available. If you want the grain visible in the final part, specify brush + anodize on your order. Both steps are applied at the Dongguan facility on the same order — no inter-supplier handling.

**Q6: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local finishing shop for brushed finish?**

A local finishing shop handles brushed finish as a standalone service — your machined parts travel to them, get brushed, and return. Every handoff introduces handling risk, particularly relevant between brushing and a secondary process like anodizing. TOP PROTO coordinates brushing and anodizing (or passivation) on the same order at the Dongguan facility. Grain direction is specified and confirmed on your drawing — not left to operator interpretation at a separate shop. Ra is measured and reported. Dedicated abrasive media for aluminium and stainless steel prevents cross-contamination that shared-media shops may not control.
