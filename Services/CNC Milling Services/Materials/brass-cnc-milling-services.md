## Section 2: Why Brass for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Brass Is the Highest Machinability-Rated Copper Alloy

Brass (copper-zinc alloy) is rated at 100% machinability on the standard machinability index — the reference point against which other metals are measured. C360 free-machining brass achieves this rating because lead additions (2–3% Pb in C360) act as internal chip breakers: as the cutter shears the metal, the lead causes chips to break into short segments rather than forming long strings. Short, brittle chips evacuate the cut cleanly, reduce cutting forces, allow higher feed rates, and produce an excellent as-milled surface finish without burring.

The practical consequence of brass's machinability is speed and surface quality. CNC mills run brass at high feed rates with minimal tool pressure — tool life on brass parts is long, cycle times are short, and the machined surface finish (Ra 0.8 µm as-milled with a fine finishing pass) often eliminates the need for post-machining polishing or buffing. For parts requiring a decorative metal finish — instrument hardware, fittings, valve bodies — brass provides the surface quality at the cutting stage rather than requiring a separate polishing operation.

Brass versus aluminium is the key choice for non-structural milled parts. Aluminium has a higher strength-to-weight ratio and lower cost per kilogram. Brass has better corrosion resistance in humid and mildly corrosive environments (without coating), higher density (8.5 g/cm³ vs 2.7 g/cm³ for aluminium) which is an advantage in counterweights and balance components, better electromagnetic shielding, and an inherent antimicrobial surface property (copper ion release kills bacteria). For connectors, fittings, valves, and decorative hardware, brass is the standard specification. For structural and lightweight applications, aluminium is correct.

Brass accepts electroplating — chrome, nickel, gold — without the pre-treatment steps required for aluminium and stainless steel. Brass's copper content makes it compatible with standard electroplating baths directly. This makes CNC milled brass the standard substrate for decorative plated hardware, contact components, and high-end instrument fittings.

## Section 3: Brass Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Brass Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Composition | Tensile Strength | Key Properties | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C360 (Free-Machining) | Cu 60%, Zn 38%, Pb 2–3% | 385 MPa | Highest machinability (100%), excellent surface finish | Precision parts, fittings, valves, connectors |
| C27400 (Yellow Brass) | Cu 63%, Zn 37% | 340 MPa | Good corrosion resistance, no lead | Food contact, water fittings, plumbing |
| C26000 (Cartridge Brass) | Cu 70%, Zn 30% | 340–510 MPa | High ductility, excellent for cold forming | Structural, drawn parts |

**Machinability notes:** C360 is the standard milling grade — lead provides chip-breaking that keeps the cut clean and the tool sharp. C27400 (unleaded brass) has similar properties but slightly lower machinability — chips are longer and require more attention to chip clearance. For food, water, and beverage contact applications where RoHS/REACH lead restrictions apply, C27400 or another lead-free grade must be specified — confirm regulatory requirements at quote stage. Cutting fluid (soluble oil or cutting oil) is recommended for brass to prevent built-up edge on copper-rich surfaces.

## Section 5: Brass CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Brass CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Connectors and Terminals** — Electrical connectors, terminal blocks, and contact pins. Brass's electrical conductivity (28% IACS for C360) and direct plating compatibility make it the standard base metal for electronic connector bodies and contacts. Gold plating over brass connector pins is the standard electronics contact specification.

**Valves and Fluid Fittings** — Valve bodies, manifold fittings, and fluid control components. Brass's corrosion resistance in water and mild chemical environments, combined with high machinability for the complex internal geometry of valves, makes it the standard material for plumbing and instrumentation fittings. Specify C27400 (lead-free) for potable water contact.

**Precision Instrument Hardware** — Knobs, bezels, locking rings, and decorative hardware for precision instruments, cameras, and optical equipment. Brass's surface finish quality (Ra 0.8 µm as-milled) and polish-ability make it the standard decorative hardware material. Nickel or chrome plating over milled brass is the standard for high-end instrument hardware.

**Musical Instrument Components** — Structural and acoustic components where brass's density, resonance properties, and aesthetic are the specification. CNC milled brass valve casings, mouthpiece blanks, and mechanical linkages for brass instrument production and custom instrument components.

**Counterweights and Balancing Components** — Brass's high density (8.5 g/cm³) makes it the standard material for counterweights in optical systems, precision balances, and mechanical assemblies where mass in a small volume is the requirement. CNC milling allows precise volume control for accurate mass targets.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Brass CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling brass?**
Standard brass milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm (ISO 2768-m). Precision features with stable fixturing achieve ±0.005 mm. Brass's high machinability (C360 rated at 100%) and chip-breaking lead content produce clean cuts without the tool deflection that limits tolerance on harder metals. Brass's CTE is 19 µm/m·°C — for tolerances tighter than ±0.005 mm, inspect at 20°C (standard ISO reference temperature). Precision connector and fitting dimensions should specify post-plate dimensions if plating is part of the specification — nickel plating adds 5–25 µm per surface.

**Q2: Which brass grades do you mill?**
C360 free-machining brass is the standard milling grade — 2–3% lead provides chip-breaking and 100% machinability rating, 385 MPa tensile strength, excellent surface finish. C27400 is the lead-free alternative for food contact, potable water, and RoHS/REACH regulated applications — similar mechanical properties to C360 with slightly lower machinability. C26000 (cartridge brass, 70% copper) provides higher ductility and better corrosion resistance for structural applications. Confirm grade at quote stage — C360 is held in stock, other grades are ordered per project.

**Q3: Is C360 brass RoHS compliant for electronic components?**
C360 contains 2–3% lead, which is a restricted substance under RoHS Directive 2011/65/EU. RoHS restricts lead in electrical and electronic equipment above 0.1% by weight in homogeneous materials. C360 brass connector bodies and housings that contact electronic assemblies may be in scope for RoHS — check the specific product category and exemption status. Exemptions exist for lead in brass alloys for electrical connectors (RoHS Annex III, Exemption 6c — lead in brass for electrical purposes, up to 4% by weight). For applications where RoHS compliance is required without exemption, specify C27400 (lead-free brass). Confirm regulatory requirements at quote stage — the engineering team will advise on grade selection.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled brass parts?**
Brass accepts a wide range of surface finishes. As-milled (Ra 1.6 µm standard, Ra 0.8 µm with fine finish), mechanical polishing (Ra <0.1 µm mirror finish), nickel plating (direct adhesion, no pre-treatment), chrome plating (decorative, plumbing hardware standard), and gold plating (electronics contact surfaces). Brass's copper content provides direct adhesion for electroplating — no zincate pre-treatment required as with aluminium. Anodizing and passivation are not applicable to brass. Powder coating is possible but uncommon for brass — confirm adhesion at quote stage.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled brass parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any brass order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for brass CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For threaded fittings and precision connectors, provide a 2D drawing with explicit thread class, tolerance callouts, and plating thickness allowances on critical dimensions.

**Q7: How does brass milling cost compare to other metals?**
Brass CNC milling cost is between aluminium and stainless steel in most cases. Brass raw material cost is higher than aluminium per kilogram, but machinability is similar — cutting times are comparable to aluminium for equivalent geometry. Brass tool life is excellent due to C360's chip-breaking lead content. Compared to stainless steel, brass machines significantly faster (lower cutting forces, higher feed rates) which reduces cycle time cost. For precision fittings, connectors, and hardware where brass is the specified material, the total cost (material + machining + finishing) is typically competitive with aluminium for complex parts, and significantly lower than stainless steel.

**Q8: When should I choose brass milling over copper milling?**
Brass (C360) and copper (C101/C110) are both copper alloys, but with different properties. Copper provides higher electrical conductivity (100% IACS for C101 vs 28% IACS for C360 brass) and higher thermal conductivity (391 W/m·K vs 120 W/m·K) — copper is specified when maximum electrical or thermal performance is the requirement (busbars, heat sinks, contacts). Brass provides higher strength (385 MPa vs 220–310 MPa for copper), better machinability (C360's lead content improves chip-breaking over pure copper), and better corrosion resistance in many environments. Choose brass for fittings, valves, connectors, and structural hardware where strength and machinability matter. Choose copper for busbars, heat sinks, and components where maximum electrical or thermal conductivity is the primary requirement.
