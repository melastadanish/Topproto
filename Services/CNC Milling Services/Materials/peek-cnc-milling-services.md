## Section 2: Why PEEK for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why PEEK Is the Specification When Standard Plastics Are Insufficient

PEEK (polyetheretherketone) is the specification for CNC milled plastic components that must perform under conditions that eliminate all other engineering plastics: sustained high temperature (continuous 250°C service, short-term 300°C), aggressive chemical environments (resistant to virtually all solvents, acids, and bases except concentrated sulphuric acid and some halogenated solvents), high mechanical load (100 MPa tensile, 170 MPa flexural strength), and radiation or biocompatibility requirements. When ABS, nylon, Delrin, and polycarbonate fail due to temperature or chemical attack, PEEK is the correct specification.

PEEK's semi-crystalline polymer structure explains most of its performance advantages. The aromatic backbone and ether/ketone linkages produce a polymer that is inherently rigid, thermally stable, and resistant to chemical attack. The degree of crystallinity (typically 30–35% for standard moulded PEEK, higher for annealed stock) determines stiffness and resistance to creep under load at elevated temperature. PEEK retains >50% of its room-temperature tensile strength at 200°C — no other common engineering plastic (except PTFE, which has much lower strength) approaches this performance.

PEEK is the only standard engineering plastic with inherent biocompatibility acceptable for implant-adjacent and short-term contact medical device applications. PEEK does not leach plasticisers, stabilisers, or catalysts — its chemical purity makes it acceptable for medical device components that contact tissue or body fluids. PEEK is used in spinal implant cages, orthopaedic instrument components, and fluid contact device parts where biocompatibility must be demonstrated. Confirm the specific biocompatibility standard required at quote stage — PEEK biocompatibility depends on grade, processing, and the applicable standard (ISO 10993).

PEEK's cost and machinability difficulty set it apart from commodity engineering plastics. PEEK raw material (rod and plate stock) costs 10–30× more per kilogram than ABS or nylon. PEEK is also harder to machine than unfilled engineering plastics — its high crystallinity and stiffness require sharp carbide tooling, controlled feed rates, and adequate chip clearance. These factors make PEEK milling more expensive than ABS, nylon, or Delrin milling. PEEK is specified when the application requires it — not as a general-purpose material.

## Section 3: PEEK Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** PEEK Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Tensile Strength | HDT (1.8 MPa) | Key Difference | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PEEK Unfilled | 100 MPa | 160°C | General-purpose; best chemical resistance | Chemical, biomedical, general high-temp |
| PEEK GF30 (glass-filled 30%) | 160 MPa | 300°C+ | Higher stiffness, higher HDT, more abrasive to tools | Structural high-temperature; load-bearing |
| PEEK CF30 (carbon-filled 30%) | 200 MPa | 300°C+ | Highest strength; electrically conductive | ESD, high-load structural; not for implant |
| PEEK Bearing Grade | 100 MPa | 160°C | PTFE/carbon filled; lower CoF | Bearing, bushing, sliding components |

**Machinability:** PEEK machines with carbide tooling — HSS tools dull quickly on PEEK's abrasive crystalline structure. Unfilled PEEK machines better than GF30 or CF30 grades; filled grades accelerate tool wear significantly. Coolant is recommended for PEEK — while PEEK can be dry-milled at low speeds, dry milling at production speeds generates heat that can cause surface delamination. Controlled feed rates and adequate chip clearance are required — PEEK chips are short and hard, and chip re-cutting damages surface finish. Pre-annealing PEEK stock before precision milling relieves internal stresses from extrusion and improves dimensional stability.

## Section 5: PEEK CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** PEEK CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Medical Device and Surgical Instrument Components** — PEEK spinal implant components (cage structures, interbody devices), orthopaedic instrument parts, and fluid path components. PEEK is one of the few plastics with demonstrated biocompatibility for medical implant-adjacent and instrument contact applications. PEEK's radiolucency (transparency to X-ray) makes it the standard material for spinal implants where post-operative imaging must show the bone healing rather than the implant.

**Semiconductor and Wafer Processing Equipment** — PEEK wafer carriers, process equipment components, and chemical bath fixtures. PEEK resists the process chemicals used in semiconductor fabrication (HF, H₂O₂, IPA, acetone, photoresist solvents) that attack most engineering plastics. PEEK's dimensional stability at process temperatures (up to 150°C) and cleanroom compatibility make it the standard plastic for wet process equipment.

**Aerospace and High-Temperature Structural Components** — Brackets, clips, and structural insert components for aerospace interiors and engine-adjacent applications. PEEK's continuous 250°C service and UL94 V-0 flammability rating (for filled grades) suit aerospace interior applications where FAR 25.853 flammability requirements must be met.

**Oil and Gas Downhole Components** — Seals, valve seats, and downhole tool components exposed to high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) environments with H₂S and CO₂ exposure. PEEK's resistance to H₂S-containing environments and thermal stability at downhole temperatures (up to 200°C+ depending on grade) make it a standard non-metallic material for HPHT downhole components.

**Chemical Processing and Laboratory Equipment** — Valve bodies, pump components, manifolds, and laboratory equipment parts exposed to aggressive solvents, acids, and bases. PEEK resists virtually all solvents and process chemicals except concentrated sulphuric acid — its chemical resistance in standard laboratory and process environments is comprehensive.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** PEEK CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling PEEK?**
Standard PEEK milling tolerance is ±0.05 mm. Precision features with annealed stock and carbide tooling achieve ±0.025 mm. PEEK's dimensional stability benefits from pre-annealing — rod and plate stock from extrusion contains residual stresses that relieve during machining and cause dimensional shift after the part is removed from the fixture. Pre-annealing at 200°C for 4 hours relieves these stresses and produces more stable dimensions after machining. For tolerance-critical PEEK parts, confirm the pre-annealing protocol at DFM stage.

**Q2: Which PEEK grades do you mill?**
Unfilled PEEK is the standard grade — 100 MPa tensile, continuous 250°C service, best chemical resistance, and best biocompatibility. PEEK GF30 (30% glass-filled) reaches 160 MPa with improved stiffness and higher HDT — for structural applications; tool wear is significantly higher than unfilled PEEK. PEEK CF30 (30% carbon-filled) achieves 200 MPa and is electrically conductive (ESD-safe) — highest strength grade but not suitable for implant-adjacent biomedical applications due to carbon fibre leaching. PEEK Bearing Grade (filled with PTFE and carbon/graphite) has reduced coefficient of friction for bearing and bushing applications. Confirm grade availability at quote stage — unfilled PEEK is held in stock; filled grades are ordered per project.

**Q3: Is PEEK biocompatible for medical device applications?**
Unfilled PEEK has demonstrated biocompatibility under ISO 10993 evaluation for cytotoxicity, sensitisation, and implantation testing. PEEK is used in spinal implants, orthopaedic instruments, and medical fluid path components. PEEK's biocompatibility is grade-dependent — unfilled PEEK processed in clean conditions passes ISO 10993 evaluation; carbon-filled and some glass-filled grades introduce additional materials whose biocompatibility must be evaluated separately. PEEK's radiolucency (X-ray transparency) makes it a preferred implant material in spinal surgery where post-operative imaging is required. Confirm the specific grade, processing history, and applicable biocompatibility standard at quote stage — the engineering team will advise.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled PEEK parts?**
PEEK's low surface energy makes coating adhesion unreliable without specialist pre-treatment. Available finishes: as-milled (Ra 1.6 µm standard, Ra 0.8 µm with fine finish) and bead blasted (uniform matte texture). Painting and electroplating are not standard for PEEK. For medical and cleanroom applications, the as-milled surface with fine finish pass is typically the correct specification — PEEK's inherent chemical resistance and smooth machined surface satisfy cleanroom and hygienic requirements without additional coating. Anodizing and passivation are metal-specific processes not applicable to PEEK.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled PEEK parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any PEEK order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for PEEK CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For medical device components, provide a 2D drawing with explicit tolerance callouts, material grade specification (unfilled/GF30/CF30), and any biocompatibility standard requirements. Note whether PEEK stock pre-annealing is required for the tolerance class specified.

**Q7: How does PEEK milling cost compare to other engineering plastics?**
PEEK milling is the most expensive standard engineering plastic to mill. Raw material cost is 10–30× higher than ABS, nylon, or Delrin per kilogram. Machining cost is also higher — PEEK's crystalline structure requires carbide tooling, controlled parameters, and more frequent tool changes than softer plastics. Pre-annealing adds an additional process step for precision parts. PEEK is specified when the application requires it — temperature, chemical resistance, or biocompatibility — and in those applications, the cost premium is part of the component specification. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: When should I choose PEEK milling over Ultem (PEI) milling?**
PEEK and Ultem (PEI — polyetherimide) are both high-performance engineering plastics with temperature resistance above standard engineering plastics. PEEK provides higher continuous service temperature (250°C vs Ultem 1010 at 217°C HDT), better chemical resistance (PEEK resists a wider solvent range than Ultem), higher tensile strength (PEEK 100 MPa vs Ultem 1010 105 MPa — comparable for unfilled grades), and the preferred biocompatibility standard for implant-adjacent medical applications. Ultem provides inherent UL94 V-0 flammability (without additives) and is the standard for aerospace FAR 25.853 compliance in interior components — it costs less than PEEK and is more available in thin sections. Choose PEEK for chemical resistance, maximum temperature, and biomedical applications. Choose Ultem when UL94 V-0 is the primary requirement, cost is a factor, or FAR 25.853 aerospace interior compliance is the specification.
