## Section 2: Why Aluminium for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Aluminium Is the Most Milled Metal

Aluminium is the most widely CNC milled metal for a direct reason: its machinability rating is the highest of any structural metal. Aluminium 6061-T6 machines at a specific cutting energy approximately 3–4× lower than 304 stainless steel and 5–8× lower than titanium Grade 5. This translates to higher spindle speeds, faster feed rates, longer tool life, and shorter cycle times — all of which reduce cost per part. For prototypes and low-volume parts where machining time is the dominant cost driver, aluminium is the most economical metal.

Aluminium's strength-to-weight ratio makes it the default structural metal where weight matters. 6061-T6 provides 276 MPa tensile strength at 2.70 g/cm³ density — a strength-to-weight ratio that exceeds mild steel on a per-unit-mass basis. 7075-T6 reaches 503 MPa tensile at the same density — approaching the yield strength of medium carbon steel at one-third the weight. These properties explain aluminium's dominance in aerospace, robotics, and portable equipment, where every gram of structural mass is a design concern.

Aluminium versus stainless steel is the primary material decision for structural CNC milled parts. Stainless steel provides better corrosion resistance (no coating required in most environments), higher hardness, and better performance at elevated temperature — but at 3× the density and 4× the machining cost. Choose aluminium when weight is a constraint, coating for corrosion protection is acceptable, and service temperature is below 150°C. Choose stainless steel when the part must be corrosion-resistant without coating (food contact, marine, medical), the part must withstand impact or wear without a surface treatment, or operating temperature exceeds aluminium's practical limit.

Anodizing is the natural surface treatment pairing for CNC milled aluminium. Type II anodizing adds 5–25 µm, increases surface hardness to 200–300 HV, and provides a colour-stable, corrosion-resistant finish. Type III hard anodizing adds 25–100 µm and achieves 400–600 HV — used for wear surfaces on aluminium tooling and structural components. Anodize dimensional change (50% inward growth, 50% outward) must be designed for — machine to pre-anodize dimensions and state the post-anodize callout on the drawing for precision features.

## Section 3: Aluminium Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Aluminium Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Tensile Strength | Yield Strength | Key Properties | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6061-T6 | 310 MPa | 276 MPa | Best balance of strength, machinability, weldability, anodize response | General structural parts, enclosures, brackets |
| 7075-T6 | 572 MPa | 503 MPa | Highest strength, lower corrosion resistance, good machinability | Aerospace, high-load structural, sports equipment |
| 2024-T3 | 483 MPa | 345 MPa | High fatigue resistance; lower corrosion resistance | Aerospace fatigue-critical parts |
| 5052-H32 | 228 MPa | 193 MPa | Best corrosion resistance in marine environments; excellent formability | Marine and coastal structural components |

**Machinability notes:** All aluminium alloys machine with carbide or HSS tooling at high speed (15,000–25,000 RPM for small cutters). Flood coolant or cutting oil prevents built-up edge on aluminium — a thin layer of aluminium that welds to the cutter face and degrades surface finish. 7075 and 2024 have slightly lower machinability than 6061 due to their higher copper and zinc content, but all three are significantly easier to machine than any stainless steel grade. 5052 is the softest and most prone to built-up edge — cutting oil is particularly important on 5052.

## Section 5: Aluminium CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Aluminium CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Aerospace and Defence Structural Components** — Brackets, ribs, spars, and housings in 6061-T6 and 7075-T6. Aluminium's strength-to-weight ratio makes it irreplaceable for aerospace structural prototypes. 7075-T6 is specified for primary structure; 6061-T6 for secondary structure and enclosures.

**Electronics Enclosures and Heat Sinks** — Milled aluminium housings, RF enclosures, and heat sink fins. Aluminium's thermal conductivity (167 W/m·K for 6061) makes it the standard material for heat management components in electronics. Bead blast plus anodize Type II is the standard finish sequence for electronics enclosures.

**Robotics and Automation** — End effectors, robot arm links, motor mounts, and structural chassis. Aluminium's stiffness-to-weight performance — Young's modulus 69 GPa at 2.70 g/cm³ — is the primary driver for robotic structural design. Milled 6061-T6 with hard anodize on wear interfaces is the standard robotics structural specification.

**Medical Device Housings and Frames** — Non-contact structural housings, camera frames, and instrument bodies. Aluminium's machinability produces complex geometries with the tight tolerances required for instrument assembly. Anodize Type II provides a cleanable, corrosion-resistant surface.

**Automotive Prototype Components** — Intake manifold prototypes, suspension components, and structural brackets. Aluminium 6061-T6 is the standard prototype alloy for automotive structural applications requiring metal properties with low machining cost.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Aluminium CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling aluminium?**
Standard aluminium milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm (ISO 2768-m). Precision features with stable fixturing and appropriate tooling achieve ±0.005 mm. For features requiring tighter than ±0.005 mm — bearing bores, precision fits — confirm the specific geometry at quote stage; ±0.0025 mm is achievable on short-run precision components with appropriate process control. Aluminium's coefficient of thermal expansion (23 µm/m·°C for 6061) means dimensions shift slightly with temperature — for tolerances tighter than ±0.005 mm, parts should be inspected at 20°C (standard ISO reference temperature).

**Q2: Which aluminium grades do you mill?**
6061-T6 is the most commonly milled grade — 310 MPa tensile, excellent machinability, good anodize response, and weldable. 7075-T6 is specified for high-load structural and aerospace applications — 572 MPa tensile at the same density as 6061, best strength-to-weight ratio of any standard aluminium alloy. 2024-T3 provides high fatigue resistance for aerospace fatigue-critical structures. 5052-H32 is the marine-grade alloy for corrosive environments. Confirm grade availability at quote stage — 6061 and 7075 are held in stock; 2024 and 5052 are ordered per project.

**Q3: What surface finish is achievable on CNC milled aluminium?**
As-milled aluminium achieves Ra 1.6 µm with a standard finish pass. A fine finish pass with appropriate cutter geometry and low chip load achieves Ra 0.4 µm. For mirror-polished surfaces (Ra <0.1 µm), hand polishing or mechanical polishing after milling is used. Bead blasting produces a uniform matte texture at Ra 2.0–3.2 µm — this is the standard pre-anodize surface. After Type II anodizing, surface roughness increases slightly (3–5 µm Ra depending on anodize thickness). After Type III hard anodizing, Ra typically increases by 5–10 µm due to the thicker, more crystalline oxide layer.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for milled aluminium parts?**
Aluminium accepts the widest range of surface finishes of any CNC milled material. As-milled (Ra 1.6 µm), bead blasted (matte texture), Type II anodizing (corrosion resistance, colour, 200–300 HV), Type III hard anodizing (wear resistance, 400–600 HV), powder coating (colour, 60–120 µm), painting (any RAL colour), nickel plating (with zincate pre-treatment), and chemical conversion coating (Alodine/Iridite — chromate conversion for corrosion and adhesion). Passivation, black oxide, and electropolishing are not applicable to aluminium in standard processes.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled aluminium parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any aluminium order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for aluminium CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format — it carries full 3D geometry. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). Provide a 2D drawing alongside the STEP file with explicit tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, and anodize requirements for any precision or finished part.

**Q7: How does aluminium milling cost compare to steel milling?**
Aluminium CNC milling costs less than stainless steel milling for equivalent geometry, due to three factors: aluminium machines 3–4× faster than stainless, tool wear on aluminium is far lower (longer tool life), and aluminium raw stock is less expensive than stainless steel per kilogram. For equivalent complexity, a milled aluminium part typically costs 30–50% less than the same part in stainless steel. The cost advantage narrows for very simple parts (where machining time is minimal) and widens for complex multi-feature parts with long cycle times.

**Q8: When should I choose aluminium milling over stainless steel milling?**
Choose aluminium milling when: weight is a design constraint, the part can be protected by anodizing or coating for corrosion resistance, the operating temperature is below 150°C, and machining cost is a factor. Choose stainless steel milling when: the part must be corrosion-resistant without coating (food contact, medical, marine), the part must withstand surface wear or impact without treatment, the design requires stainless steel's higher density for balance/vibration damping, or the part operates above 150°C where aluminium strength degrades. For structural prototypes where weight matters and coating is acceptable, aluminium is the correct specification. For fluid-path, hygienic, and no-maintenance parts, stainless steel is correct.
