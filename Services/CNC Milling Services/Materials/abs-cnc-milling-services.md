## Section 2: Why ABS for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why ABS Is the Standard Plastic for CNC Milled Prototypes

ABS is the default choice for CNC milled plastic prototypes for three reasons: it machines cleanly at high feed rates with minimal tool wear, it holds tight tolerances on complex geometry, and it accepts paint, primer, and adhesive bonding better than polyolefins (PP, HDPE) or fluoropolymers (PTFE). An ABS prototype milled to drawing and painted is visually indistinguishable from an injection-moulded production part — which makes ABS the standard for design validation and pre-production samples.

Machinability is ABS's primary advantage over other engineering plastics in the milling context. ABS is an amorphous thermoplastic with a glass transition temperature (Tg) of approximately 105°C. Below Tg it mills cleanly with sharp carbide tooling — no gumminess, no tearing, minimal burring on exit edges. Surface roughness on milled ABS is readily achievable at Ra 1.6 µm as-machined, and can be improved to Ra 0.8 µm with a finishing pass and appropriate cutter geometry. ABS does not require coolant for most milling operations — compressed air or dry cutting with chip clearance is standard practice.

ABS versus polycarbonate (PC) is the most common material choice in prototype milling. PC provides higher impact strength (250× glass) and better heat resistance (HDT 120°C vs ABS 85–100°C), but PC requires more careful heat management during milling and is more prone to stress cracking from solvent contact. For prototypes where impact resistance and optical clarity are not required, ABS machines faster and with lower cost. Choose ABS for housings, brackets, jigs, and functional prototypes. Choose PC when the part must be transparent, must survive impact loading, or must operate above 100°C.

Surface finishing of milled ABS is straightforward. ABS accepts spray primer and topcoat directly — no adhesion promoter required. The surface can be solvent-bonded with MEK (methyl ethyl ketone) for assembly. Bead blasting before painting provides a uniform matte texture. ABS can be chrome-plated with appropriate electroless pre-treatment for decorative parts. ABS does not respond to anodizing or passivation — those processes are for metals only.

## Section 3: ABS Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** ABS Material Properties for CNC Milling

| Property | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 40–50 MPa | Grade-dependent |
| Flexural Modulus | 2.1–2.6 GPa | Stiff enough for structural prototypes |
| Density | 1.03–1.06 g/cm³ | Light compared to metals |
| HDT (heat deflection) | 85–100°C | Standard grade; PC-ABS blends higher |
| Glass Transition (Tg) | ~105°C | Operating limit; above this, parts soften |
| Impact Strength (Izod notched) | 10–20 kJ/m² | Good — higher than acrylic, lower than PC |
| Moisture Absorption | <0.5% | Negligible dimensional change from humidity |
| UL94 Flammability | HB (standard) | V-0 available in FR grades |

**Grades commonly milled:**

| Grade | Key Difference | Best Application |
|---|---|---|
| Standard ABS | General purpose, balanced properties | Housings, enclosures, brackets |
| ABS-PC Blend | Higher HDT (115°C), better impact | Parts requiring >100°C service temperature |
| ABS-GF15/GF30 | Glass-filled: 30–50% higher stiffness, lower CTE | Dimensional stability under load; more tool wear |
| ABS FR (V-0) | UL94 V-0 flame retardant | Electrical enclosures, EV components |

**Machinability notes:** ABS machines at high spindle speeds (10,000–20,000 RPM typical) with sharp HSS or carbide tooling. Dull tools generate heat and produce a gummy, melted surface rather than clean cuts. Chip evacuation is critical — ABS produces long, stringy chips that can re-weld to the machined surface if not cleared. Compressed air at the cut is standard. Coolant is generally avoided on thin sections — liquid coolant temperature differentials can induce warping on walls thinner than 2 mm.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** ABS CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling ABS plastic?**
Standard ABS milling tolerance is ±0.1 mm (ISO 2768-m equivalent). On precision features with stable fixturing and appropriate tooling, ±0.05 mm is achievable. ABS is dimensionally stable — moisture absorption is less than 0.5%, so there is no significant humidity-driven dimensional change after machining. Glass-filled ABS grades (GF30) have lower CTE and better dimensional stability under load, but the glass content increases tool wear — confirm grade selection at quote stage. For features tighter than ±0.05 mm, discuss the geometry and fixturing strategy with the engineering team.

**Q2: Which ABS grades are available for CNC milling?**
Standard ABS is the most commonly milled grade — balanced impact resistance (10–20 kJ/m² Izod notched), tensile strength 40–50 MPa, and HDT 85–100°C. ABS-PC blend provides higher heat deflection (HDT 115°C) and better impact performance where the part must survive above 100°C service temperature. ABS-GF30 (30% glass-filled) provides higher stiffness and lower CTE for dimensionally critical parts, at the cost of increased tool wear and a rougher machined surface. ABS FR (UL94 V-0) is available for flame-retardant requirements in electrical and EV enclosure applications. Confirm grade availability at quote stage.

**Q3: Does ABS require coolant during CNC milling?**
Standard ABS milling uses compressed air or dry cutting rather than liquid coolant. Liquid coolant can cause thermal shock on thin-walled ABS parts (wall thickness <2 mm), inducing warping as sections heat and cool unevenly. Compressed air clears chips and prevents heat buildup at the cutter without the thermal cycling risk. For deep pockets and high-aspect-ratio features where chip re-welding is a concern, mist coolant at low flow rate is used. Chip evacuation is more important than cooling for ABS — ABS chips are long and stringy, and re-welded chips produce surface defects. The engineering team confirms the cutting strategy at DFM stage.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled ABS parts?**
As-milled ABS achieves Ra 1.6 µm as standard, improvable to Ra 0.8 µm with a fine finishing pass. Bead blasting produces a uniform matte texture suitable as a base for painting. Spray primer and topcoat are applied directly to ABS without adhesion promoter — any standard automotive or industrial spray paint adheres to ABS. Solvent bonding with MEK (methyl ethyl ketone) is used for ABS assembly joints. Chrome plating (decorative) and nickel plating are possible with electroless pre-treatment — confirm at quote stage. Anodizing, passivation, black oxide, and electropolishing are metal-only processes and are not applicable to ABS.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled ABS parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any ABS order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order. There is no batch minimum.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for ABS CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format — it carries full 3D geometry, tolerance callouts from the associated 2D drawing, and surface finish notes. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF. For precision features, provide a 2D drawing alongside the STEP file with explicit tolerance callouts — do not rely on default ISO 2768-m for any feature requiring better than ±0.1 mm.

**Q7: How does CNC milled ABS cost compare to other plastic milling options?**
ABS is one of the lowest-cost plastic milling materials — low tool wear, fast cycle times, and readily available raw stock in multiple grades. ABS material cost is lower than PEEK, polycarbonate, Delrin, and Ultem. Milling cycle time on ABS is shorter than on harder plastics — ABS at 15,000 RPM cuts significantly faster than polycarbonate at equivalent chip load. The total cost for a milled ABS prototype is typically lower than equivalent parts in any other engineering plastic. For housings and enclosures where material properties do not require a premium plastic, ABS is the cost-optimal choice.

**Q8: When should I choose ABS over polycarbonate for CNC milled parts?**
Choose ABS when: the part is a visual prototype requiring painting, the service temperature is below 85–100°C, impact resistance is not a primary requirement, and optical clarity is not needed. Choose polycarbonate when: the part must be transparent or translucent, impact resistance is a primary requirement (PC is 250× stronger than glass in impact vs ABS at 10–20 kJ/m²), or the part must operate above 100°C. ABS machines faster, costs less per part, and is easier to paint. PC is harder to mill cleanly (requires careful heat management to avoid stress) and is more susceptible to solvent stress cracking. For enclosures, housings, jigs, and brackets where opacity is acceptable and service temperature is moderate, ABS is the correct specification.
