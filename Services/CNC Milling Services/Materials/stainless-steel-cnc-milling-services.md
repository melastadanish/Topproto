## Section 2: Why Stainless Steel for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Stainless Steel Is Specified for Corrosion-Resistant Milled Parts

Stainless steel is the specification when corrosion resistance without coating is the requirement. Aluminium anodized or powder-coated provides corrosion resistance — but the coating adds dimensions, can be damaged, and must be removed for repair and recoating. Stainless steel's chromium oxide passive layer (Cr₂O₃) regenerates on contact with oxygen — a scratched stainless steel surface re-passivates in air within minutes without any treatment. For food contact, medical, marine, and chemical environments where a coating-free, cleanable, corrosion-resistant metal surface is the specification, stainless steel is the correct material.

Stainless steel's machinability is the primary trade-off against aluminium. Stainless steel (304, 316L) has a machinability rating of approximately 45–55% on the standard index — substantially lower than aluminium 6061 (approximately 150–200%) and lower than brass C360 (100%). The reasons are: high work hardening rate (316L hardens rapidly when the cutter rubs rather than cuts, dulling tools and increasing cutting force), high thermal strength (stainless retains strength at elevated temperature, resisting the chip shear), and low thermal conductivity (15–25 W/m·K vs 167 W/m·K for aluminium 6061 — heat concentrates at the tool-chip interface rather than dissipating into the chip and workpiece). Managing stainless steel milling requires sharp tooling, controlled feed rate to avoid rubbing, flood coolant, and frequent tool changes.

Grade selection in stainless steel determines machinability and corrosion properties. 303 is the free-machining grade — sulphur additions (0.15% min) break chips and reduce tool pressure, with machinability approaching 78% of the reference. 303 trades this for slightly lower corrosion resistance than 304 due to the sulphide inclusions. 316L adds molybdenum (2–3%) for superior pitting corrosion resistance in chloride environments — marine, pharmaceutical, and chemical process applications. 17-4PH is precipitation-hardening stainless — heat-treatable to 1000–1170 MPa tensile for applications requiring stainless steel strength approaching tool steel.

Passivation and electropolishing are the standard post-milling treatments for stainless steel. Passivation (ASTM A967) removes free iron contamination introduced by machining tools and restores the intact Cr₂O₃ passive layer — zero dimensional change. Electropolishing (ASTM B912) removes 5–25 µm of surface material, improving Ra, removing micro-roughness, and producing a chrome-enriched surface with superior pitting resistance — with dimensional change that must be designed for.

## Section 3: Stainless Steel Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Stainless Steel Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Tensile Strength | Key Property | Machinability | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 303 | 620 MPa | Free-machining (sulphur); lower corrosion than 304 | ~78% | General precision parts; not for weld or marine |
| 304 | 515 MPa | Standard austenitic; good corrosion resistance | ~45% | General structural, food contact, architecture |
| 316L | 485 MPa | Mo for chloride/pitting resistance; low carbon | ~45% | Marine, pharma, medical, chemical process |
| 17-4PH | 1000–1170 MPa (H900) | Precipitation hardening; heat-treatable | ~45% (annealed) | Aerospace, high-load, fatigue-critical |

**Machinability notes:** Stainless steel requires flood coolant, sharp carbide tooling with TiAlN or TiCN coating, and controlled chip load to avoid work hardening. Work hardening builds up when feed rate is too low — the cutter rubs rather than cuts, work-hardening the surface and accelerating tool wear in a compounding cycle. Maintain positive chip load at all times. 303's sulphur content reduces this risk. 316L is the most challenging to mill — its lower carbon content (L grade) reduces chromium carbide precipitation but does not improve work hardening. 17-4PH must be milled in the annealed condition before heat treatment; milling hardened 17-4PH significantly increases cutting forces and tool wear.

## Section 5: Stainless Steel CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Stainless Steel CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Medical Device and Surgical Instrument Components** — Milled 316L instrument bodies, housings, and structural frames. 316L provides the corrosion resistance required in the surgical environment, passivation removes machining contamination, and electropolishing achieves the Ra <0.4 µm surface required for cleanable medical device surfaces.

**Food and Beverage Processing Equipment** — Valve bodies, manifold blocks, conveyor components, and equipment frames in 304 or 316L. Stainless steel's corrosion resistance in food acids, cleaning chemicals (NaOH, nitric acid), and hot water sanitisation makes it the standard material for food contact equipment. 316L is specified for applications with chloride-containing cleaning agents (salt brines, certain sanitisers).

**Pharmaceutical Manufacturing Components** — Fluid path fittings, manifold bodies, and filter housings for pharmaceutical process equipment. 316L with electropolishing and passivation to Ra <0.4 µm is the standard specification for pharmaceutical fluid contact surfaces — smooth, chrome-enriched, and fully traceable.

**Marine and Coastal Equipment** — Structural brackets, fasteners, and equipment components for marine environments. 316L's molybdenum content provides pitting corrosion resistance in chloride-containing salt water environments where 304 would corrode. Passivation ensures the full passive layer integrity after milling.

**Aerospace and Defence** — High-load structural components, fasteners, and fittings where stainless steel's combination of strength, corrosion resistance, and non-magnetic properties is the specification. 17-4PH at H900 temper provides 1170 MPa tensile — approaching the strength of high-alloy tool steel at stainless corrosion resistance.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Stainless Steel CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling stainless steel?**
Standard stainless steel milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm (ISO 2768-m). Precision features with controlled parameters and sharp tooling achieve ±0.005 mm. Stainless steel work-hardening is the primary factor limiting tight tolerances — if the cutting tool rubs rather than cuts (at too-low feed rate or with a dull tool), the surface work-hardens and subsequent passes cannot reach dimension. The engineering team maintains positive chip load throughout to prevent work hardening. For tolerances tighter than ±0.005 mm on stainless steel, confirm the specific geometry and feature at quote stage.

**Q2: Which stainless steel grades do you mill?**
303 is the free-machining grade — sulphur additions improve chip-breaking and tool life, with machinability approximately 78% of the reference; slightly lower corrosion resistance than 304 due to sulphide inclusions, not weldable, not suitable for marine environments. 304 is the standard austenitic grade — 515 MPa tensile, good general corrosion resistance, weldable, food-safe. 316L adds 2–3% molybdenum for superior pitting corrosion resistance in chloride environments — marine, pharmaceutical, chemical process. 17-4PH is precipitation-hardenable — milled in annealed condition, heat-treated to 1000–1170 MPa for aerospace and high-load applications. Confirm grade availability at quote stage.

**Q3: What is work hardening and how does it affect stainless steel milling?**
Work hardening is the increase in hardness and strength of stainless steel when it deforms plastically under the cutter. Austenitic stainless steels (303, 304, 316L) have a high work hardening rate — they become significantly harder than the parent material when cold-worked. If the milling cutter rubs the stainless surface (due to too-low chip load, a dull tool, or incorrect cutting parameters), the surface work-hardens before it is cut. The next tool pass encounters a harder material, dulls faster, rubs more, work-hardens further — a compounding cycle that accelerates tool wear and prevents achieving the target dimension. The solution is: sharp tooling, flood coolant, and maintaining a positive minimum chip load at all times. 303's sulphur reduces work hardening susceptibility. The engineering team specifies cutting parameters to avoid work hardening on all stainless milling operations.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled stainless steel parts?**
Stainless steel accepts the widest range of metal surface finishing of any milled material. As-milled (Ra 1.6 µm), bead blasted (uniform matte), brushed finish (directional grain, architectural), passivation (ASTM A967 — removes free iron, restores passive layer, zero dimensional change), electropolishing (ASTM B912 — Ra improvement to 0.2–0.8 µm, chrome enrichment, 5–25 µm removal), mechanical polishing (Ra 0.4–0.8 µm for medical and pharmaceutical), and black oxide (specialist process for stainless — confirm at quote). Anodizing is not applicable to stainless steel.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled stainless steel parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any stainless steel order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for stainless steel CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For medical and pharmaceutical parts, provide a 2D drawing specifying grade, surface finish specification (passivation or electropolishing), Ra requirements on fluid contact surfaces, and any applicable material traceability or certification requirements.

**Q7: How does stainless steel milling cost compare to aluminium milling?**
Stainless steel milling costs significantly more than aluminium milling for equivalent geometry — typically 2–3× higher. Three cost drivers: stainless steel machines at 45–55% machinability vs aluminium's 150–200%, meaning stainless cycle times are 3–4× longer; stainless tool wear is higher than aluminium (shorter tool life, more tool changes); and stainless raw material costs more per kilogram than aluminium. For structural parts where aluminium's coating can meet corrosion requirements, aluminium is the more economical choice. For food contact, medical, marine, and chemical applications where coating-free corrosion resistance is required, stainless steel is the correct specification despite the higher cost.

**Q8: When should I choose 316L over 304 for CNC milled stainless steel parts?**
Choose 316L when the part will contact chloride-containing environments — salt water (marine), saline solutions (medical), chloride-containing cleaning chemicals, or chloride-bearing process fluids. 304 is susceptible to pitting corrosion in chloride environments — the chloride ions disrupt the chromium oxide passive layer, allowing localised corrosion that penetrates into the material as pits. 316L's molybdenum (2–3%) stabilises the passive layer in chloride environments and significantly improves pitting resistance. Choose 304 for general indoor, food (non-chloride), and dry service environments — 304's lower molybdenum content makes it less expensive than 316L, and its corrosion resistance is adequate in most non-chloride applications. For all pharmaceutical fluid path, marine, and medical device applications, specify 316L as the default.
