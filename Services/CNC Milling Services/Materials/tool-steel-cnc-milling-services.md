## Section 2: Why Tool Steel for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Tool Steel Is Milled for Moulds, Dies, and Tooling

Tool steel is milled for one class of application: tooling — injection moulds, die casting dies, stamping dies, punches, jigs, and fixtures where the tool itself is the product rather than a structural component. Tool steel's defining property is that it can be hardened (by heat treatment to 58–68 HRC) to a hardness that allows it to withstand repeated high-pressure contact with other materials without permanent deformation, wear, or failure. The milling operation creates the 3D geometry of the tool; the subsequent heat treatment converts the soft annealed steel (typically 20–30 HRC before heat treatment) into the hardened tool that will produce thousands of parts.

The sequence for tool steel milling is: machine in the annealed (soft) condition, heat treat to the target hardness, then grind or EDM to final dimension and finish. CNC milling in annealed tool steel is substantially easier than milling hardened steel — annealed D2, for example, has a machinability rating of approximately 45%, similar to stainless steel. After heat treatment to 62 HRC, D2 cannot be practically milled — it requires grinding or wire/sinker EDM for material removal on hardened surfaces. The pre-heat treatment milling operation leaves a machining allowance (typically 0.1–0.3 mm per surface) that is removed by grinding after hardening to achieve the final dimension.

Pre-hardened tool steel grades (P20 typically supplied at 28–32 HRC, H13 at various tempers) are used for prototype moulds and low-volume tooling where the hardness of the pre-hardened grade is sufficient and the cost and distortion risk of a second heat treatment are to be avoided. P20 at 30 HRC machines considerably harder than mild steel but is still practical with carbide tooling — cycle times are 2–3× longer than mild steel. Pre-hardened P20 is the standard material for prototype injection moulds that will run 500–5,000 cycles before replacement.

Tool steel milling is the entry point for a longer manufacturing sequence — DFM review must consider the complete sequence (mill → heat treat → grind/EDM → polish → assemble) and its dimensional implications. The machined pre-heat-treatment dimension must account for the expected dimensional change from heat treatment (typically 0.05–0.2% linear, depending on grade and section size) and leave the correct post-heat-treatment stock for grinding. The engineering team reviews the complete tooling sequence at DFM stage.

## Section 3: Tool Steel Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Tool Steel Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Hardness (HT) | Key Property | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| D2 (Cold Work) | 58–62 HRC | High chromium (12% Cr); wear resistance; air-hardening | Stamping dies, blanking tools, long-run cutting tools |
| H13 (Hot Work) | 50–54 HRC | Chromium-molybdenum-vanadium; thermal fatigue resistance | Die casting dies, hot forging dies, extrusion tooling |
| P20 (Mould Steel) | 28–32 HRC (pre-hardened) | Pre-hardened; no post-HT required; 1,000–30,000 mould cycles | Prototype and low-volume injection moulds |
| S7 (Shock-Resisting) | 56–58 HRC | High impact resistance; toughness | Punches, chisels, shock-loading tooling |
| O1 (Oil Hardening) | 60–64 HRC | Oil-quench; less distortion than water-hardening | Precision tooling with tight post-HT tolerance |

**Machinability:** Tool steel machinability varies by grade. Annealed D2: approximately 45% machinability — high chromium carbides in D2 are abrasive to tooling even in the annealed state; carbide tooling required. Annealed H13: approximately 65% — more machinable than D2; carbide tooling. Pre-hardened P20 (30 HRC): approximately 35–40% — carbide tooling, lower feed rates than mild steel. Hardened tool steel (>55 HRC): cannot be practically milled — grinding and EDM required. All tool steel milling requires flood coolant, sharp carbide tooling with appropriate coating (TiAlN for hot work steels), and conservative chip loads to prevent premature tool failure.

## Section 5: Tool Steel CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Tool Steel CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Injection Mould Cavities and Cores** — P20 pre-hardened moulds for prototype injection moulding (500–5,000 cycles), H13 for production moulds running hot-runner systems, and D2 for long-run abrasive-material moulds. CNC milling creates the 3D cavity geometry; EDM finishes tight corners and complex surfaces that milling cannot access. Polish to SPI standard A1/A2 for optical-quality moulded surfaces.

**Stamping and Blanking Dies** — D2 die plates, punch sets, and die blocks for sheet metal stamping. D2's high chromium carbide content provides wear resistance against the abrasive action of repeated blanking — D2 dies run millions of cycles in production stamping operations. CNC mill the die geometry in annealed condition, heat treat to 60–62 HRC, grind to final dimension.

**Die Casting Dies and Inserts** — H13 hot work tool steel for aluminium and zinc die casting die inserts, slides, and cores. H13's chromium-molybdenum-vanadium composition provides resistance to thermal fatigue from repeated heating and cooling during die casting cycles. H13 dies withstand 50,000–300,000 die casting shots in aluminium die casting before replacement.

**Jigs, Fixtures, and Gauges** — Hardened tool steel jig plates, fixture components, and go/no-go gauges requiring a hard, wear-resistant surface that maintains dimensional accuracy through repeated use. O1 tool steel (oil-hardening) is preferred for precision gauges because oil quenching produces less distortion than water quenching, allowing tighter post-heat-treatment dimensions.

**Cutting Tools and Punches** — Punch presses, piercing punches, trimming tools, and cutting tool blanks. S7 shock-resistant tool steel is specified for punches subject to impact loading — S7's high impact toughness resists cracking under shock loading that would fracture D2 or H13.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Tool Steel CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling tool steel?**
In the annealed condition (pre-heat treatment), standard tool steel milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm — with a machining allowance of 0.1–0.3 mm per surface left for post-heat-treatment grinding. After heat treatment and grinding, final dimensions achieve ±0.005 mm. The full tooling sequence — mill → heat treat → grind → EDM → polish — is designed at DFM stage, and each operation's dimensional implications are factored into the machined pre-heat-treatment dimensions. For tight-tolerance mould cavities and die faces, the post-heat-treatment EDM and grinding operations determine the final dimension, not the milling pass.

**Q2: Which tool steel grades do you mill?**
D2 (cold work, 12% Cr, 58–62 HRC after heat treatment) for stamping, blanking, and long-run wear-resistant tooling. H13 (hot work, Cr-Mo-V, 50–54 HRC) for die casting and hot forging dies — thermal fatigue resistance. P20 (pre-hardened mould steel, 28–32 HRC) for prototype and low-volume injection moulds — no post-milling heat treatment required. S7 (shock-resisting, 56–58 HRC) for punches and shock-loaded tooling. O1 (oil-hardening, 60–64 HRC) for precision gauges and tooling where minimal heat-treatment distortion is required. Confirm grade availability at quote stage.

**Q3: Can tool steel be CNC milled in the hardened condition?**
Hardened tool steel (>55 HRC) cannot be practically CNC milled with conventional tooling — the surface hardness exceeds the capability of carbide end mills to remove material at practical feed rates and without immediate tool failure. The correct sequence is: mill in the annealed condition, heat treat, then finish by grinding (surface and cylindrical grinding), wire EDM (for profiles and tight-tolerance 2D features), or sinker EDM (for complex 3D cavities). Hard milling (milling hardened steel) is possible with ceramic tooling or CBN (cubic boron nitride) end mills at very low feed rates — it is used for final die polishing passes on hardened P20 and H13 but is not a general milling solution for fully hardened D2 or O1.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for tool steel milled parts?**
Tool steel milling produces as-milled surfaces at Ra 1.6 µm in the pre-heat-treatment condition. Post-heat-treatment operations add: surface grinding (Ra 0.4–0.8 µm, ±0.005 mm), wire EDM (Ra 1.6–3.2 µm recast layer removed by EDM skimming passes to Ra 0.4 µm), sinker EDM (cavity and complex surface finishing), polishing (SPI standard A1/A2/A3 for injection mould surfaces — A1 = mirror to Ra 0.012 µm), black oxide (corrosion protection for tooling), and electroless nickel (surface hardening and release properties — confirm at quote). Anodizing and passivation are not applicable to tool steel.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled tool steel parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any tool steel order including single prototype moulds or die inserts. One tool is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for tool steel CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For injection moulds, provide a 2D drawing with cavity dimensions post-heat-treatment (the engineering team applies machining allowance from the post-HT dimension), parting line definition, draft angles, ejector pin locations, gate positions, and surface finish standard (SPI A1/A2/B1/B2/C1). For stamping dies, provide the blanking/forming geometry with material and thickness of the sheet to be stamped.

**Q7: How does tool steel milling cost compare to standard steel milling?**
Tool steel milling (annealed condition) costs 2–4× more than mild steel milling for equivalent geometry. Cost drivers: tool steel's higher hardness and abrasive carbide content accelerate tool wear even in the annealed condition — more frequent tool changes than mild steel; carbide tooling required throughout; conservative parameters to avoid chatter or deflection on complex cavity geometry. The complete tooling sequence (mill + heat treat + grind + EDM) adds heat treatment cost, grinding time, and EDM time above the milling cost. For production tooling, this total cost is amortised over the mould's cycle life — the upfront cost is justified by the tool life at target part volume. Upload your STEP file for a complete tooling cost including all operations.

**Q8: When should I choose D2 versus H13 tool steel for milling?**
Choose D2 (cold work) for dies and tools operating at room temperature — stamping dies, blanking tools, plastic injection moulds (where the mould itself is at room temperature between cycles, despite the hot plastic contact), cold-forming tools. D2's high chromium carbide content (12% Cr) provides exceptional room-temperature wear resistance. Choose H13 (hot work) for dies and tooling operating at elevated temperature — aluminium and zinc die casting dies (contact with 650–700°C aluminium), hot forging dies, extrusion tooling. H13's Cr-Mo-V composition provides thermal fatigue resistance and hot hardness — it retains usable hardness at temperatures that would cause D2 to soften (D2 secondary hardening temperature is ~500°C; H13 hot work performance extends to 600°C+). The key distinction: cold work tooling in contact with cold or warm materials → D2. Hot work tooling in contact with molten or near-molten materials → H13.
