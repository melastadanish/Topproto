## Section 2: Why Polycarbonate for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Polycarbonate Is the Specification for Transparent Structural Parts

Polycarbonate is specified for CNC milled parts where transparency and impact resistance must coexist. PC achieves 92% light transmission — the same value as acrylic (PMMA) — while providing impact strength 250× that of glass and approximately 5× the impact resistance of acrylic. This combination of optical clarity and toughness makes PC the correct specification for protective glazing, sight glasses, optical housings, and structural transparent covers where acrylic would shatter under impact loading.

Polycarbonate's amorphous structure (no crystalline phase) provides optical isotropy — light passes through PC without polarisation or birefringence artefacts that would be caused by a partially crystalline polymer. This property makes PC the preferred transparent material for optical applications (lenses, light guides, diffuser panels) and for transparent parts that will be viewed under polarised light (LCD inspection windows, polarimetry components). Acrylic also has good optical isotropy; the choice between PC and acrylic for optical applications depends on impact requirement and solvent environment.

Polycarbonate milling requires careful management of heat and stress. PC is an amorphous thermoplastic with a glass transition temperature (Tg) of 147°C — below Tg it is rigid and mills cleanly; above Tg it becomes rubbery and smears under the cutter. Tool geometry must produce a clean shear rather than a rubbing action — sharp cutters with high positive rake angle, high spindle speed, and light chip load. PC also develops internal stress under clamping force during fixturing — poor fixturing leaves residual stress in the part that manifests as crazing or cracking when the part contacts solvents. Vacuum fixturing or soft jaw clamping strategies are used for PC to minimise induced stress.

Solvent stress cracking is polycarbonate's critical limitation. PC is highly susceptible to stress cracking when contacted by solvents — even low-concentration solvents (IPA, acetone, MEK, ketones) attack stressed PC and produce crazing or cracking within minutes. Unstressed PC is more resistant but still vulnerable to aromatic hydrocarbons and chlorinated solvents. For applications where the polycarbonate part will contact solvents during cleaning, assembly, or in service, confirm chemical compatibility at DFM stage.

## Section 3: Polycarbonate Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Polycarbonate Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Key Property | Best Application |
|---|---|---|
| General-purpose PC | 92% transmission, 250× glass impact | Enclosures, covers, shields, structural transparent |
| Optical-grade PC | Low birefringence, high purity | Lenses, light guides, optical elements |
| UV-stabilised PC | UV stabiliser additive; resists yellowing | Outdoor glazing, exterior covers |
| Flame-retardant PC (V-0) | UL94 V-0 rating | Electrical enclosures, EV battery covers |

**Machinability:** PC mills cleanly with sharp carbide tooling at high spindle speed (12,000–20,000 RPM). Heat management is critical — dull tools generate frictional heat that melts and smears the cut surface. Coolant choice matters for PC: water-based coolants can fog optical surfaces; cutting oil or dry milling with compressed air is preferred for optical-grade parts. PC produces long, stringy chips — compressed air blast for chip evacuation. Avoid acetone or ketone-based coolants — they attack PC directly. Standard tolerance on PC is ±0.05 mm; transparent and optical parts may require tighter callouts on critical surfaces.

## Section 5: Polycarbonate CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Polycarbonate CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Protective Glazing and Safety Shields** — Machine guards, equipment covers, and protective panels requiring transparency and impact resistance. PC at 3–4 mm provides ballistic-grade transparency for machine tool splash guards and equipment covers. PC resists the impact from flying debris that would shatter acrylic equivalents.

**Optical Housings and Light Guides** — Camera lens housings, light guide panels, and LED diffuser components. PC's 92% transmission and amorphous optical isotropy produce clear, undistorted optical paths. Milled PC light guides replace injection-moulded parts for prototype and low-volume production of optical assemblies.

**Medical Device Windows and Inspection Ports** — Sight glasses, viewing ports, and autoclave-resistant transparent covers. PC resists standard autoclave conditions (121°C steam) — unlike acrylic, which softens at 85–95°C, PC's higher HDT allows autoclave sterilisation of transparent instrument components. Confirm the specific autoclave temperature and cycle count at quote stage.

**Electronic Enclosures and Display Windows** — Transparent front panels, display windows, and indicator covers for control panels and test equipment. Flame-retardant PC (UL94 V-0) is specified for electrical enclosure panels — PC provides transparency with the flammability rating required for electrical safety standards.

**Automotive Interior and Exterior Components** — Instrument cluster covers, tail light lenses, and sunroof glazing prototypes. UV-stabilised PC resists yellowing from UV exposure that would affect standard PC. PC's impact resistance and optical clarity suit automotive lens and glazing prototype applications.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Polycarbonate CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling polycarbonate?**
Standard polycarbonate milling tolerance is ±0.1 mm. Precision features with low-stress vacuum fixturing and sharp tooling achieve ±0.05 mm. PC's amorphous structure provides dimensional stability without moisture-related change (PC absorbs <0.35% moisture — far below nylon). The main dimensional risk on PC is residual stress from clamping — fixtures that apply localised clamping force can induce stress that causes parts to spring slightly when released. Vacuum fixturing distributes clamping force and reduces springback. For optical or precision PC parts, discuss fixturing strategy at DFM stage.

**Q2: What polycarbonate grades do you mill?**
General-purpose PC is the standard grade — 92% light transmission, impact resistance 250× glass, tensile strength 55–60 MPa, HDT 120°C. Optical-grade PC is produced with tighter purity control and lower birefringence — specified for lens and light guide applications. UV-stabilised PC resists yellowing from UV exposure — for outdoor and sun-exposed applications. Flame-retardant PC (UL94 V-0) is for electrical enclosures. Confirm grade availability at quote stage — general-purpose PC is held in stock; optical and UV grades are ordered per project.

**Q3: Can CNC milled polycarbonate be optically polished to achieve clarity?**
Yes — milled polycarbonate can be polished to optical clarity. As-milled PC at Ra 1.6 µm is hazy — the surface roughness scatters light. Flame polishing (passing a controlled gas flame over the surface) melts the surface micro-roughness to Ra <0.1 µm, restoring optical clarity on flat and simply curved surfaces. Diamond polishing (sequential abrasive polishing passes to final buffing) achieves Ra <0.1 µm on complex surfaces that flame polishing cannot reach. Vapour polishing (solvent vapour condensation) smooths the surface at the molecular level — suitable for internal surfaces and complex geometry. Specify the polishing method and the surface(s) requiring optical clarity on the drawing.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled polycarbonate parts?**
As-milled PC (Ra 1.6 µm) is suitable for structural, non-optical applications. Flame polishing restores transparency on flat surfaces. Diamond polishing achieves optical clarity on complex surfaces. Bead blasting produces a diffuse matte surface for light-diffusing applications. Painting is possible with adhesion promoter — avoid solvent-based paints that attack PC. Anodizing, passivation, and black oxide are metal-only processes. For optical PC parts, specify the polishing method and the target Ra on each surface requiring clarity.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled polycarbonate parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any polycarbonate order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for polycarbonate CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For optical parts, provide a 2D drawing specifying which surfaces require optical polishing, the target Ra or transmission specification, and any scratch-dig quality callouts. Note the service environment — solvent contact in service must be flagged at DFM stage.

**Q7: How does polycarbonate milling cost compare to acrylic milling?**
Polycarbonate milling cost is comparable to or slightly higher than acrylic milling. PC raw material costs more per kilogram than PMMA acrylic. PC machining cycle time is similar to acrylic — both machine at high spindle speed with sharp tooling. Optical polishing (flame or diamond) adds similar cost for both materials. The specification decision between PC and acrylic should be made on material properties (PC for impact resistance and higher HDT; acrylic for scratch resistance, lower cost, and easier bonding), not cost — the difference is small relative to total part cost. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: When should I choose polycarbonate milling over acrylic milling?**
Choose polycarbonate when: impact resistance is a requirement (PC impact strength is 5–10× higher than acrylic), the part must survive dropping or ballistic impact (machine guards, protective covers), the part must be sterilised by autoclave (PC withstands 121°C vs acrylic HDT of 85–95°C), or optical clarity combined with toughness is the specification. Choose acrylic when: scratch resistance is a priority (acrylic is harder than PC and more scratch-resistant), the part will be bonded with solvent cement (acrylic bonds easily; PC is solvent-sensitive and bonds with more difficulty), UV exposure over long periods is a concern (acrylic has better inherent UV stability than PC without UV additive), or cost is the primary driver (acrylic is less expensive per kilogram). For transparent structural parts subject to impact, PC is correct. For display panels, clear covers, and non-impact transparent parts, acrylic is often the better choice.
