## Section 2: Why Titanium for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Titanium Is the Specification for Maximum Strength-to-Weight Ratio

Titanium is specified when the design requires the maximum structural performance per unit mass. Ti-6Al-4V (Grade 5) achieves 880–950 MPa tensile strength at 4.43 g/cm³ density — a specific strength (strength divided by density) higher than any standard steel, aluminium, or stainless steel alloy. For aerospace brackets, surgical implants, motorsport structural components, and any application where minimum weight at maximum load capacity is the design constraint, Ti-6Al-4V is the engineering material of choice.

Titanium's corrosion resistance is exceptional — it passivates in contact with oxygen, forming a TiO₂ layer that resists seawater, chlorides, body fluids, and most industrial chemicals. Titanium's corrosion resistance exceeds even 316L stainless steel in aggressive chloride environments and is essentially immune to crevice and pitting corrosion that affects austenitic stainless steels. For implants and medical devices where long-term biostability is required, Grade 2 commercially pure titanium is the standard — its biocompatibility has been established by decades of clinical use in bone screws, dental implants, and prosthetic components.

Titanium's machinability is the primary engineering challenge. Ti-6Al-4V has a machinability rating of approximately 22% — it is one of the most difficult structural metals to CNC mill. Three properties drive this difficulty: low thermal conductivity (6.7 W/m·K vs 167 W/m·K for aluminium 6061) concentrates heat at the tool-chip interface rather than dissipating it into the chip; high chemical reactivity causes titanium to weld to the tool face (titanium affinity for carbide tool materials at elevated temperature — a phenomenon called galling or tool welding); and spring-back from titanium's high strength-to-modulus ratio (Young's modulus 114 GPa vs 200 GPa for steel — titanium is relatively flexible for its strength) causes the material to push away from the cutter and then spring back, reducing dimensional accuracy. Managing titanium milling requires: sharp carbide tooling with TiAlN coating, flood coolant at high flow rate to remove heat, low cutting speed with high chip load, and frequent tool inspection.

Titanium's cost reflects its difficulty: raw material is more expensive than aluminium or stainless steel, and the longer machining cycle time (due to low cutting speed and high tool change frequency) further increases per-part cost. Titanium milling is specified only when the application requires it — and in those applications (aerospace, medical, motorsport), the material cost is a fixed part of the design specification.

## Section 3: Titanium Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Titanium Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Tensile Strength | Density | Key Property | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grade 2 (CP Titanium) | 345 MPa | 4.51 g/cm³ | Maximum corrosion resistance; biocompatible | Medical implants, chemical processing |
| Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) | 880–950 MPa | 4.43 g/cm³ | Highest strength-to-weight; standard aerospace alloy | Aerospace structural, surgical instruments |
| Grade 23 (Ti-6Al-4V ELI) | 860–900 MPa | 4.43 g/cm³ | Extra low interstitials; superior biocompatibility | Implant applications per ASTM F136 |

**Machinability notes:** Titanium requires dedicated machining strategy. Cutting speed: 30–60 m/min (vs 300–400 m/min for aluminium) — low speed prevents heat concentration. Chip load: high (thick chips carry more heat away in the chip). Flood coolant: mandatory at high flow rate — air cooling is insufficient for titanium. Tooling: sharp carbide, TiAlN or AlTiN coating, 4-flute for slotting (more flutes trap chips in titanium slots). Galling risk: new sharp tools for every titanium job — dull tools rub and gall immediately. Thin-wall titanium parts require careful fixturing — titanium's spring-back tendency means inadequate clamping allows the part to move during cutting.

## Section 5: Titanium CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Titanium CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Aerospace Structural Components** — Brackets, fittings, ribs, and structural members where minimum weight at maximum structural load is the specification. Ti-6Al-4V's specific strength exceeds high-strength aluminium 7075-T6 — for primary aerospace structure where weight is critical, titanium replaces aluminium or steel. Milled titanium structural parts are found throughout commercial and military aircraft.

**Medical Implants and Surgical Instruments** — Bone screws, spinal implant components, joint replacement parts, and surgical instrument frames. Grade 2 CP titanium and Grade 23 Ti-6Al-4V ELI are the materials for implantable components — biocompatibility established under ISO 10993 and ASTM F67/F136. Titanium's osseointegration properties (bone grows onto the titanium oxide surface) make it the standard orthopaedic implant material.

**Motorsport Structural and Drivetrain Components** — Connecting rods, suspension uprights, and structural chassis components for Formula racing and high-performance motorsport. Titanium's specific strength allows components to be lighter than aluminium equivalents at higher load capacity. Weight reduction at unsprung and rotating mass has measurable effect on vehicle performance.

**Defence and Tactical Equipment** — Armour fixings, weapons components, and equipment housings requiring high strength, corrosion resistance, and non-magnetic properties. Titanium is non-magnetic — important in mine countermeasures, magnetic anomaly detection equipment, and applications where steel's magnetic signature is a concern.

**Chemical and Offshore Processing** — Valve bodies, pump components, and process equipment parts in aggressive chemical and seawater environments. Titanium's corrosion resistance in chloride environments, oxidising acids, and seawater exceeds all common engineering metals including 316L stainless steel. Offshore platform components and seawater system hardware in titanium provide maintenance-free corrosion performance.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Titanium CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling titanium?**
Standard titanium milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm (ISO 2768-m). Precision features with dedicated tooling, rigid fixturing, and controlled cutting parameters achieve ±0.005 mm. Titanium's spring-back (Young's modulus 114 GPa — relatively compliant for its strength) causes the workpiece to deflect under cutting force and spring back as the tool passes, creating dimensional error on thin walls and narrow features. Rigid fixturing minimises spring-back deflection. For tight-tolerance titanium features (bores, precision fits), discuss the geometry and fixturing strategy at DFM stage.

**Q2: What titanium grades do you mill?**
Grade 2 (commercially pure titanium, CP Ti) provides maximum corrosion resistance and the best biocompatibility — 345 MPa tensile, used for medical implants, chemical processing, and corrosion-critical applications where structural load is moderate. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is the standard aerospace alloy — 880–950 MPa tensile at 4.43 g/cm³, the highest specific strength of standard structural alloys. Grade 23 (Ti-6Al-4V ELI — Extra Low Interstitials) reduces oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, and iron content for improved fracture toughness and fatigue performance in implant applications per ASTM F136. Confirm grade and stock availability at quote stage.

**Q3: Why is titanium expensive to CNC mill?**
Titanium's machining cost is driven by three factors. First, low cutting speed: titanium must be cut at 30–60 m/min vs 300–400 m/min for aluminium — cycle times are 5–10× longer for equivalent geometry. Second, high tool consumption: titanium's low thermal conductivity concentrates heat at the tool-chip interface, accelerating tool wear; titanium's chemical affinity for carbide causes tool welding (galling) when tools overheat or dull — frequent tool changes are required. Third, mandatory flood coolant: high-flow flood coolant is required for all titanium operations — dry or mist milling is not adequate for controlling heat. These factors produce a machining cost 5–10× higher than aluminium for equivalent geometry. Titanium is specified when the application requires it; for structural applications where aluminium or stainless steel is adequate, those materials reduce cost significantly.

**Q4: Is titanium biocompatible for medical implant applications?**
Grade 2 commercially pure titanium and Grade 23 Ti-6Al-4V ELI have established biocompatibility for implant applications under ISO 10993 and ASTM standards (F67 for CP Ti, F136 for Ti-6Al-4V ELI). Titanium's osseointegration property — the ability of bone to grow directly onto the TiO₂ surface — makes it the standard material for bone-contacting implants. Titanium's corrosion resistance in body fluids ensures no ion release that would cause adverse tissue response. Grade 5 standard (non-ELI) is used for surgical instruments and implant-adjacent components. Grade 23 ELI is specified for primary implant components where fatigue performance and biocompatibility documentation at the highest standard are required. Confirm grade, documentation requirements, and applicable standards at quote stage.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled titanium parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any titanium order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for titanium CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For aerospace and medical applications, provide a 2D drawing with explicit tolerance callouts, grade specification (Grade 2 / Grade 5 / Grade 23), surface finish specification, and any certification or material traceability requirements.

**Q7: How does titanium milling cost compare to stainless steel milling?**
Titanium milling is more expensive than stainless steel milling — typically 3–5× the cost for equivalent geometry. Titanium raw material costs more per kilogram than stainless steel. Titanium's machinability rating (approximately 22%) is lower than 304/316L stainless (approximately 45–55%), producing longer cycle times and higher tool consumption. Titanium is specified when the strength-to-weight ratio is the design requirement — the cost is justified in aerospace and medical applications where weight reduction or biocompatibility is not achievable with stainless steel. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: When should I choose titanium milling over stainless steel milling?**
Choose titanium when: minimum weight is a hard requirement (Ti-6Al-4V at 4.43 g/cm³ vs 316L at 8.0 g/cm³ — titanium is 45% lighter at greater specific strength), biocompatibility for implant applications is required (Grade 2 or Grade 23 is established for implants; stainless steel is not standard for primary implants), corrosion resistance in aggressive chloride or oxidising acid environments is required (titanium exceeds 316L in chloride resistance), or the part must be non-magnetic (titanium is non-magnetic; austenitic stainless is weakly magnetic after machining). Choose stainless steel when: structural load is moderate and weight is not a constraint, cost is a significant factor, the part requires surface hardness or wear resistance not available from titanium, or the geometry is simple (titanium's machining premium is most significant for complex parts with long cycle times).
