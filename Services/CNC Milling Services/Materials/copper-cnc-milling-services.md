## Section 2: Why Copper for CNC Milling

**H2:** Why Copper Is Specified for Electrical and Thermal Parts

Copper is specified when maximum electrical or thermal conductivity is the requirement — no other standard engineering metal comes close. C101 electrolytic tough pitch copper achieves 100% IACS (International Annealed Copper Standard) electrical conductivity and 391 W/m·K thermal conductivity. For comparison: aluminium 6061 achieves 167 W/m·K thermal conductivity and 43% IACS electrical conductivity. In busbar, heat sink, and electrical contact applications, copper provides nearly 2.5× the thermal performance and more than twice the electrical performance of aluminium. When those properties define the part's function, copper is the only correct specification.

Copper's machinability is more challenging than aluminium or brass. Pure copper is ductile — it deforms plastically under the cutter rather than shearing cleanly. This produces gummy, stringy chips that adhere to the cutter face (built-up edge) and degrade surface finish. Managing copper milling requires: sharp carbide tooling with polished flutes (to prevent chip adhesion), high spindle speed with moderate chip load, cutting fluid (soluble oil or cutting oil) at the cut to lubricate and prevent built-up edge, and regular tool monitoring to replace cutters before built-up edge develops. With correct tooling and cutting parameters, copper mills well — tolerances of ±0.01 mm are routine, and finer tolerances are achievable on stable geometry.

Copper versus brass is the critical material decision for electrical and thermal components. Brass (C360) machines more easily than copper, costs less per kilogram, and provides similar aesthetic. But brass's zinc and lead content reduce electrical conductivity to 28% IACS and thermal conductivity to ~120 W/m·K — significantly below copper. For busbars, high-current contacts, and thermal management where maximum conductivity drives the specification, copper is correct. For fittings, connectors, and hardware where conductivity requirements can be met by brass, brass is the correct choice due to lower machining cost and easier processing.

Copper is not the typical choice for structural milling — its tensile strength (220–310 MPa depending on temper) is adequate but lower than 6061-T6 aluminium at significantly higher density (8.96 g/cm³ vs 2.70 g/cm³). Copper structural components are heavier than aluminium equivalents for the same strength. Copper is specified structurally only when a single component must combine structural function with maximum electrical conductivity — such as busbar structures that carry both electrical current and mechanical load.

## Section 3: Copper Milling — Material Properties and Machinability

**H2:** Copper Grades for CNC Milling

| Grade | Designation | Electrical Conductivity | Thermal Conductivity | Key Property | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| C101 (ETP Copper) | UNS C10100 | 100% IACS | 391 W/m·K | Standard copper; trace oxygen present | Busbars, heat sinks, contacts |
| C110 (OFHC) | UNS C11000 | 101% IACS | 398 W/m·K | Oxygen-free; no hydrogen embrittlement risk | Welded assemblies, vacuum applications |
| C102 (Oxygen-Free) | UNS C10200 | 100% IACS | 391 W/m·K | Oxygen-free; good for plating | Electronics, high-reliability contacts |

**Machinability notes:** Pure copper is rated at 20% on the machinability index (vs 100% for C360 brass) — it is significantly more difficult to mill cleanly than brass or aluminium. Polished-flute carbide end mills with high positive rake angle are required. Cutting oil or soluble oil at the cut prevents built-up edge. Spindle speed: high (15,000–20,000 RPM for small cutters), chip load: light. Compressed air alone is insufficient for copper — liquid cutting fluid is required. Tool changes are more frequent than on aluminium or brass. These factors increase cycle time and cost compared to equivalent parts in brass or aluminium.

## Section 5: Copper CNC Milling Applications

**H2:** Copper CNC Milled Parts — Applications and Industries

**Electrical Busbars and Distribution Components** — Milled copper busbars, busbar joints, and current distribution components for power electronics, EV battery systems, and industrial switchgear. Copper's 100% IACS conductivity minimises resistive heating at the current levels carried by busbars. Milling produces the precise hole patterns, slot geometry, and joint faces required for assembled busbar systems.

**Heat Sinks and Cold Plates** — Milled copper heat sinks, cold plates, and liquid-cooled thermal management components for power electronics, laser optics, and high-performance computing. Copper's 391 W/m·K thermal conductivity — 2.3× that of aluminium 6061 — is specified when aluminium heat sinks cannot meet the thermal resistance target. Milled copper cold plates with internal channel geometry are used in laser module cooling and semiconductor test equipment.

**Electrical Contacts and Terminals** — Contact blocks, terminal faces, and switch contacts for high-current switching equipment. Copper's conductivity and the ability to plate with silver or gold directly onto the milled copper surface makes milled copper the standard for high-current electrical contact applications.

**RF and Microwave Components** — Waveguide sections, resonator cavities, and shielding components for RF and microwave systems. Copper's electrical conductivity minimises skin-effect losses at microwave frequencies — copper's high conductivity means current flows efficiently in the thin skin depth layer at RF. Milled copper waveguide and cavity components are standard in radar, satellite communication, and scientific RF equipment.

**Semiconductor and Scientific Equipment** — Vacuum feedthroughs, thermal anchors, and cryogenic components. C110 (OFHC) oxygen-free copper is specified for high-vacuum and cryogenic applications — the absence of oxygen prevents hydrogen embrittlement at high temperatures and outgassing in vacuum environments.

## Section 6: FAQ

**H2:** Copper CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances are achievable when CNC milling copper?**
Standard copper milling tolerance is ±0.01 mm (ISO 2768-m). Precision features on stable geometry achieve ±0.005 mm with appropriate tooling and fixturing. Copper's ductility makes it more susceptible to tool deflection on thin features than brass or steel — minimum wall thickness of 1.0 mm is recommended for precision copper milled parts. Copper's CTE is 17 µm/m·°C — for tight-tolerance features, inspect at 20°C. Confirm tolerance requirements at quote stage, particularly for busbar joint faces and contact surfaces where flatness and surface finish interact with conductivity.

**Q2: What is the difference between C101 and C110 copper for CNC milling?**
C101 (ETP — electrolytic tough pitch) is the standard copper grade, with 100% IACS conductivity and trace oxygen (0.02–0.05% O₂). The oxygen is harmless in most applications but causes hydrogen embrittlement when ETP copper is heated above 400°C in a hydrogen-containing atmosphere — the hydrogen reacts with oxygen at grain boundaries and causes cracking. C110 (OFHC — oxygen-free high conductivity) contains <0.001% oxygen — it is safe to braze, weld, and use in hydrogen atmospheres without embrittlement risk. C110 is specified for applications involving high-temperature joining (brazing, welding), hydrogen environments, and high-vacuum applications where oxygen outgassing is a concern. For room-temperature busbar and heat sink applications, C101 is the standard grade.

**Q3: Why is copper harder to CNC mill than brass?**
Pure copper (C101, C110) is ductile — it deforms plastically under the cutter rather than shearing cleanly. This produces gummy, stringy chips that weld onto the cutter face (built-up edge), degrading surface finish and accelerating tool wear. Brass achieves its 100% machinability rating because C360's 2–3% lead content acts as internal chip breakers — lead makes chips brittle and short, preventing built-up edge. Pure copper has no chip-breaker mechanism. Managing copper milling requires polished-flute carbide tooling, high positive rake angle, light chip load, and cutting oil at the cut. Tool changes are more frequent on copper than on brass or aluminium, which increases cycle time and cost.

**Q4: What surface finishes are available for CNC milled copper parts?**
Copper accepts direct electroplating without pre-treatment — this is a significant advantage over aluminium (which requires zincate pre-treatment) and stainless steel (which requires activation). Available surface finishes for milled copper: as-milled (Ra 1.6 µm standard), electroless nickel plating (corrosion protection, direct adhesion), gold plating (contact surfaces — 0.5–2.5 µm gold over nickel undercoat), silver plating (high-conductivity contact surfaces — silver has higher conductivity than gold for contact applications), tin plating (solderable surface for electronics assembly), and black copper oxide (confirm at quote). Passivation and anodizing are not applicable to copper.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milled copper parts?**
No minimum. CNC milling is applied to any copper order including single prototypes. One part is a valid order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for copper CNC milling?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred format. Also accepted: IGES, DXF, DWG, STL, PDF, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), Parasolid (.x_t). For busbar assemblies and contact components, provide a 2D drawing with flatness callouts on joint faces, plating thickness allowances, and any conductivity or surface finish specifications relevant to the application.

**Q7: How does copper milling cost compare to brass and aluminium milling?**
Copper CNC milling is more expensive than both aluminium and brass milling for equivalent geometry. Three cost drivers: copper raw material is more expensive per kilogram than aluminium; copper's lower machinability (20% vs 100% for C360 brass) means longer cycle times, higher cutting force, and more frequent tool changes; and copper's surface sensitivity requires more careful tooling management. For applications where brass or aluminium can meet the conductivity requirement, those materials reduce cost significantly. Copper is specified only when maximum conductivity is required — and in those applications, the material cost is a fixed part of the specification. Upload your STEP file for exact pricing within 24 hours.

**Q8: When should I choose copper milling over aluminium milling for heat sinks?**
Choose copper for heat sinks when aluminium cannot meet the thermal resistance target at the available geometry. Copper's thermal conductivity (391 W/m·K) is 2.3× that of aluminium 6061 (167 W/m·K) — a copper heat sink at the same geometry as an aluminium heat sink delivers 2.3× lower thermal resistance. In practice, a copper heat sink can be made smaller than an aluminium equivalent and still meet the same thermal target — copper is used where size and weight are constrained and the higher cost is acceptable. Choose aluminium when: the geometry allows adequate thermal performance at aluminium conductivity, weight is a concern (copper at 8.96 g/cm³ is 3.3× denser than aluminium), and cost is a factor. For most commercial electronics thermal management, aluminium is used; copper is used in high-power density applications (laser cooling, power module thermal management, cryogenics) where aluminium performance is insufficient.
