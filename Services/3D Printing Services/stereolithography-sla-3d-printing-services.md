## What Is Stereolithography (SLA)?

Stereolithography — SLA — is the original vat photopolymerisation process. A UV laser traces each layer onto the surface of a liquid photopolymer resin bath, curing the resin solid where the beam strikes. The build platform descends one layer at a time — typically 25 to 100 µm — until the part is complete. After printing, parts go through post-cure exposure under UV light to fully develop mechanical properties.

SLA parts achieve Ra 3.2 µm surface finish straight off the machine — smoother than SLS, MJF, or FDM by a significant margin. Layer thickness at 25 µm allows fine features and sharp edges that other processes cannot resolve. Support structures — thin resin scaffolding that anchors overhanging geometry during the build — are removed after printing, leaving witness marks that sanding or priming can address.

The process suits high-detail visual prototypes, clear optical parts, functional enclosures, and first-article models that need to represent final geometry precisely before injection tooling starts. For parts where surface finish Ra value matters at the prototype stage, SLA is the correct process.

## SLA vs SLS — When to Use Each

Both SLA and SLS produce polymer parts without tooling. The right choice depends on geometry, surface quality requirement, and functional demand.

**Use SLA when:**
- Surface finish Ra value is critical — SLA achieves Ra 3.2 µm standard, Ra 1.6 µm polished
- Part geometry requires fine features under 1 mm
- You need a clear or transparent part — clear resin is SLA-specific
- Visual prototype fidelity matters more than mechanical strength
- Layer thickness at 25 µm is needed for thin-wall accuracy

**Use SLS when:**
- Part requires functional nylon PA12 mechanical properties — tensile strength, chemical resistance, hinge fatigue life
- Geometry is highly complex with internal channels, lattices, or interlocking features — SLS requires no support structures
- Wall thickness is 0.7 mm or greater and snap-fit or living hinge performance is required
- Surface finish Ra is acceptable at 6–10 µm as-printed (SLS standard)
- Build volume up to 380 × 280 × 380 mm fits your part

**Quick decision rule:** If your part is a visual or clear prototype — choose SLA. If it is a functional nylon part with complex geometry — choose SLS.

## SLA Materials

### Engineering Resin
The standard choice for functional prototypes. Engineering resin delivers an ABS-like stiffness-to-weight profile — rigid, dimensionally stable, and suitable for snap-fit testing, housing mock-ups, and assembly checks.

- Tensile strength: ~55–65 MPa
- HDT: ~55–65°C
- Best for: Functional enclosures, housings, brackets, assembly prototypes
- Surface finish: Ra 3.2 µm as printed

### Clear Resin
Clear resin transmits light — the only SLA material that produces optically transparent parts after post-processing. Parts require sanding and polishing through a grit sequence to achieve lens-grade clarity.

- Transmittance: High — clarity improves with polishing
- Best for: Fluid channel mock-ups, light pipes, optical covers, display windows
- Note: UV exposure over time causes yellowing — not suited for long-term outdoor display

### Tough Resin
Tough resin absorbs impact without brittle fracture — closer to ABS impact performance than standard engineering resin. Use it where snap-fit clips, thin hinge sections, or repeated assembly cycles are part of the test protocol.

- Tensile strength: ~50–60 MPa
- Elongation at break: ~15–20%
- Best for: Snap fits, living hinges, connectors, parts under repeated mechanical load

## SLA Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 0.5 mm | Thinner walls deflect during UV curing and may not survive support removal |
| Minimum feature size | 0.3 mm raised / 0.5 mm recessed | Below these dimensions, features may not resolve at 100 µm layer height |
| Support considerations | All overhangs over 45° require support — plan witness mark location | Support witness marks are removable but require post-processing time |
| Orientation | Orient to minimise support contact on cosmetic surfaces | Reduces post-processing and improves surface finish on critical faces |
| Hole sizing | Add 0.1–0.2 mm to nominal hole diameter | Resin shrinkage during UV cure reduces hole diameter by ~0.1–0.2 mm |
| Hollowing large parts | Hollow with drain holes ≥3 mm for large solid volumes | Solid large parts pool uncured resin internally, causing delamination risk |
| Thread features | M3 minimum for printed threads; insert use preferred for M4 and below | Photopolymer thread flanks wear rapidly under repeated assembly — brass inserts last longer |
| File prep | Watertight STL with no inverted normals | Non-manifold geometry causes build failures at slicing stage |

## SLA 3D Printing — Frequently Asked Questions

### What tolerance can TOP PROTO hold on SLA 3D printed parts?

SLA 3D printing at TOP PROTO holds ±0.1–0.2 mm on parts up to 100 mm across. For larger parts, tolerance scales to ±0.2% of the nominal dimension — a 300 mm part carries a ±0.6 mm working tolerance. Photopolymer resin shrinks slightly during UV post-cure, which is the primary source of dimensional deviation. TOP PROTO compensates for known shrink at the slicing stage, but tight-tolerance features — holes for press fits, snap-fit boss diameters — should include a ±0.1–0.15 mm allowance in your design. If you have a specific callout under ±0.1 mm, flag it at quoting stage and the engineering team will assess feasibility for your geometry.
