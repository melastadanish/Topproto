## What Is FDM 3D Printing?

Fused Deposition Modeling — FDM — builds parts by melting a filament of thermoplastic material and extruding it through a nozzle along a programmed path. Each pass deposits one bead of material. The printer stacks these beads layer by layer, from the base of the part upward, until the geometry is complete. Layer height is adjustable between 0.1 mm and 0.3 mm — finer layers produce smoother surfaces and better feature resolution, coarser layers build faster and cost less.

Layer adhesion — the bond strength between adjacent beads and between stacked layers — determines the mechanical performance of FDM parts. In-plane (XY) properties are close to the bulk material. Z-axis (through-layer) strength is lower, because the layer interfaces are the weakest points in the structure. Print orientation is therefore a design decision, not just a printing convenience: orienting a part so load paths run in the XY plane, and critical features lie parallel to the build bed, gets the best mechanical result.

Infill — the internal fill pattern and density — controls weight, stiffness, and material consumption. Sparse infill (15–20%) produces light, cost-effective parts for form and fit validation. Dense infill (80–100%) approaches solid material properties for functional testing. Warping — thermal contraction pulling corners off the build plate during printing — is managed through material selection, bed adhesion strategy, and enclosure temperature control. ABS warps more than PETG; ASA is formulated specifically for thermal stability without the warping tendency of ABS.

## FDM vs SLA — When to Use Each

FDM and SLA (Stereolithography) are both accessible 3D printing processes, but they serve different use cases. The distinction comes down to surface finish, material properties, and throughput.

**Use FDM when:**
- Your part is a functional prototype — enclosure, bracket, duct, housing — where structural performance matters more than surface smoothness.
- You need a specific engineering thermoplastic: TPU for flexible components, ASA for UV and weather resistance, PETG for chemical resistance.
- Part size exceeds SLA's practical sweet spot — FDM builds up to 300 × 300 × 400 mm at reasonable cost.
- Speed and cost efficiency are the priority — FDM is the fastest, most cost-effective route to a physical prototype in most geometries.
- Colour matters for presentation — FDM filament is available in a wide range of colours without painting.

**Use SLA when:**
- Surface finish is a primary requirement — SLA layer lines are finer (25–100 µm) and surfaces need less post-processing for visual models.
- Fine detail resolution is critical — SLA produces sharper thin features and smaller text than FDM at standard settings.
- Clear or translucent parts are needed — SLA resins include transparent options that FDM cannot replicate.
- The part is primarily visual, a display model, or requires a painted finish without visible layer texture.

## FDM Materials

### ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)

ABS is a rigid, impact-resistant thermoplastic suited to functional enclosures, structural brackets, and mechanical components. It machines and sands cleanly for post-processing. ABS requires an enclosed build environment to manage warping — thermal contraction on large flat areas can pull corners off the build plate if bed adhesion and enclosure temperature are not controlled. It is the default choice for general-purpose functional prototypes where temperature resistance up to approximately 80–100 °C is required.

**Best for:** Enclosures, brackets, housings, mechanical assemblies, parts requiring post-machining or sanding.

### PLA (Polylactic Acid)

PLA is the most dimensionally stable FDM material under standard printing conditions. It produces clean, accurate parts with low warping tendency — making it the best choice when dimensional accuracy within the ±0.3 mm band matters more than heat or chemical resistance. Heat deflection is low (around 60 °C under load), so PLA parts are not suited to high-temperature environments. Colour range is wide.

**Best for:** Form and fit models, visual prototypes, dimensionally accurate validation parts, non-structural components.

### PETG (Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol)

PETG combines good chemical resistance with low warping and layer adhesion that outperforms ABS in the Z-axis. It handles exposure to oils, solvents, and mild acids better than PLA. Heat deflection is higher than PLA — approximately 70–80 °C under load. PETG is the go-to material for fluid-contact parts, lab equipment housings, and any functional prototype where ABS's warping tendency is a risk.

**Best for:** Fluid-contact housings, lab equipment, food-safe applications (unpigmented), chemical-resistant enclosures.

### TPU (Thermoplastic Polyurethane)

TPU is a flexible filament that produces parts with Shore hardness typically in the 85–95A range — similar to firm rubber. Layer adhesion in TPU is good in the XY plane; parts handle repeated bending and compression without delaminating. Print orientation matters more in TPU than rigid materials — orient bending loads parallel to layer lines. Warping is not a concern with TPU.

**Best for:** Gaskets, seals, flexible grippers, vibration-damping mounts, soft-touch overmold prototypes, wearable device components.

### ASA (Acrylonitrile Styrene Acrylate)

ASA is the outdoor-rated alternative to ABS. It resists UV degradation, moisture, and weathering without the colour fading that affects ABS in sunlight. Mechanical properties are similar to ABS, and it processes in a comparable way. ASA requires an enclosed build environment to control warping on large parts — the warping tendency is lower than ABS but present on wide flat surfaces.

**Best for:** Exterior automotive trim prototypes, outdoor enclosures, signage, parts exposed to UV or weathering in testing.

## FDM Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.2 mm (rigid materials); 0.8 mm (TPU) | Thinner walls may not fill reliably at standard nozzle diameters |
| Minimum feature size | 1.0 mm | Smaller features may not resolve at 0.1–0.3 mm layer heights |
| Overhangs | ≤ 45° without support structures | Angles beyond 45° require support material, which adds cost and post-processing |
| Support structures | Auto-generated; specify critical surfaces | Support removal leaves witness marks — orient part to keep supports away from functional faces |
| Infill density | 15–20% for visual parts; 50–100% for functional | Infill directly controls stiffness, weight, and material cost |
| Print orientation | Specify load direction at quoting stage | Layer adhesion in Z is lower than XY — orient critical load paths parallel to the build plane |
| Warping risk | Flag large flat base areas | ABS and ASA on wide flat areas risk corner lift — the DFM review identifies this before the build |
| File format | STEP preferred; STL accepted | STEP retains geometry precision; STL facet count affects feature resolution |

**How does FDM compare to SLA for 3D printed prototypes?**

FDM and SLA serve different prototype needs, and the right choice depends on your priorities. FDM holds ±0.3 mm tolerance and builds up to 300 × 300 × 400 mm, with layer heights from 0.1 to 0.3 mm. Choose FDM for functional parts where material performance and speed matter; choose SLA for visual models, display pieces, or applications where surface finish is the primary requirement.
