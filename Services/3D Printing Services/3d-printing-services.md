## Section 3: What Is 3D Printing for Prototyping

**Design:** 2-column. Text left, process illustration right. White background.

**Heading:** Why Engineers Use 3D Printing for Prototypes

3D printing builds parts layer by layer from digital geometry. Each layer fuses or cures before the next begins. No tooling is required. You go from STEP file to physical part in 2–6 days.

The technology choice matters. SLA uses UV resin and produces the tightest feature detail. SLS sinters nylon powder and skips support structures entirely. MJF fuses PA12 with thermal fusing agents for strong, isotropic parts. FDM extrudes thermoplastic filament — fast and low cost for structural validation.

We run all four processes in-house. That means one DFM review, one quote, one shipment — regardless of which process your geometry calls for.

## Section 4: Technologies — Which Process Is Right

**Design:** 4-card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Choose Your 3D Printing Process

---

### SLA — Stereolithography
**Best for:** High-detail visual models, clear and translucent parts, fine surface finish

**Key specs (📊 Reference):**
- Tolerance: ±0.1–0.2mm
- Layer height: 25–100µm
- Lead time: 3–4 business days
- Materials: Engineering resins, clear resin, tough resin

**When to use SLA:**
- Part has fine features under 1mm
- Optical clarity is required
- Client-facing cosmetic model
- Smooth surface finish needed before painting

→ [[stereolithography-sla-3d-printing-services]]

---

### SLS — Selective Laser Sintering
**Best for:** Functional nylon parts, complex geometry, assemblies without support cleanup

**Key specs (📊 Reference):**
- Tolerance: ±0.3mm (±0.35% for parts over 100mm)
- Layer height: 100–120µm
- Lead time: 5–6 business days
- Materials: PA12 Nylon, carbon-filled polyamide

**When to use SLS:**
- Part has internal channels, lattices, or undercuts
- No post-processing for support removal
- Mechanical testing required
- Batch of 5–50 identical nylon parts

→ [[selective-laser-sintering-sls-3d-printing-services]]

---

### MJF — HP Multi Jet Fusion
**Best for:** Strong functional parts with fine detail, small-to-medium production batches

**Key specs (📊 Reference):**
- Tolerance: ±0.2mm (±0.25% for parts over 100mm)
- Layer height: ~80µm
- Lead time: 4–5 business days
- Materials: PA12 Nylon (natural and black)

**When to use MJF:**
- Isotropic mechanical properties required
- Small features with consistent wall thickness
- Functional end-use prototypes in nylon
- Higher throughput than SLS for batch quantities

→ [[hp-multi-jet-fusion-mjf-3d-printing-services]]

---

### FDM — Fused Deposition Modeling
**Best for:** Structural validation, large parts, fast turnaround, low-cost iterations

**Key specs (📊 Reference):**
- Tolerance: ±0.3mm
- Layer height: 0.1–0.3mm
- Lead time: 2–3 business days
- Materials: ABS, PLA, PETG, TPU, ASA

**When to use FDM:**
- Part is large (over 200×200mm)
- Geometry is simple and feature detail is not critical
- Cost and speed outweigh surface finish
- Multiple design iterations in a short sprint

→ [[fdm-3d-printing-services]]

## Section 7: Materials Overview

**Design:** Material cards with callout specs. White background.
**Heading:** 3D Printing Materials

### Resins (SLA)
- **Standard resin** — General-purpose visual prototypes. Off-white finish.
- **Engineering resin** — Higher tensile strength, heat resistance to 58°C. Functional testing.
- **Clear / transparent resin** — Optical clarity after sanding and polishing. Fluid systems, display housings.
- **Tough resin** — ABS-like properties. Impact-resistant housings and enclosures.

### Nylon (SLS + MJF)
- **PA12 Nylon** — Chemical resistance, low moisture absorption, good fatigue resistance. Best for living hinges, snap fits, functional enclosures.
- **Carbon-filled polyamide (SLS)** — Higher stiffness than standard PA12. Structural parts requiring rigidity.
- **PA12 (MJF, black)** — Consistent density and isotropic properties. Ideal when part-to-part consistency matters.

### Thermoplastics (FDM)
- **ABS** — Impact resistance, machineable. Housings, brackets, prototype enclosures.
- **PETG** — Better layer adhesion than ABS. Chemical resistance, food-safe applications.
- **ASA** — UV-resistant, weatherable. Outdoor-use prototypes.
- **TPU** — Flexible, rubber-like. Seals, gaskets, grips.
- **PLA** — Low-cost, fast. Concept models, form-check parts.

## Section 12: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** 3D Printing Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Which 3D printing process should I use for my prototype?**
Process choice depends on your requirements. SLA is best for high-detail visual models and clear parts — it achieves the finest surface finish of the four. SLS and MJF are both nylon-based and suit functional, load-bearing parts. SLS handles complex geometry with no support cleanup. MJF offers slightly better isotropy and finer detail. FDM is fastest and cheapest — suited to large structural parts where surface finish is secondary. Send us your STEP file and state your key requirement (surface finish, strength, speed, cost) — we'll recommend the right process.

**Q2: What tolerances can 3D printing achieve?**
Tolerances depend on the process. SLA holds ±0.1–0.2mm for parts up to 100mm. SLS holds ±0.3mm (or ±0.35% for larger parts). MJF holds ±0.2mm (or ±0.25% for larger parts). FDM holds ±0.3mm under controlled conditions. Note: tolerance achievability depends on geometry, orientation, and feature size. We'll flag any tolerance risks in the DFM review before printing. *All specs 📊 Reference — confirm with client.*

**Q3: What materials are available for 3D printing?**
SLA: engineering resins, clear resin, tough resin, standard resin. SLS: PA12 Nylon, carbon-filled polyamide. MJF: PA12 Nylon (natural and black). FDM: ABS, PETG, ASA, TPU, PLA. If your application requires a specific mechanical property — heat resistance, chemical resistance, flexibility, food safety — specify it in your enquiry and we'll confirm the right material.

**Q4: What file formats do you accept for 3D printing?**
We accept STL, STEP (.stp/.step), OBJ, and 3MF. STEP is preferred — it preserves geometry more accurately than STL mesh files, especially for curved surfaces and assemblies. PDF drawings are useful for tolerance callouts but not required for quoting.

**Q5: Do you have a minimum order quantity for 3D printing?**
No. We accept single-part orders across all four processes. There is no setup fee or batch minimum. Prototyping requires the ability to test one part at a time — we're built for that.

**Q6: How long does 3D printing take at TOP PROTO?**
FDM: 2–3 business days. SLA: 3–4 business days. MJF: 4–5 business days. SLS: 5–6 business days. Lead times are from order confirmation, not from quote request. Complex orders with post-processing (painting, insert installation) add 1–2 days. Rush orders available — ask at quote stage. *All lead times 📊 Reference — confirm with client.*

**Q7: How does 3D printing quality get verified?**
Every order includes a dimensional inspection check against your STEP file. A dimensional report is available on request [VERIFY — confirm what's standard vs. on-request]. Material batch certificates are available for regulated industries. We flag any out-of-tolerance features before shipment, not after.

**Q8: Do you ship 3D printed parts internationally?**
Yes. We ship via DHL, FedEx, and UPS to the USA, UK, Europe, and Australia. Standard export packaging for parts under 500mm. Larger assemblies are crated individually. Import documentation is included with each shipment.
