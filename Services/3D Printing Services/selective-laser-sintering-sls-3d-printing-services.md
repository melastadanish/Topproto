## What Is Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)?

Selective laser sintering — SLS — builds parts inside a heated powder bed of nylon PA12 polymer. A CO₂ laser scans each layer, sintering — fusing — the powder particles together at precise locations. Unsintered powder surrounds the part throughout the build, which means no support structures are needed. When the build completes, the part is excavated from the powder cake, bead blasted to remove loose particles, and ready for use or post-processing.

Because the surrounding powder supports the part during sintering, SLS handles geometries that vat photopolymerisation and FDM cannot — internal channels, interlocking moving parts, lattice structures, and sharp undercuts all build without the support removal step that limits those processes. Part density from a properly sintered SLS build runs 95–98% — high enough for functional testing under real mechanical loads.

Layer thickness in SLS runs 100–120 µm. That is coarser than SLA (25 µm minimum), which means SLS surface finish is rougher — Ra 6–10 µm as-printed. For parts where appearance matters after SLS, dyeing, bead blasting, or painting bring the finish to an acceptable level. Where mechanical function matters more than cosmetics — and it usually does in functional prototyping — the as-printed surface is acceptable.

## SLS vs SLA — When to Use Each

SLS and SLA both produce polymer parts without tooling. They serve different purposes and rarely compete on the same application.

**Use SLS when:**
- The part needs functional nylon PA12 properties — tensile strength ~48 MPa, chemical resistance, fatigue life under cycling loads
- Geometry includes internal channels, undercuts, or interlocking features — no support structures means no access constraint
- Wall thickness is 0.7 mm or above and snap-fit or living hinge performance is required
- Part density of 95–98% is required for pressure or structural testing
- Surface finish Ra of 6–10 µm as-printed is acceptable, or dyeing/painting will follow

**Use SLA when:**
- Surface finish Ra value is critical — SLA achieves Ra 3.2 µm standard vs SLS Ra 6–10 µm
- Part requires optical transparency — clear resin is SLA-specific, no SLS equivalent
- Fine features under 0.5 mm need to resolve accurately — SLA at 25 µm layer height handles this, SLS at 100–120 µm does not
- The prototype is a visual or ergonomic model, not a functional load-bearing part
- You need a fast first look at geometry — SLA lead time is 3–4 days vs SLS 5–6 days

**Quick decision rule:** If the part must work mechanically — bear loads, cycle under stress, resist chemicals — choose SLS nylon PA12. If it must look right and surface quality is the priority — choose SLA.

## SLS Materials

### PA12 Nylon (Standard)
PA12 is the workhorse SLS material — a semi-crystalline polyamide with a long track record in functional prototyping and low-volume production. Parts are white/grey as-printed and accept dye, paint, and primer readily.

- Tensile strength: ~48 MPa
- Elongation at break: ~15–20%
- HDT: ~175°C
- Chemical resistance: Good against oils, greases, and many solvents
- Best for: Functional housings, brackets, snap-fit assemblies, ducting, cable routing components, jigs and fixtures
- Note: PA12 absorbs moisture from air over time — parts in humid environments may see minor dimensional change under sustained loading

### Carbon-Filled Polyamide (PA12 Carbon)
Carbon-filled polyamide adds chopped carbon fibre to the PA12 matrix, increasing stiffness without the wall-thickness penalty of increasing part geometry. The result is a stiffer, lighter part than standard PA12 at equivalent geometry.

- Tensile strength: ~60–70 MPa
- Stiffness: Significantly higher than standard PA12
- Surface finish: Darker, carbon-grey appearance as-printed
- Best for: Structural brackets, stiffened enclosures, UAV components, robotics links requiring high specific stiffness
- Note: Carbon-filled polyamide is more brittle than standard PA12 — avoid in applications requiring impact absorption or crack propagation resistance

## SLS Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 0.7 mm structural / 1.0 mm recommended | Walls under 0.7 mm may not sinter fully and will be fragile |
| Minimum feature size | 0.8 mm for raised features | Below 0.8 mm, sintered nylon powder resolution degrades |
| Internal channels | Minimum 3 mm diameter for powder escape | Unsintered powder traps inside closed channels — needs an exit route |
| Clearance for moving parts | 0.3–0.5 mm gap between moving surfaces | SLS prints assembled mechanisms; clearance prevents fused joints |
| Wall uniformity | Keep uniform wall thickness where possible | Thick-to-thin transitions cause differential sintering shrinkage |
| Orientation | Not critical — no support structures | Optimal orientation maximises part density and minimises layer-edge surface effects |
| Tolerances on fit features | Add ±0.15 mm allowance on press and slip fits | Sintering shrinkage and powder bed temperature gradient affect hole diameter and boss OD |
| File prep | Watertight STL, manifold geometry | Non-manifold surfaces cause build errors at slice generation |

## SLS 3D Printing — Frequently Asked Questions

### What tolerance can TOP PROTO hold on SLS 3D printed parts?

SLS 3D printing at TOP PROTO holds ±0.3 mm on features up to 100 mm. For features larger than 100 mm, tolerance scales to ±0.35% of the nominal dimension — a 200 mm length carries ±0.7 mm working tolerance. The primary source of dimensional deviation in SLS is thermal shrinkage as the sintered nylon cools — TOP PROTO compensates for typical PA12 shrink at the build prep stage, but fit-critical features such as press-fit bores and snap-fit boss diameters should include a ±0.15 mm allowance in your design. If you have a specific callout tighter than ±0.3 mm, flag it at quoting stage and the engineering team will assess feasibility for that feature and geometry.

### How long does SLS 3D printing take at TOP PROTO?

SLS parts ship in 5–6 business days from order confirmation. The quote itself returns within 24 hours of uploading your STL file.

### What file formats does TOP PROTO accept for SLS quotes?

STL is the preferred format for SLS — it maps directly to slicing and nesting software without conversion. ### What SLS materials does TOP PROTO offer?

Two materials cover the main SLS applications. Standard PA12 nylon is the primary material — tensile strength ~48 MPa, 15–20% elongation at break, good chemical resistance, white/grey as-printed and accepts dyeing and painting. Carbon-filled polyamide is less impact-resistant than standard PA12. Specify your material at quoting stage; comparison samples in both materials can run in the same quote.

A palm-sized PA12 nylon bracket — roughly 80 × 60 × 40 mm — typically runs USD 40–100 depending on geometry. Carbon-filled polyamide carries a material premium over standard PA12. Post-processing such as dyeing or painting adds cost proportional to surface area and finish complexity. Upload your STL file and receive an itemised quote within 24 hours.
