## What Is HP Multi Jet Fusion?

HP Multi Jet Fusion is a powder-bed 3D printing process. Each layer begins with a spread of PA12 Nylon powder across the build platform. The print head then applies a fusing agent — a liquid that absorbs infrared energy — over all regions to be solidified. A detailing agent is simultaneously applied at the feature boundaries to sharpen edges and prevent thermal bleed into adjacent powder. Infrared lamps pass across the full bed, activating the fusing agent and solidifying those regions while the surrounding powder stays loose.

This fusing agent and detailing agent combination is the defining difference between MJF and selective laser sintering. SLS traces each cross-section with a single laser, defining feature boundaries by spot size and dwell time. MJF heats the full layer simultaneously — which produces the thermal uniformity across the build platform that prevents the shrinkage gradients responsible for warping and dimensional variation in other powder-bed processes.

The practical result is isotropic strength: mechanical properties consistent in X, Y, and Z directions. For structural brackets, snap-fit housings, and functional assemblies, an MJF part performs like a moulded part — not like a layered stack where Z-axis load pulls at interlayer boundaries.

## MJF vs SLS — When to Use Each

Both MJF and SLS print in PA12 Nylon powder without support structures. The choice turns on feature resolution, build size, and part count economics.

**Use MJF when:**
- Fine boundary detail is required — the detailing agent tightens feature edges below what SLS laser spot size achieves
- Tolerance is critical — MJF holds ±0.2 mm versus SLS at ±0.3 mm, a meaningful difference on snap-fit latch and press-fit bore geometries
- Isotropic strength is a design requirement — MJF's thermal uniformity produces consistent mechanical properties across all load directions
- Wall thickness runs below 1.5 mm — MJF resolves thinner walls at equivalent tolerances
- Lead time matters — MJF ships in 4–5 business days versus SLS at 5–6 business days

**Use SLS when:**
- Your part exceeds MJF's 264 × 343 × 348 mm build envelope — SLS reaches 380 × 280 × 380 mm
- Carbon-filled polyamide properties are required — SLS supports filled nylon materials MJF does not
- You are packing a large batch of small parts at maximum density — SLS build economics can favour high-density runs
- Surface finish uniformity across the batch is less critical than raw part quantity

## Materials

### PA12 Nylon — Black

Black is the standard MJF output. Parts emerge grey from the build chamber and are dyed black during post-processing. The dye penetrates 0.5–1.0 mm into the surface, producing consistent colour across the batch. Black PA12 suits functional prototypes, structural enclosures, snap-fit components, and end-use brackets where cosmetic uniformity matters.

- Tensile strength: ~48 MPa
- Elongation at break: ~18%
- Heat deflection temperature: ~175°C
- Best for: functional prototypes, structural housings, end-use fixtures

### PA12 Nylon — White

White PA12 requires a post-processing whitening and sealing treatment after printing. Colour uniformity is less consistent than black across a batch — expect some variation at the part level. White finish suits medical device housings requiring light surfaces, consumer product visual prototypes, and pre-paint surfaces where the base colour should be neutral.

- Structural properties identical to black PA12
- Best for: medical device prototypes, consumer visual models, parts to be painted

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is HP Multi Jet Fusion and how does it work?

HP Multi Jet Fusion is a powder-bed 3D printing process that uses a fusing agent and detailing agent to selectively bind PA12 Nylon powder layer by layer. The fusing agent marks regions to be solidified; the detailing agent sharpens boundaries and prevents thermal bleed into adjacent powder. Infrared lamps then activate both agents across the full layer simultaneously — which is what produces MJF's thermal uniformity advantage over laser-traced sintering. The layer-wide heat application fuses powder evenly, resulting in isotropic strength: mechanical properties consistent in all three axes, not weakened at interlayer boundaries. Parts build inside an unfused powder bed, so no support structures are needed — even for internal channels and complex undercuts.

### What tolerance can TOP PROTO hold on MJF parts?

Standard MJF tolerance is ±0.2 mm for all dimensions below 100 mm. For larger dimensions, the tolerance scales to ±0.25% of the relevant feature. These are process-level figures — achievable as a baseline across standard geometry. Critical mating features such as press-fit bores and snap-fit latch arms should be called out on a 2D drawing and flagged at quoting stage. Inspection reports are available on request; specify this requirement during quoting so the engineering team can include dimensional verification in the build plan.

### What materials are available for MJF 3D printing at TOP PROTO?

TOP PROTO MJF printing uses PA12 Nylon in black and white. Black is the standard post-processing output — parts are dyed during finishing, achieving consistent colour penetration 0.5–1.0 mm into the surface. White PA12 requires additional treatment and shows more batch-to-batch colour variation. Both grades share the same structural properties: tensile strength approximately 48 MPa, elongation at break around 18%, and heat deflection temperature around 175°C. These properties hold in X, Y, and Z because MJF's thermal uniformity prevents the layer-bonding weakness that reduces Z-axis strength in other additive processes.

### How does MJF compare to SLS for functional nylon parts?

MJF outperforms SLS on tolerance and feature resolution. MJF holds ±0.2 mm versus SLS at ±0.3 mm — that 0.1 mm gap matters on snap-fit latches, press-fit bores, and closely mating housings. Feature resolution is 0.5 mm on MJF versus approximately 1.0 mm on SLS. Surface finish is also smoother from MJF because the detailing agent defines boundaries more precisely than a laser traces them. SLS has a larger build envelope (380 × 280 × 380 mm versus MJF's 264 × 343 × 348 mm) and supports carbon-filled nylon materials. For large parts or filled-material requirements, SLS is the right process. For most functional PA12 prototypes where tolerance and feature resolution matter, MJF is faster and more accurate.

### What is the minimum order quantity for MJF 3D printing?

No minimum. TOP PROTO accepts single-part MJF orders. Multiple parts can be nested in the same build to reduce per-part cost — the DFM review confirms nesting options for your specific geometry. One structural housing or thirty snap-fit brackets for functional testing — the quoting and quality process is the same either way.
