## What Is Vacuum Casting?

Vacuum casting — also called urethane casting or silicone mould casting — produces polyurethane parts that replicate injection-moulded quality at prototype quantities of 1 to 50 units. The process starts with a master pattern, typically a CNC-machined or SLA-printed part finished to the exact surface quality you want on the final cast. That master pattern is suspended inside a casting box, and liquid silicone is poured around it and cured under controlled temperature.

Once the silicone mould splits and the master is removed, the mould cavity holds a perfect negative impression of the master — including every texture, radius, and parting-line detail. Liquid polyurethane resin is then mixed, de-gassed, and poured into the closed mould inside a vacuum chamber. The vacuum draws out trapped air, eliminating the voids and porosity that ruin structural integrity in open-pour casting methods. After the demould time specified by the resin system — typically 30 to 90 minutes at 65–70°C — the part is removed, sprues are trimmed, and post-processing begins.

Each silicone mould yields 15–25 castings before dimensional drift or surface degradation exceeds tolerance. Part-to-part consistency across those shots is high because the mould material holds its shape under repeated thermal cycling. For quantities above 25 units, a second mould is made from the same master pattern — which means you never re-machine the master unless the design changes.

## Vacuum Casting vs Injection Molding — When to Use Each

The decision comes down to quantity, timeline, and tooling budget. Vacuum casting uses a silicone mould — which costs a fraction of an aluminium or steel injection mould and is ready in days rather than weeks. That makes vacuum casting the right call for 1 to 50 units where you need production-representative quality without a hard tool.

Injection molding uses a rigid aluminium or steel cavity. Tooling investment is higher and the lead time to first samples is longer, but part-to-part tolerance tightens to ±0.1mm and the mould survives thousands to millions of shots. At quantities above 50 to 100 units, injection molding typically becomes cost-competitive per part.

## Step-by-Step: The Vacuum Casting Process

### Step 1 — Master Pattern Production
The master pattern is CNC machined or SLA printed to your exact geometry, then hand-finished to the surface quality you want replicated on the cast parts. Surface roughness on the master transfers directly to the mould cavity — a polished master produces a gloss cast; a textured master produces a matte or grained cast. Dimensional accuracy of the master sets the ceiling for casting accuracy.

### Step 2 — Silicone Mould Making
The finished master pattern is suspended in a casting frame and liquid silicone — typically a platinum-cure silicone rated for 150°C+ service — is poured around it. The assembly goes into a vacuum chamber to remove trapped air from the silicone before it cures. After curing at 40°C for 8–16 hours, the mould is cut along the parting line, the master is removed, and both mould halves are inspected for completeness of detail transfer.

### Step 3 — Resin Preparation and Casting
The polyurethane resin is selected to match your target material properties — rigid ABS-like, flexible Shore A 40 rubber-like, transparent PC-like, or any of the standard resin systems. The two-part resin is mixed at the correct ratio, de-gassed under vacuum, and poured into the closed silicone mould while still inside the vacuum chamber. Atmospheric pressure then returns, forcing resin into every detail of the cavity.

### Step 4 — Curing and Demoulding
The filled mould cures in a temperature-controlled oven at 65–70°C. Demould time depends on the resin system — rigid systems typically demould in 30–45 minutes; flexible systems in 60–90 minutes. The part is removed carefully to avoid tearing the mould, and the sprue and vents are trimmed flush.

### Step 5 — Post-Processing and Finishing
Cast parts can be painted, primed, sanded, chrome-effect coated, or fitted with threaded metal inserts captured during the pour. Colour matching to RAL or Pantone standards is available. Each part goes through a dimensional check against your drawing before shipment.

### Step 6 — Inspection and Delivery
Dimensional inspection runs against your 2D drawing or STEP file reference dimensions. Inspection reports are available on request. Parts ship from Dongguan, China via DHL, FedEx, or UPS within 5–15 business days from master pattern approval.

## Polyurethane Resin Systems

Polyurethane resins — two-part thermoset materials that cure to specific mechanical properties — allow vacuum casting to simulate nearly any engineering thermoplastic without hard tooling.

| Resin Type | Simulates | Shore / Hardness | Key Properties | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rigid PU — ABS-like | ABS | Shore D 80+ | Impact resistant, rigid, paintable | Consumer housings, enclosures, prototypes |
| Rigid PU — PC-like | Polycarbonate | Shore D 80+ | High stiffness, heat resistant to 120°C | Structural covers, functional brackets |
| Rigid PU — Nylon-like | PA Nylon | Shore D 75+ | Tough, slightly flexible, good fatigue resistance | Clips, snap-fit parts, functional components |
| Transparent PU | PMMA / PC | Shore D 75+ | Optical clarity, UV-stable grades available | Lenses, light pipes, display covers |
| Flexible PU — Soft | Silicone / TPE | Shore A 10–50 | Rubber-like, compressible, excellent tear resistance | Seals, grips, overmoulded soft-touch parts |
| Flexible PU — Medium | TPU | Shore A 50–90 | Semi-rigid, good abrasion resistance | Gaskets, protective boots, ergonomic handles |
| Overmoulding PU | Overmould layer | Shore A 30–60 | Bonds to rigid substrate, colour-matchable | Two-material assemblies, soft-touch grips |

Shore hardness — a measure of a material's resistance to permanent indentation — determines how rigid or flexible the final cast part feels in use. Shore A 10 is gel-soft; Shore A 90 is firm rubber; Shore D 80+ is rigid engineering plastic. Specify your target Shore value in the quote form and the DFM review will confirm the closest resin match.

## Quality — RS-01

TOP PROTO applies a three-stage quality process to every vacuum casting order.

**Pre-Production**
The master pattern is dimensionally checked against your STEP file or 2D drawing before silicone mould making begins. Surface finish is verified against your stated requirement. Resin batch lot numbers are recorded for traceability. Mould parting line position, vent location, and gate placement are reviewed for each geometry to minimise witness lines on cosmetic surfaces.

The first cast from each new mould undergoes a full dimensional check before the production run continues. Resin mix ratio and pot life are controlled per the material datasheet. **Final Inspection**
Each cast part is visually inspected for voids, short shots, and surface defects. Critical dimensions are checked against your drawing. Inspection reports are available on request. Parts are packed individually to prevent contact damage during transit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is vacuum casting and how does it work for prototype parts?**
Vacuum casting uses a silicone mould — made from a CNC-machined or SLA-printed master pattern — to cast polyurethane resin parts under vacuum conditions. The vacuum removes trapped air from the resin before it sets, producing dense, void-free parts that replicate the master pattern's surface detail. At TOP PROTO's Dongguan, China facility, each silicone mould yields 15–25 production-equivalent cast parts. The process suits engineers who need 1–50 prototype units that perform and look like injection-moulded parts — without the time or cost of hard tooling.

**What tolerance can vacuum casting achieve?**
Standard vacuum casting tolerance at TOP PROTO is ±0.2–0.8mm to ISO-2768-CL, depending on part geometry and resin shrinkage. Local features — bosses, holes, snap-fit interfaces — can hold ±0.15mm when the master pattern is machined to that precision. Tolerance in vacuum casting is influenced by three factors: master pattern accuracy, resin shrinkage rate (typically 0.1–0.3% for standard polyurethane systems), and thermal stability during the cure cycle. For tighter tolerances across the whole part, CNC machining or injection molding may be a better fit — your DFM review will flag this.

**How long does vacuum casting take from file upload to delivered parts?**
Lead time is 5–15 business days from master pattern approval. The master pattern itself takes 1–3 days to machine or print and finish. Mould making adds 1–2 days. Each casting cycle — pour, cure, demould, trim — takes several hours, and multiple shots per day are possible once the mould is ready. Upload your STEP file to receive a DFM-reviewed quote within 24 hours, including a project-specific lead time estimate based on part complexity and quantity.

**What is the minimum order quantity for vacuum casting?**
There is no minimum order quantity. TOP PROTO accepts orders for a single cast part — which is useful when you need one functional prototype for fit checking before committing to a run of 15 or 20. Each silicone mould costs the same whether you pull 1 cast or 25, so per-part cost decreases through the mould life. Request a quote with the quantity you actually need, not the quantity that justifies the tooling.

**What polyurethane materials are available and what do they simulate?**
Seven polyurethane resin systems are available: rigid ABS-like, rigid PC-like, rigid Nylon-like, transparent PMMA/PC-like, soft flexible (Shore A 10–50), medium flexible (Shore A 50–90), and overmoulding PU for two-material assemblies. Shore hardness ranges from Shore A 10 (gel-soft) to Shore D 80+ (engineering-rigid). Material datasheets for each system include tensile strength, elongation at break, heat deflection temperature, and Shore hardness. Specify your target material in the quote and the DFM review confirms the closest resin match.

**What file formats do you accept for vacuum casting quotes?**
STEP files are preferred for 3D geometry. IGES, STL, SolidWorks (.sldprt), CATIA (.CATPart), and Parasolid (.x_t) are also accepted. 2D engineering drawings in PDF or DWG should accompany the 3D file where critical dimensions need explicit tolerancing. Upload directly on the quote page — you receive a DFM-reviewed response within 24 hours.

**How does the cost of vacuum casting compare to injection molding for small quantities?**
For quantities of 1 to 50 units, vacuum casting typically costs 60–80% less than producing an injection mould and running the equivalent quantity in plastic. The silicone mould costs a small fraction of an aluminium injection mould. The quantity crossover point — where injection tooling becomes more cost-effective per part — typically falls between 50 and 150 units depending on part size and material. Your 24-hour DFM quote includes a per-part cost at your stated quantity, so you can compare with real numbers rather than estimates.

**How does vacuum casting compare to 3D printing for functional prototypes?**
Both processes serve prototype quantities, but they serve different needs. SLA 3D printing produces parts faster — often in 1–3 days — but the photopolymer resins used are brittle under sustained load and degrade with UV exposure. Vacuum-cast polyurethane parts use resin systems with defined mechanical properties — tensile strength, elongation at break, Shore hardness — that closely match engineering thermoplastics. Surface finish from vacuum casting is also superior for cosmetic applications because the cast replicates the master pattern directly, rather than building up layer by layer. For functional testing, drop testing, user trials, and pre-production sign-off, vacuum casting delivers results closer to what the injection-moulded production part will behave like.
