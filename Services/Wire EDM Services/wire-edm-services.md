## How Wire EDM Works

Wire EDM — wire electrical discharge machining — erodes material using a continuous brass wire electrode, typically 0.1–0.3mm in diameter, that never physically contacts the part. Controlled electrical discharges arc between the wire and the workpiece through dielectric fluid — deionised water — which flushes debris and keeps the cut zone cool. The result: precise 2D profiles and tapered cuts in materials too hard or too delicate for conventional milling.

No cutting force reaches the part during the process. That absence of cutting force is why wire EDM handles hardened D2 tool steel at 62 HRC, thin-walled features down to 0.3mm, and titanium aerospace profiles where milling pressure would introduce distortion. The wire electrode follows a programmed path, held to the kerf width set by the wire diameter — leaving a burr-free edge and a surface roughness traceable to the number of skim passes run.

Ra finish depends on pass count. A single roughing pass leaves Ra 1.2–1.6µm. Two passes reach Ra 0.4–0.8µm. Three passes achieve Ra 0.2–0.4µm. Mirror finishing — multiple skim passes — brings the surface to Ra 0.1µm for sealing faces and optical mounts. Every pass is logged against the surface specification on your drawing.

## Wire EDM vs CNC Machining — When to Use Each

Both processes machine metal to tight tolerances. The right choice depends on material hardness, feature geometry, and required corner radius.

**Use Wire EDM when:**
- Material is hardened and cannot be milled without tool wear — D2, H13, M2, tungsten carbide
- Internal corner radius must be smaller than any milling tool can reach (below 0.1mm)
- Thin ribs or delicate features would deflect under conventional milling cutting forces
- The cut geometry requires a taper angle through the part thickness
- Your drawing calls for zero burr on cut edges without a secondary deburring step
- Post-hardening machining is required — heat treatment distorts pre-machined features

**Use CNC machining when:**
- Material is in soft or annealed state — machine first, harden after
- 3D surface geometry is required — wire EDM cuts 2D profiles and tapers only
- Part features span multiple cut axes that a single wire path cannot reach
- Material is non-conductive — polymers, ceramics, composites

When your part needs both 3D profiles and precision hardened features, the correct workflow is CNC machining first, then wire EDM after heat treatment. TOP PROTO runs both processes in-house.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is wire EDM machining and how does it work?**
Wire EDM — wire electrical discharge machining — removes material using controlled electrical sparks between a thin wire electrode and the workpiece, separated by dielectric fluid (deionised water). The wire never contacts the part. Spark energy erodes the material one discharge at a time, following a programmed 2D path to produce profiles, slots, and internal features. Because no cutting force reaches the part, the process works on hardened materials, thin walls, and fragile geometries where conventional milling would cause deflection or tool failure. At TOP PROTO, wire EDM cuts to ±0.002–0.005mm tolerance with surface finish to Ra 0.4µm.

**What materials can wire EDM machine?**
Wire EDM machines any electrically conductive material. At TOP PROTO, the standard material list covers tool steel (D2, H13, M2), hardened steel (4140, 4340 post-heat treatment), titanium Grade 5, stainless steel (304, 316L), aluminium (6061, 7075), carbide, copper, and brass. Non-conductive materials — polymers, ceramics, composites — are not compatible with the process. If your material is conductive and not listed, confirm at quote stage.

**What tolerance can wire EDM hold on my parts?**
Standard tolerance for wire EDM at TOP PROTO is ±0.01mm, consistent with ISO 2768-m. High-precision work holds ±0.002–0.005mm on cut geometry. The achievable tolerance depends on part geometry, material, and wall thickness — thin features approaching 0.3mm require more conservative tolerance targets. Specify your critical dimensions and tolerance requirements on the drawing; the DFM review at quote stage flags anything that requires discussion.

**What is the minimum corner radius wire EDM can cut?**
The minimum internal corner radius is approximately 0.06mm — set by the wire electrode diameter. A 0.1mm wire leaves a corner radius of roughly half its diameter. For designs requiring internal corners tighter than any milling tool can reach — typically below 0.5mm — wire EDM is the correct process. If your drawing calls for a sharp internal corner, the DFM review will confirm the smallest achievable radius for your specific wire diameter.

**What file formats do you accept for wire EDM quotes?**
DXF and DWG files are preferred for wire EDM — they carry the 2D cut profile directly. STEP files work for parts that also require 3D CNC features. If your drawing is in PDF format, include the DXF alongside it so the cut path is unambiguous. Upload files directly on the quote page — the DFM review covers conductivity, start hole requirements, corner radius, and taper geometry before any cutting starts.

**How much does wire EDM machining cost?**
Wire EDM cost depends on cut length, number of passes, material, and part thickness. A short profile in aluminium with one pass costs less than a long punch profile in hardened D2 with three finish passes. The 24-hour quote process at TOP PROTO gives you a fixed price tied to your specific DXF and material — no estimates, no surprises. Upload your STEP or DXF file to receive a DFM-reviewed quote with the pass count and lead time specified.

**How long does wire EDM take from quote to delivery?**
Prototype wire EDM parts ship in 1–5 business days from order confirmation. Quotes arrive within 24 hours of uploading your file. Complex profiles with multi-pass finishing or internal start holes take longer than simple perimeter cuts — the DFM review at quote stage confirms the realistic lead time for your specific geometry. Rush delivery within 1 business day is available; confirm availability at quote.

**How does wire EDM compare to CNC milling for tight-tolerance hardened steel parts?**
Wire EDM holds ±0.002–0.005mm on hardened D2 or H13 steel without any tool wear degrading tolerance mid-run. CNC milling of the same material at 60+ HRC wears tooling rapidly — tolerance drifts as the cutter dulls, and the process becomes economically impractical beyond a few parts. Wire EDM also achieves internal corner radii below 0.1mm, which no milling tool can match. The trade-off: wire EDM cuts only 2D profiles and tapers; CNC milling handles 3D geometry. Parts requiring both use CNC milling first, then wire EDM after heat treatment — a combined workflow TOP PROTO runs in-house.
