## What Is Rapid Prototyping

Rapid prototyping is the production of a physical part directly from a 3D CAD model, in the shortest time possible, for the purpose of design validation, functional testing, or investor presentation. The word "rapid" is measured in days, not weeks. The output is a real part in real material — not a rendering or a scaled model.

The process you choose determines what you get. CNC machining cuts from solid metal or plastic stock — your part has the same material properties as a production component. 3D printing builds layer by layer — geometry that cannot be milled or cast is suddenly achievable, though material properties differ by process. Sheet metal bending and laser cutting produce enclosures, brackets, and formed parts with production-equivalent strength. Vacuum casting replicates injection-moulded aesthetics in small batches of 1–50 parts.

Choosing the wrong process for a prototype wastes time and money. Choose CNC when material properties must match production. Choose SLA when surface detail and tight dimensional accuracy matter more than strength. Choose SLS or MJF when you need functional nylon parts with complex geometry and no support marks. Choose sheet metal when the final design is sheet metal. Choose vacuum casting when you need 5–20 copies of a complex form at near-injection-moulded quality without tooling cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I choose the right rapid prototyping process for my part?**
The right process depends on three things: required material properties, tolerance, and geometry. Use CNC machining when material properties must match the production part — aluminium 6061, titanium Grade 5, or PEEK, for example. Use SLA when your part has fine surface detail and ±0.1–0.2mm is acceptable. Use SLS or MJF for complex nylon geometry with functional strength and no support structure marks. Use sheet metal when the production design is sheet metal — no sense in 3D printing something that will be bent from 1.5mm aluminium. Upload your STEP file and the DFM review at quote stage will confirm the recommended process and flag any geometry risks.

**What are the typical lead times for rapid prototyping?**
CNC machined prototypes ship in 1–5 business days from order confirmation. FDM 3D printing takes 2–3 days. SLA takes 3–4 days. MJF takes 4–5 days. SLS takes 5–6 days. **What materials are available for rapid prototypes?**

SLA 3D printing uses engineering resins including clear and tough grades. SLS and MJF use PA12 nylon. FDM uses ABS, PLA, PETG, TPU, and ASA. Vacuum casting uses rigid, flexible, and transparent polyurethane resins. Sheet metal works in aluminium, stainless steel, mild steel, brass, and copper.
