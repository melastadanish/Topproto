## Precision Laser Cutting for Sheet Metal Blanks

Laser cutting services at TOP PROTO produce flat sheet metal blanks to ±0.1mm cut profile tolerance from their Dongguan, China facility. A focused high-power laser traces the programmed cut path, parting the sheet along the profile outline and through hole patterns in a single setup. Kerf width — the material removed by the beam — runs 0.1–0.3mm and is offset in the cut program so finished dimensions hit the target. ISO 9001:2015 certified. DFM review included with every quote. Single-part prototype blanks accepted with 1–2 business day lead time.

Laser cutting is the blanking operation that feeds every downstream sheet metal process. A bracket, enclosure, or panel starts as a flat blank cut from sheet stock — then moves to CNC press brake bending to become a three-dimensional part. Getting the blank right matters: a hole positioned 0.15mm off on the blank becomes a misaligned fastener hole on the finished assembly.

The cut program accounts for kerf width, taper compensation on thicker materials, and nesting efficiency when multiple different profiles cut from the same sheet. Your DXF geometry drives the program directly — no manual reinterpretation, no redrawing. Complex profiles, internal pockets, and irregular outlines cut with the same accuracy as simple rectangular blanks.

## Laser Cutting vs. Alternative Cutting Methods

| Factor | Laser Cutting | Waterjet Cutting | Plasma Cutting |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cut profile tolerance | ±0.1mm | ±0.1–0.2mm | ±0.5–1.0mm |
| Kerf width | 0.1–0.3mm | 0.8–1.5mm | 1.5–3.0mm |
| Heat-affected zone | Narrow (0.1–0.5mm) | None | Wide (1–3mm) |
| Min hole diameter | ~1× material thickness | ~1× material thickness | >3mm |
| Best material range | Thin to mid-gauge metals | Thick metals, stone, composites | Thick steel, structural sections |
| Edge quality | Clean, minimal burr | Slightly rough | Rough, requires secondary finishing |
| Setup per profile | Low — DXF direct to program | Low — similar | Low — similar |
| Speed on thin materials | Fast | Slower | Faster on thick steel |

Laser cutting is the right choice for sheet metal blanks under 6mm where cut profile accuracy, edge quality, and hole positioning matter. Waterjet suits thicker structural sections or materials sensitive to heat. Plasma is cost-effective on structural steel above 10mm but cannot hold the profile accuracy that prototype sheet metal work requires.

## Materials for Laser Cutting

| Material | Grades | Thickness Range | Laser Cutting Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aluminium | 5052-H32, 6061-T6 | 0.5mm and above | Clean cut edge, minimal oxide layer, no secondary deburring on most profiles |
| Stainless Steel | 304, 316L | 0.5mm and above | Nitrogen assist gas gives bright oxide-free edge on thin gauges |
| Mild Steel | SPCC, Q235 | 0.5mm and above | Oxygen assist for thicker gauges; slight oxide on cut edge, removes with light sanding |
| Brass | C27400, C36000 | 0.5–5mm | Reflective surface requires adjusted beam parameters; edge quality clean on thin gauges |
| Copper | C101, C110 | 0.5–5mm | High reflectivity; beam parameters adjusted per thickness; clean profile on gauges up to 3mm |

Aluminium 5052-H32 and stainless steel 304 produce the cleanest laser cut edges with minimal secondary finishing required. Brass and copper require adjusted laser parameters because of their high surface reflectivity — edge quality is clean but the cut speed is lower than ferrous materials. Specify your material grade and sheet thickness when uploading your DXF so the cut program is optimised for that combination.

## Design Guidelines for Laser Cutting

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min hole diameter | 1.2× sheet thickness | Smaller diameters tear; secondary drilling required |
| Hole to edge distance | 2× sheet thickness minimum | Closer holes distort the cut edge |
| Min slot width | Equal to sheet thickness | Narrower slots risk beam interference |
| Corner radii on internal cutouts | 0.5mm minimum | Sharp internal corners cause micro-cracking on some alloys |
| Text and engraving | 1.5mm stroke width minimum | Thinner lines lose definition on most gauges |
| Nesting direction | State "nesting allowed" on DXF | Allows multiple profiles from one sheet, reduces material cost |
| Grain direction (brushed finishes) | Specify on drawing if critical | Laser cut orientation affects grain alignment on brushed panels |
| File geometry | Closed profiles only — no open contours | Open contours cannot be cut; DFM review flags these before cutting |
| Tolerance callouts | Add to PDF drawing, not DXF | DXF carries geometry; PDF carries tolerance notes and special requirements |

Your DFM review checks all of these before the first cut. Open contours, undersized holes, and tight slot widths are flagged with the quote so you can correct the drawing before fabrication starts.

## Laser Cutting — Frequently Asked Questions

**What tolerance can TOP PROTO hold on laser cut profiles?**

Cut profile tolerance on laser cutting at TOP PROTO is ±0.1mm on flat blank dimensions and hole positions. This tighter-than-standard tolerance is achievable because the laser cut program directly controls the beam path — there is no tool deflection, no fixture variation, no repositioning error. General sheet metal tolerance (ISO 2768-c) is ±0.2mm; laser cutting beats that standard on the cut profile itself. If your design has features requiring tolerances tighter than ±0.1mm — press-fit holes or close-clearance slots — discuss those features during the DFM review.

**What is laser kerf and how does it affect my part dimensions?**

Laser kerf is the width of material removed by the laser beam as it cuts through the sheet — typically 0.1–0.3mm depending on material type and sheet thickness. Without kerf compensation, your blank would be smaller than the DXF geometry by half the kerf width on each side of every cut. TOP PROTO applies kerf offset in the cut program before the first sheet goes on the table, so your finished blank dimensions match the DXF directly. Kerf width also affects the minimum spacing between adjacent nested profiles — parts must be spaced at least 2× kerf width apart on the sheet to prevent heat interference between adjacent cuts.

**What materials can you laser cut at TOP PROTO?**

TOP PROTO laser cuts aluminium (5052-H32, 6061-T6), stainless steel (304, 316L), mild steel (SPCC, Q235), brass (C27400, C36000), and copper (C101, C110). Minimum sheet thickness is 0.5mm across all materials. Maximum thickness for brass and copper is 5mm. Aluminium and stainless steel produce the cleanest cut edges with minimal secondary finishing. Brass and copper require adjusted beam parameters because of high surface reflectivity — edge quality remains clean but cut speed is lower. Specify your grade and thickness on the upload.

**How long does laser cutting take at TOP PROTO?**

Flat laser cut blanks are ready in 1–2 business days from confirmed DXF. If your blanks proceed to CNC press brake bending as part of the same order, the complete formed sheet metal part ships in 3 business days. Low-volume batches of cut blanks typically ship within 1 week. Quote turnaround is 24 hours including a DFM review of your file.

**What file format should I send for laser cutting?**

DXF is the preferred file format for laser cutting — it gives the cut program the exact geometry without interpretation or translation error. DWG is also accepted. STEP files are accepted for folded 3D models; the engineering team unfolds the geometry before generating the cut program. PDF drawings alongside the DXF are useful for capturing tolerance callouts and special requirements that DXF geometry alone does not convey. Include material grade, sheet thickness, and quantity in the upload.

**What is nesting and does it reduce my cost?**

Nesting arranges multiple different blank profiles on a single sheet to minimise material waste between parts. When your order includes several different bracket or panel shapes cut from the same material and thickness, the cut program nests them together — reducing the number of sheet setups and the volume of material scrap. This reduces cost on prototype batches where you need five or six different shapes without ordering a full production run of any single profile. State "nesting approved" on your DXF or in the order notes and the team will apply it where beneficial.

**How much does laser cutting cost for prototype sheet metal blanks?**

Laser cutting cost depends on three factors: material type and thickness, total cut path length, and sheet utilisation from nesting. A simple rectangular aluminium 5052-H32 blank with a few holes costs less than a complex stainless 304 profile with curved outlines and a dense hole pattern — because the laser spends more time on the longer cut path. Setup is included in the quote. Single-part prototype blanks are accepted with no batch minimum. Upload your DXF and receive a firm quote with a DFM review within 24 hours — cost is specific to your geometry, not a per-hour estimate.

**How does laser cutting compare to waterjet or plasma cutting for sheet metal prototypes?**

Laser cutting holds ±0.1mm cut profile tolerance, which waterjet matches but plasma cannot — plasma typically holds ±0.5–1.0mm. For thin-to-mid-gauge sheet metal under 6mm, laser cutting is faster than waterjet and produces a cleaner edge with no secondary abrasive cleanup. The heat-affected zone on laser cutting is narrow — 0.1–0.5mm — and does not affect the mechanical properties of the cut blank for standard sheet metal applications. Waterjet is the better choice for thick structural sections or heat-sensitive materials where even a narrow heat-affected zone is not acceptable. For prototype sheet metal brackets, enclosures, and panels in the 0.5–5mm range, laser cutting is the standard process.
