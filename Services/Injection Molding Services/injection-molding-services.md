## How Injection Molding Works

Injection molding forces molten thermoplastic under pressure into a precision-machined metal cavity. The clamp holds the two mould halves closed against injection pressure — measured in tonnes — while the material fills the cavity through the gate location, which is the entry point for molten plastic into the mould. The plastic cools and solidifies in contact with the tool steel or aluminium walls, then the mould opens and an ejector system pushes the finished part clear. Cycle time — the time from mould close to next mould close — ranges from a few seconds to several minutes depending on wall thickness, material, and cooling channel design.

The tool itself is the major investment in injection molding. An aluminium rapid tooling tool costs significantly less than a hardened P20 or H13 steel production tool, but it survives fewer shots. Aluminium rapid tooling is the right choice when you need T1 samples in 2 weeks to validate design, assembly, and function before committing to a full production tool. Steel production tooling is the right choice when part volumes, surface finish requirements, or material abrasiveness demand a tool rated for tens of thousands of shots.

Draft angle — the small taper applied to vertical walls in a mould — is critical to injection molding design. Without sufficient draft, parts stick in the tool during ejection, causing surface scuffing or deformation. Typical draft angles run from 0.5° to 3° per side depending on wall depth and surface texture. Sink marks — shallow depressions in the part surface caused by material shrinkage over thick sections — are managed by controlling wall thickness uniformity and gate location relative to heavy sections.

## Rapid Tooling vs Production Tooling — Which Do You Need?

Both tooling types run the same injection molding process. The difference is the tool material, tool life, and what that means for your project timeline and budget.

Rapid tooling uses aluminium — specifically 7075-series aluminium for its strength-to-machinability ratio. Aluminium machines quickly, which brings your T1 first-article sample in approximately 2 weeks. The trade-off is tool life: aluminium tools are suited to prototype verification and bridge production runs, not long-run manufacturing.

Production tooling uses hardened steel — P20, H13, or S136 depending on the material being moulded and the surface finish required. Steel tools take longer to machine and harden, but they survive hundreds of thousands of shots while holding SPI A-grade surface finishes that aluminium cannot maintain over repeated cycles.

## Step-by-Step: The Injection Molding Process

### Step 1 — DFM Review
Before tooling design begins, TOP PROTO reviews your STEP file for injection molding manufacturability. Draft angles, wall thickness uniformity, undercuts, parting line placement, gate location options, and sink mark risk — all are assessed and reported. Issues flagged at this stage cost nothing to fix. The same issues found after the tool is cut require expensive rework. DFM review is included with every quote.

### Step 2 — Tool Design
The mould is designed in CAD — cavity and core geometry, runner system, gate location, ejector pin placement, cooling channel routing, and venting positions. Gate location determines where material enters the part and directly affects weld line position, sink mark risk, and fill balance in multi-cavity tools. Cooling channel layout determines cycle time and part warpage.

### Step 3 — Tool Machining
The aluminium or steel mould block is CNC machined — and, for precision features, wire EDM cut — to the designed cavity geometry. Mold cavity tolerance at TOP PROTO is held to ±0.02mm. After machining, the tool is benched, polished to the specified SPI finish grade, and assembled with ejectors, cooling fittings, and the runner system.

### Step 4 — T1 First Article Sample
The first shots off the new tool are the T1 samples — your first look at the actual injection-moulded part. Dimensional inspection runs against your drawing. Surface finish, gate vestige location, weld line position, and ejector pin witness marks are all documented. Any tool adjustments required — steel removal, polish, venting changes — are made at this stage before the tool is approved for production.

### Step 5 — Production Run
Parts are moulded to the T1-approved standard. Cycle time is managed to balance part quality and output. Material drying, barrel temperature, injection speed, packing pressure, and cooling time are set and recorded per the approved process sheet. Each cavity in a multi-cavity tool is checked for balance — consistent fill and part weight across cavities — before a production run is released.

### Step 6 — Inspection and Delivery
Final inspection checks critical dimensions against your drawing. Visual inspection covers surface finish, sink marks, short shots, flash, and gate vestige condition. Inspection reports are available on request. Parts ship from Dongguan, China via DHL, FedEx, or UPS within 5–15 business days from tooling approval.

| Finish Grade | Standard | Description |
|---|---|---|
| SPI A1 | Mirror polish | Optical surfaces, premium cosmetic parts |
| SPI A2 | High gloss | Consumer products, display bezels |
| SPI B1 | Fine semi-gloss | General cosmetic, moderate sheen |

SPI (Society of the Plastics Industry) finish grades are the standard system for specifying injection mould surface quality. A1 requires diamond-polished tool steel; D1 is achieved by sandblasting the tool cavity. Specify your required SPI grade in the drawing or quote notes — the DFM review will confirm which grades your tool material can achieve.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is injection molding and how does the process work?**
Injection molding forces molten thermoplastic into a precision-machined metal mould cavity under high pressure. The plastic fills the cavity through the gate location — the entry point — then cools against the tool walls until solid. The mould opens, ejector pins push the part clear, and the mould closes for the next cycle. Cycle time ranges from a few seconds for small thin-walled parts to several minutes for large thick-walled components. TOP PROTO runs injection molding at its Dongguan, China facility, with aluminium rapid tooling and hardened steel production tooling both available from the same DFM-reviewed tool design.

**How much does injection molding tooling cost?**
Tooling cost depends on part size, geometry complexity, number of cavities, and whether aluminium or steel tooling is required. Aluminium rapid tooling for a simple single-cavity part costs significantly less than a hardened steel multi-cavity production tool for the same geometry. The DFM review included with your quote will include a tooling recommendation — aluminium or steel — based on your geometry, material, and target volume. Upload your STEP file and receive a tooling cost estimate within 24 hours.

**What is the difference between rapid tooling and production tooling?**
Rapid tooling uses aluminium 7075 for the mould cavity and core. It machines quickly — T1 samples are available in approximately 2 weeks — but aluminium has a lower tool life than hardened steel. Production tooling uses P20, H13, or S136 hardened steel, which takes 3–6 weeks to produce but survives hundreds of thousands of shots and holds SPI A-grade surface finishes that aluminium cannot maintain long-term. Both types run the same injection molding process and hold part-to-part tolerance to ±0.1mm. The right choice depends on your volume, cosmetic requirements, and how the prototype design will evolve before production is committed.

Material selection drives tooling material choice — PTFE and PVC require steel tooling due to chemical aggressiveness or abrasiveness. Filled grades, such as glass-filled nylon or carbon-filled PEEK, are available on request and also require steel tooling. Specify your material grade in the quote and the DFM review will confirm tooling compatibility and any processing considerations.

**How long does injection molding take from file to delivered parts?**
T1 first-article samples arrive within 2 weeks of tool design approval for aluminium rapid tooling. Production parts ship within 5–15 business days after your T1 sample is approved and the production run is released. For steel production tooling, the T1 sample lead time extends to 3–6 weeks. **How does injection molding compare to vacuum casting for prototype quantities?**
Vacuum casting delivers prototype parts in 5–15 business days without any hard tooling cost — each silicone mould yields 15–25 polyurethane cast parts to ±0.2–0.8mm. Injection molding requires a tooling investment and a 2-week lead time to T1 samples, but parts are moulded in actual engineering thermoplastics to ±0.1mm part-to-part tolerance with consistent mechanical properties. For 1 to 50 units, vacuum casting is typically faster and less expensive. For 50 to 500+ units where production-representative materials, tighter tolerances, or long-term mechanical testing are required, injection molding becomes the right answer. The DFM review on your quote will include a process recommendation if the right choice is not clear from your quantity and application alone.
