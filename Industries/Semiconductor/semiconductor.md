## Section 3: Semiconductor Manufacturing at TOP PROTO

**Heading:** Precision Manufacturing for Semiconductor Process Components

TOP PROTO machines semiconductor process equipment components at its Dongguan, China facility — vacuum chamber bodies, wafer handling fixtures, etch chamber liners, and alignment fixtures delivered to tolerances as tight as ±0.005mm. ISO 9001:2015 certified. DFM review is included with every quote.

Semiconductor process equipment operates under conditions that expose machined components to corrosive chemistries, high vacuum, and plasma environments. Material selection and surface finish are not specifications to approximate — they are the difference between a component that survives 10,000 process cycles and one that fails at 100. TOP PROTO machines stainless steel 316L, titanium Grade 2, PEEK, and PTFE for these environments, selecting the process and surface treatment to match the specific chemical and thermal exposure.

Vacuum integrity is the primary structural requirement for chamber components. Porosity — microscopic voids in the metal — allows outgassing, the slow release of trapped gases under vacuum that contaminates the process environment. TOP PROTO selects vacuum-grade bar stock and billet, and applies electropolishing on request. Electropolishing — an electrochemical surface removal process — removes the damaged surface layer left by machining and achieves Ra <0.4µm, which reduces the surface area available for gas adsorption and particle entrapment.

Wafer handling fixtures and alignment components demand dimensional stability across repeated thermal cycles. 5-axis CNC — which machines complex features in one clamping without repositioning — eliminates the setup transfer errors that accumulate in multi-setup machining and degrade positional accuracy on critical alignment features. Wire EDM holds slot and aperture tolerances to ±0.002–0.005mm on hardened fixture materials where conventional cutting tools cannot maintain that window.

## Section 8: Quality & Process (RS-01)

**Heading:** How TOP PROTO Controls Quality for Semiconductor Process Components

Vacuum integrity starts at material selection. TOP PROTO sources aluminium and stainless billet from certified suppliers with mill certifications available on request. Porosity testing is applied to castings where specified — prototype components are machined from wrought billet by default to eliminate casting porosity risk.

Electropolishing is performed after final machining. The process removes 10–30µm of surface material, eliminates the work-hardened layer from cutting, and reaches Ra <0.4µm — confirmed by surface roughness measurement before delivery. Passivation on stainless components follows ASTM A967 procedure on request.

CMM inspection is available for critical features — positional accuracy on wafer fixture contact points, flatness of sealing faces, and concentricity of vacuum port bores. Inspection reports are available on request — specify at quoting stage. Nothing ships without the specified inspection criteria confirmed.

DFM review is included with every quote. For semiconductor components, DFM review covers internal feature access for electropolishing, corner radii against tool diameter for internal pockets, and thread specification against vacuum hardware standards. Catching incompatibilities at quoting stage prevents first-article rework.

## Section 11: Frequently Asked Questions

**Heading:** Semiconductor Manufacturing — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What certifications does TOP PROTO hold for semiconductor parts?**
TOP PROTO is ISO 9001:2015 certified — the internationally recognised quality management standard requiring documented process control, material traceability, and corrective action procedures. Semiconductor equipment suppliers are typically qualified through OEM-specific audit processes; ISO 9001:2015 is the baseline certification most equipment manufacturers require. Material certifications and inspection reports are available on request — specify at quoting stage.

**Q2: What tolerances can you achieve on semiconductor process chamber components?**
CNC milling holds chamber body and insert dimensions to ±0.01mm on standard features and ±0.005mm on critical fits with 5-axis machining. Wire EDM holds precision slots and apertures in hardened materials to ±0.002–0.005mm — zero cutting force eliminates the tool deflection and spring-back that degrade tolerance in conventional milling. Sealing face flatness and concentricity of vacuum port bores are inspected by CMM on request. Confirm feature-specific tolerance requirements on your 2D drawing at quoting stage.

**Q3: What materials do you machine for semiconductor process equipment?**
Stainless steel 316L is the standard for ultra-clean process components — electropolishable to Ra <0.4µm and resistant to halogen-adjacent chemistries. Aluminium 6061 covers structural chamber components where chemical exposure is managed. Titanium Grade 2 handles aggressive etch chemistries including HF and chlorine plasma environments where stainless degrades. PEEK — polyether ether ketone, an engineering polymer stable to 250°C — is machined for insulating spacers and wafer contact components. PTFE covers the broadest chemical resistance range.

**Q4: Can you achieve the surface cleanliness required for process chamber components?**
Electropolishing on stainless 316L reaches Ra <0.4µm — the surface finish level that reduces particle entrapment and outgassing in high-vacuum environments. The process removes 10–30µm of the machined surface layer, eliminating embedded contaminants and the work-hardened zone from cutting. Passivation per ASTM A967 is available on request for stainless components. Surface roughness is measured and reported before delivery. Specify your SEMI cleanliness class or Ra requirement at quoting stage.

**Q5: What semiconductor equipment parts do you manufacture most frequently?**
Process chamber inserts and liners in stainless 316L and titanium Grade 2 are the most common components. Wafer handling fixtures and robotic end-effectors in aluminium 6061 or PEEK follow. Vacuum flanges — machined to KF, CF, or ISO-K standards — are produced in both stainless and aluminium. Alignment fixtures in hardened stainless with Wire EDM slots are common for precision wafer positioning applications. Etch chamber liners and electrode components in titanium complete the regular part mix.

**Q6: How long does it take to machine semiconductor process components?**
CNC machined chamber components ship in 5 days on standard lead time, or 1–3 days on rush. Wire EDM components with precision slots ship in 1–5 days. Parts requiring electropolishing add 1–2 days to the schedule — specify at quoting stage so it is built into the lead time. 3D printed fixture geometry prototypes in SLA are ready in 2–6 days. Upload your STEP file for a confirmed lead time alongside the DFM-reviewed quote within 24 hours.

**Q7: How much does machining semiconductor process chamber components cost?**
Cost is driven by material, feature complexity, internal access for electropolishing, and inspection requirements. Stainless 316L and titanium Grade 2 carry higher raw material costs than aluminium — and both machine slower, adding process time. Wire EDM for precision slots adds cost over CNC but produces tolerances and surface finishes that CNC cannot match on certain geometries. Single-part and small-batch prototype orders are accepted. Upload your STEP file and drawing for a specific price within 24 hours.

**Q8: How does TOP PROTO compare to a local machine shop for vacuum chamber components?**
A general machine shop can produce the external dimensions of a vacuum chamber component but typically lacks electropolishing capability, in-house Wire EDM, and familiarity with outgassing material requirements. TOP PROTO applies electropolishing to Ra <0.4µm in-house, runs Wire EDM to ±0.002–0.005mm for precision apertures, and sources vacuum-grade billet with mill certificates available on request. DFM review at quoting stage covers internal feature access for electropolishing and thread specifications against vacuum hardware standards — details a general shop would surface only after first-article failure.
