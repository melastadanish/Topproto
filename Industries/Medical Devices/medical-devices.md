## Section 3: Medical Device Manufacturing at TOP PROTO

**Heading:** Precision Manufacturing for Medical Devices

TOP PROTO produces medical device prototype and low-volume parts to tolerances of ±0.005mm at its Dongguan, China facility — CNC machining, Wire EDM, SLA, and injection molding in stainless 316L, titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5, PEEK, Delrin (POM), and acrylic (PMMA). ISO 9001:2015 certification governs every order: documented process control, material traceability from stock to finished part, and dimensional verification available on request. Every quote includes a DFM review — geometry, material selection, surface finish requirements, and tolerance feasibility reviewed before machining starts.

Medical device parts carry requirements that commodity machining cannot address: confirmed biocompatible materials, dimensional evidence that critical features were held, and a traceable record linking the finished part to its material source. Stainless 316L and titanium Grade 2/5 are biocompatible — confirmed by material grade and alloy composition, not by surface finishing alone. PEEK (polyether ether ketone — a high-performance engineering thermoplastic with biocompatibility for implant-adjacent applications, chemical resistance, and mechanical properties stable up to 250°C) is machined to tight tolerances for implant trials, surgical guides, and instrument components where metal is too dense or radiopaque.

Wire EDM (electrical discharge machining — material removed by a controlled electrical discharge between a wire electrode and the workpiece, with no cutting force applied to the part) reaches ±0.002–0.005mm on complex profiles, slots, and apertures in stainless and titanium. No cutting force means no deflection in thin-wall instrument blades, cannula components, or fine surgical geometry. Surface finish after EDM reaches Ra 0.4µm — sufficient for sealing surfaces, instrument joints, and precision mechanical interfaces without secondary lapping.

For device housings, diagnostic enclosures, and form-fit evaluation of instrument grip geometry, SLA (stereolithography — photopolymer resin cured layer by layer under UV light) produces prototypes to ±0.1–0.2mm with fine surface detail. SLA accelerates design validation before committing to a machined stainless or injection-molded housing — critical when a development programme carries a regulatory timeline. Prototype runs start at a single part. No minimum order quantity applies.

## Section 7: Certifications Relevant to Medical Devices

**Heading:** Certifications and Quality Standards for Medical Devices

**ISO 13485** is the quality management standard for medical device manufacturers and their supply chains. It extends ISO 9001 with requirements for design controls, a Device History File (DHF — documentation that demonstrates the device was designed and manufactured in accordance with the Design History File), risk management per ISO 14971, and post-market surveillance. Regulatory submissions under FDA 510(k), CE marking, and similar pathways require the device manufacturer to maintain an ISO 13485-compliant quality management system.

For machining vendors in a medical device supply chain, ISO 13485 governs requirements for process validation, traceability of materials and operations, control of non-conforming product, and change control. Many device manufacturers require their machining suppliers to hold ISO 13485 registration, particularly for parts that contact the patient or are used in a sterile field.

TOP PROTO holds **ISO 9001:2015** certification, which covers documented quality management, process control, traceability, and corrective action. ISO 13485 is not currently held as a registered certification. For medical device projects that require ISO 13485-compliant supplier documentation — including design control traceability, process validation records, or specific DHF inputs — discuss these requirements at the quoting stage. The engineering team will confirm what is directly supported.

Biocompatibility is driven by material selection, not surface finishing alone. Stainless 316L, titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5, and PEEK are established biocompatible materials. Material certifications confirming alloy grade and compliance with relevant specifications (e.g. ASTM F138 for implant-grade stainless, ASTM F136 for Ti-6Al-4V ELI) are available on request — specify this at the quoting stage.

## Section 8: Quality & Process

**Heading:** Quality Steps That Matter for Medical Device Parts

Every order at TOP PROTO follows a documented process chain: material traceability from incoming stock through to the finished part, in-process inspection at critical operations, and dimensional check before shipment. For medical device parts, material traceability is particularly important — the record linking a finished part to its incoming material lot is the foundation of any device history. Material certifications are available on request; specify this requirement when submitting your quote.

DFM (design for manufacturability) review is included with every quote. For surgical instruments and implant trial components, the review checks critical feature accessibility, fixturing strategy, and tolerance stack — including whether a specified tolerance requires Wire EDM or 5-axis CNC rather than standard 3-axis machining. Surface finish requirements are also reviewed: passivation of stainless 316L, for example, removes free iron from the surface to improve corrosion resistance — but this requires post-machining processing time that must be factored into the lead time.

CMM (coordinate measuring machine — measures part geometry to micron-level accuracy against your drawing dimensions) inspection is available on request. Dimensional inspection reports confirming measured values against drawing tolerances can be provided — specify at the quoting stage. For instrument components with GD&T (geometric dimensioning and tolerancing) call-outs, confirm these on your drawing at submission.

**Q2: What tolerances can you achieve for medical device parts?**

CNC machining holds ±0.01mm standard and ±0.005mm on critical features — seating surfaces, bore positions, and mating interfaces.
